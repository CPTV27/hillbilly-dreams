LOMONTVILLE

The woodworker in the valley
BY ANDREW HICKEY
Ulster County Press
He has all of his fingers. You can
see the close calls he’s had with
any number of blades over the
years, though, as he runs his hand
over a long slab of solid pine, three
inches thick.
“I like this,” Stefan Geisel said as
he stood next to his Hidden Valley
workshop in Lomontville. “This
what I’ve wanted to do my entire
life.”
Geisel has been working with
wood since he was a boy in Romania. As many European families
were during World War II, Geisel’s
family was torn apart - his mother
was taken to Siberia under Stalin’s
orders; his father escaped to America; and Geisel and his siblings
were left with their grandmother.
Eventually, Geisel said, he made
his way to the United States to reunite with his father, also a woodworker, in New Jersey. It was a reunion that was short-lived.
“My father was here for nine
years as a cabinet maker,” he said.
“He died (at age 48) three months
after I came. But he knows I do
this. I know he knows.”
Geisel came to the United States
at age 27 and, after honing his
woodworking skills while learning
from his father’s colleagues in New
Jersey, he moved to Lomontville in
1968, and has been there ever since.
Geisel’s shop is a tall, renovated
barn he rehabilitated himself.

PHOTO BY MATT PETRICONE

STEFAN GEISEL working on a slab of pine in his workshop.
Classical music plays from what
can only be described as a 1980sera boom box, complete with tape
deck that is tucked away on a shelf
in the corner. Tools cover most of
each wall, but the shop is clean and
organized.
“It got so bad,” Geisel explained
about the first two years he went
out on his own in Ulster County, “I
couldn’t make the mortgage payments. I almost lost my home.”
But, as many artisans in the area
sometimes find, word of mouth
spread about Geisel and his talent
of working with wood.
He’s built everything from custom cabinets (which constitutes

most of his work) to bar tops for
everyone from your average new
homeowner looking to renovate to
actress Demi Moore (he built her a
hutch for her Vermont summer
home).
“It says it on the side of my
truck,” he said, pointing towards a
red pick-up. “You name it, I’ll make
it.”
On an average job, Geisel will
learn from the customer what
they want and he takes the dimensions. Then he draws it to scale on
paper, cutting the wood based on
his drawings. He fits the wood together and sets the glue to dry
with clamps. That process can

take three days, or it can take
three months, depending on the
job.
“Putting it together, I enjoy the
most,” Geisel said through a noticeable German accent, his native
country. “That’s when you start
seeing what you’re making. That’s
what gives me the high.”
While most of the work he does
is with oak, Geisel said his favorite
wood is cherry.
“The color is just...so many little
knots and you get the red and
brown,” he said with a smile. “It’s
such a smooth wood.”
Geisel gets most of his material
from either Albany or New York
City. Between the two, he said, you
can find just about anything you
need. Some of the wood he gets
from trees on his own property. An
entertainment center in his living
room grew from the ground outside his home.
At 66 years-old, Geisel stands
next to a shed full of kiln-dried
planks as old as he is. He plans to
make doors for his house with
them.
Between keeping his customers
happy, tackling projects around his
own house and...oh yeah, going out
dancing every Friday and Saturday night to keep in shape, Geisel
counts himself among the lucky,
having found his way to his patch
of land in Marbletown and doing
something he loves.

Pierson moved to the Woodstock area about 10
years ago and, in addition to harvesting the existing, rich music scene and rounding up talent for
Mechanical Bull, he calls the area an inspiration on
it’s own.
After growing up in the Berkshires, Pierson
went on to live virtually all over the country. But,
he said, “this area has always felt like home. Aside
from the being really beautiful, I like the country,
rural feel and slowed down pace of life. It’s a really
cool community of creative and nurturing people
up here.”
Pierson first got the itch for country music while
living in Boston in his early 20s after hearing what
he describes as rockabilly.
“I was like, shit, I’m gonna move to Texas and
check this out,” Pierson said. “I got in my car and
drove to Austin.”
There, he immersed himself in country music
and the real-deal, Texas music scene. Eventually, he
wandered his way back Northeast and landed in Ulster County.
“Mechanical Bull wouldn’t have evolved outside
of Woodstock,” he said.
With the release of “A Million Yesterdays,” Pierson said, Mechanical Bull is aiming to spread their
version of Woodstock country across the northeast.
“I think we’re going to give it a shot. It’s a lot of
fun for us,” he said, adding that the band is planning a tour of the northeast. “You never know with
these things.”
In fact, Mechanical Bull has had success with
their first album, Pierson said. The folks from Hollywood came calling and scooped up the rights to
“All Hat No Cattle” to use in a film.
For now, you can catch Mechanical Bull at the
Bearsville Theater in Woodstock on Nov. 1 for their
album release party. Doors open at 8 p.m. and the
show starts at 9 p.m. with special guest Erin Sax
Seymour. Tickets are $10. The Bearsville Theater is
at 291 Tinker St. in Woodstock and can be reached
at 679-4406 or at www.bearsvilletheater.com.
