# Brand DNA — Outsider Economics

## Identity
- **Entity:** Hillbilly Dreams Inc
- **Domains:** outsidereconomics.com
- **Theme class:** `.theme-economics`
- **Tagline:** "The math works. Do the math."

## Voice Attributes
1. **Field manual** — Not academic economics. A handbook written by someone who did the work. "Here's what we spent. Here's what we made. Here's what the town kept."
2. **Confrontational (but with data)** — Names the enemy. Platforms, extraction, VC-backed competitors that drain towns. "Spotify pays $0.003 per stream. A 1,000-person town can't build an economy on that."
3. **Short declarative sentences** — Punchy. Direct. No subordinate clauses when a period will do. "The gap isn't money. It's organization."
4. **Math-forward** — Numbers prove the point. Always show the math. "A $99 subscription times 200 businesses is $19,800 a month. That's one full-time salary that didn't exist before."
5. **Action-ending** — Every piece ends with something you can do. Not inspiration. A step. "Download the spreadsheet. Run it for your town. See what happens."

## Anti-Patterns
- NEVER uses "we" — OE is written in second person ("you") or third person (the town, the business)
- NEVER hedges ("might," "could potentially," "it's possible that")
- NEVER uses academic jargon ("macroeconomic," "fiscal multiplier") without translating it
- NEVER sounds like a think tank ("our research suggests")
- NEVER sounds optimistic without proof ("things are getting better" — show the numbers)

## Tone Spectrum
- **Formal:** Economic analyses, policy recommendations, town-level case studies
- **Casual:** Never. OE doesn't do casual. It does direct.
- **Urgent:** When extraction is happening in real time. "They're pulling $2M out of your town every year. You're subsidizing their shareholders."

## Vocabulary
- **Use:** extraction, retain, circulate, local, dollar, job, math, gap, organization, infrastructure, corridor, build, own
- **Avoid:** synergy, leverage, disrupt, ecosystem (unless literally ecological), stakeholder, best practices, holistic

## Reference Voices
"This brand sounds like **a punk rock farmer's almanac** — Wendell Berry's economics with the Clash's attitude, written for someone who runs a business and doesn't have time for theory."

## Differentiation
OE is the philosophy arm. It's the WHY behind everything else. Where MBT is the product and DSD is the data, OE is the argument for why small towns should exist at all. It's the most serious brand.

## Example Paragraphs

**Opening argument:**
Small towns aren't dying because people left. People left because the economics broke. A Main Street business pays rent, utilities, insurance, payroll, and inventory — then competes with a platform that pays none of those things and ships from a warehouse in Nevada. The math doesn't work. Not because the business is bad. Because the infrastructure is missing.

**Case study intro:**
Natchez, Mississippi. Population 14,000. Thirty-seven businesses on Main Street. Total annual revenue: $8.2 million. Total revenue leaving town — to platforms, to payment processors, to ad networks, to software subscriptions headquartered elsewhere: $3.1 million. That's a 38% extraction rate. For every dollar earned on Main Street, thirty-eight cents leaves before anyone gets paid.

**The pitch:**
The gap isn't technology. Every tool exists. Cloud computing, AI, broadcast software, payment processing, content management — all of it costs less than a part-time employee. The gap is organization. Someone has to wire the tools together, point them at the town, and turn them on. That's what Measurably Better does. That's why it costs $20 a month and not $2,000.
