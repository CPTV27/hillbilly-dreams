## Book 2: The Hands That Built Natchez — A History of Black Life on the Mississippi

**Partner:** TBD (Natchez Museum of African American History, local churches, community organizations)
**Revenue Split:** TBD — consider donating percentage to local preservation/education
**Format:** 10×12 hardcover, 160-200 pages
**Retail Price:** $60
**Production Cost:** ~$20/unit at 500

**Content Structure:**
1. Forks of the Road — The second-largest slave market in America
   - The site today (NPS marker, the ground itself)
   - Historical context — what happened here, how many people, where they came from
   - The auction process — unflinching account

2. The Builders — Enslaved craftsmen who built the mansions
   - The irony: Pilgrimage tours celebrate the houses, rarely name the people who built them
   - Brick masons, carpenters, ironworkers — the skills that built a city
   - Specific houses and what's known about who built them

3. The Churches — Living history
   - Zion Chapel AME (founded 1866)
   - Holy Family Catholic Church
   - Rose Hill Baptist
   - Oral histories from congregation elders

4. The Families — Descendants still in Natchez
   - Portrait series: families who've been here 150+ years
   - Their stories in their own words
   - What they remember being told, what they've learned, what they want preserved

5. The Other Natchez — The neighborhoods tourists don't visit
   - Pine Ridge
   - St. Catherine Street corridor
   - The community that exists parallel to the antebellum fantasy

6. The River — The Mississippi as witness
   - The Underground Railroad routes
   - The Vidalia connection (Louisiana side)
   - The river as both boundary and escape

7. Reclamation — Modern Black Natchez
   - Black-owned businesses
   - Artists, musicians, makers
   - The next generation's relationship with the history

**Research Sources:**
- Natchez Museum of African American History & Culture
- Mississippi Department of Archives and History
- NPS Natchez National Historical Park (Forks of the Road unit)
- Concordia Parish (Vidalia) historical records
- Church archives and oral history recordings
- Community elders — begin interview scheduling

**Shot List Priority:**
- [ ] Forks of the Road site — multiple angles, seasons
- [ ] Church exteriors and interiors
- [ ] Elder portraits — formal, in their spaces
- [ ] Neighborhood architecture — the non-tourist Natchez
- [ ] Detail shots — hands, doors, ironwork by enslaved craftsmen
- [ ] River at Natchez Under-the-Hill
- [ ] Community gatherings, church services (with permission)

**Ethical Notes:**
- This book exists to honor, not exploit
- Every person photographed gives informed consent and sees their images before publication
- Community advisory board reviews content before print
- Revenue sharing must benefit the community documented
- No poverty tourism. No pity narrative. Dignity and truth.

**Status:** Research phase. Begin community outreach and interview scheduling.

## Production Pipeline

### Pre-Production
1. Partner agreement (rev share, distribution, content approval)
2. Content outline and shot list
3. Template layout in InDesign / Affinity Publisher (or Blurb BookWright for POD)

### Production
1. Shoot as opportunities arise — don't force a timeline
2. Edit in Lightroom/DxO (Chase's standard workflow)
3. Drop edited images into book template folders by chapter
4. Write captions and essays as images accumulate
5. Layout review when 70% complete

### Post-Production
1. Final layout and color proofing
2. Partner review and approval
3. Print proof (single copy)
4. Corrections
5. Print run

### Distribution
- Partner's physical locations (garden club gift shops, museum stores)
- BuyCurious Art marketplace (buycurious.art)
- Amazon (for reach, despite the extraction irony)
- Local bookstores along the corridor
- Big Muddy Inn — sell at the front desk

### Print Options
- **Premium:** Blurb Trade Books or local printer for hardcover ($18-22/unit at 500)
- **POD reorders:** Blurb or Amazon KDP Print for on-demand after initial run
- **Local printing:** Check Natchez/Jackson print shops for local production (keep it in the ecosystem)
