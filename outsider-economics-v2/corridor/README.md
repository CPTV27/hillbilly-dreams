# The Mississippi Corridor — Memphis to New Orleans

**450 miles. 5 cities. 1 river. One thesis.**

---

## The Thesis

Every town on the Mississippi between Memphis and New Orleans has the same problem: plenty of people come through, but the money doesn't stay. Tourism dollars flow in and flow out. Cultural value gets created locally and captured externally. The talent stays poor while the buildings get expensive.

These aren't failing towns. They're uncoordinated towns.

The corridor thesis is simple: if each city ran even one coordination experiment — one food co-op, one musician cooperative, one tour guide collective, one maker space — the results would compound. Not because the cities merge into one economy, but because they federate into a network of local economies that's stronger than any single city alone.

Federation, not scale. Five cities of 14,000-620,000 people, each running their own experiments, sharing what works, and connecting where it makes sense. A musician cooperative in Memphis can share booking infrastructure with one in New Orleans. A farm-to-table pipeline in Clarksdale can supply restaurants in Natchez. A tour guide model that works in Vicksburg can be copied in any town with a national park.

The Mississippi River already connects these places physically. The corridor project connects them economically.

## The Federation Model

These five cities don't need to merge. They need to connect.

**Shared infrastructure:**
- Booking platform: musicians in any corridor city can be found and booked through one network
- Directory: the Deep South Directory expands to cover every corridor city — curated, vetted, local-first recommendations
- Supply chain: Delta farms supply restaurants from Clarksdale to New Orleans. One logistics coordinator, shared cold chain
- Knowledge sharing: what works in Natchez gets documented and adapted for Vicksburg. What fails in Memphis saves New Orleans the trouble

**What federation looks like in practice:**
- A tourist books 3 nights at Big Muddy Inn in Natchez. The Directory recommends a day trip to Vicksburg (tour guide co-op) and a night in Clarksdale (juke joint circuit). The musician they saw in Natchez also plays a residency in New Orleans. The hot sauce they bought at the Under-the-Hill market ships to their house monthly from a Clarksdale farm.
- One trip. Five cities touched. Zero corporate intermediaries. Every dollar stays in the corridor.

**What federation is not:**
- Not a corporation. No central authority. No CEO.
- Not a franchise. Each city runs its own experiments. No one tells Clarksdale to be like Memphis.
- Not a government program. No grants, no applications, no reporting requirements.
- It's a protocol. A shared set of agreements about how to connect, how to refer, how to share what works. Like the internet — millions of small networks connected by shared standards. No one owns it. Everyone benefits.
