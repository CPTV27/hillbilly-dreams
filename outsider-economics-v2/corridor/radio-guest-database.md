# Big Muddy Radio — Guest Database & Outreach Plan

**Station:** Radio Big Muddy, broadcasting from the parlor of The Big Muddy Inn, Natchez, MS
**Host:** Chase Pierson
**Format:** Long-form conversation, recorded at the round table. The same room where Amy plays piano, where the Blues Room shows happen, where everyone ends up at the end of the night.
**Coverage:** Mississippi corridor history, culture, music, and the stories that don't get told. Memphis to New Orleans, river to ridge.

---

## Tier 1: Dream Guests (High Profile)

### Billy Bob Thornton

**Why he's a fit:** Billy Bob grew up poor in Hot Springs, Arkansas — right on the corridor's western edge. He's not Hollywood's idea of Southern, he's the actual thing. He plays blues with the Boxmasters, has talked extensively about growing up in a house with no electricity, and has a Southern Gothic sensibility that runs through everything he touches — Sling Blade, A Simple Plan, the Fargo series. He doesn't romanticize poverty and he doesn't apologize for where he's from. That's exactly the voice this show needs.

**Connection points:**
- **The Boxmasters** — his band plays roots rock and country. They tour clubs, not arenas. He takes the music seriously, not as a celebrity vanity project.
- **Southern Gothic sensibility** — Sling Blade was set in rural Arkansas. He wrote it, directed it, starred in it. The character of Karl Childers is one of the most honest portrayals of poor Southern life ever put on screen.
- **A Simple Plan, Jayne Mansfield's Car** — films about small-town people making desperate choices. The corridor is full of those stories.
- **His public interviews** — he's talked on Howard Stern, Marc Maron, and others about growing up with nothing, his grandfather's influence, and the culture of the rural South. He's not guarded about it.
- **Monster's Ball** — filmed in Louisiana, deals directly with race, poverty, and the death penalty in the Deep South.

**Outreach strategy:**
1. **Agent contact:** Billy Bob is represented by CAA (Creative Artists Agency). His publicist and manager handle press inquiries. The ask should go through his music publicist (Boxmasters side), not his film agent — the music people are more approachable and the pitch aligns with the Boxmasters' brand.
2. **Social media approach:** Billy Bob is active on Instagram (@billybobthorntonfanpage is fan-run; his actual presence is sporadic). The Boxmasters have their own social accounts. Comment engagement on Boxmasters posts about touring the South, then a direct DM pitch — short, specific, no fawning.
3. **The pitch:** "We're building something real in Natchez, not Hollywood South. A 40-seat room in a 200-year-old inn. We record radio shows at a round table in the parlor. No greenscreen, no production assistant with a clipboard. Just conversation about the South that made us. You've talked about Hot Springs your whole career. We want to talk about why the corridor — from your hometown to ours — matters. The Boxmasters should play the Blues Room while you're here. It seats 40. Your kind of room."
4. **Sweetener:** Offer a Boxmasters live session at the Blues Room, streamed on Radio Big Muddy. Intimate venue, real audience, good recording. That's content his team can use too.

**Episode concept: "The South That Made You"**
Billy Bob on growing up in Hot Springs. The house with no running water. His grandfather's stories. How the blues found him. Why he keeps going back to Southern stories in his work. The difference between how Hollywood sees the South and how the South actually is. What he hears in the music. Whether the corridor — the river towns, the Delta, the ridge — still has what it had when he was a kid, or if it's gone.

### Morgan Freeman

**Why he's a fit:** He literally lives in the corridor. Charleston, Mississippi — 30 miles from Clarksdale. He co-owns Ground Zero Blues Club in Clarksdale with Bill Luckett. He put real money into the Delta when nobody else would. He's not a tourist, he's a resident. He chose to live in one of the poorest places in America after he could have lived anywhere on earth.

**Connection points:**
- **Ground Zero Blues Club** — co-owned with Bill Luckett since 2001. The club is the anchor of Clarksdale's tourism economy. He didn't franchise it or phone it in — it's a real juke joint.
- **Charleston, MS residence** — he's been living in the Delta for decades. His farm, his community ties, his daily life is in the corridor.
- **Madidi restaurant** — another Clarksdale investment (now closed, but the story matters).
- **His public advocacy** for Mississippi — he's defended the state when outsiders dismiss it, while also being honest about its problems.
- **The blues** — he's spoken publicly about what the music means to him and why it matters that it stays in the Delta.

**Outreach strategy:**
1. **Bill Luckett connection:** Luckett is the more accessible partner. He's a Clarksdale attorney, former mayoral candidate, and active in Mississippi politics and business. He's the door. Chase should reach out to Luckett first — businessman to businessman. "We're building a media company anchored in Natchez that tells corridor stories. We'd love to have you on, and we'd love to eventually have Morgan on to talk about what Ground Zero meant for Clarksdale."
2. **Agent route:** Freeman is represented by WME. His publicist is selective. The pitch needs to be specific and different from the thousand interview requests he gets. The angle: this isn't a press junket. This is a conversation between two people who chose to live in Mississippi.
3. **Local network:** Natchez and Clarksdale are 3.5 hours apart, but the Mississippi business and cultural community is small. Someone at Visit Mississippi, the Mississippi Arts Commission, or the Mississippi Delta National Heritage Area likely has a warm connection.

**Episode concept: "Why Mississippi"**
Morgan Freeman on choosing to live in the Delta. What Charleston looks like at 6 AM. What Ground Zero was supposed to be versus what it became. Whether celebrity investment actually changes a town or just puts a famous face on the same problems. What he'd tell a young person in the Delta right now. What the blues sounds like in your own backyard versus what it sounds like exported to a festival in Chicago.

### Buddy Guy

**Why he's a fit:** Born in Lettsworth, Louisiana — corridor town on the west bank of the Mississippi. Started playing on a two-string diddley bow he made himself. Moved to Chicago and became one of the greatest living blues guitarists. He's the bridge between the Delta tradition and everything that came after. He's 89 years old and still performing. The stories he carries are irreplaceable.

**Connection points:**
- Born in Lettsworth, LA (between Natchez and Baton Rouge on the river)
- Learned guitar from listening to John Lee Hooker and Muddy Waters on the radio — the same radio waves that traveled the corridor
- His autobiography "When I Left Home" details growing up in rural Louisiana
- Legends nightclub in Chicago — he built his own room, just like we're building ours
- Direct influence on Hendrix, Clapton, Stevie Ray Vaughan

**Outreach strategy:** His team at Buddy Guy's Legends in Chicago handles bookings and press. The pitch: "You left the corridor. The corridor never left you. Come back and talk about Lettsworth." Offer to record at Ground Zero in Clarksdale or at the Blues Room in Natchez — bring the mic to him if he's touring through the South.

**Episode concept: "The Two-String Diddley Bow"**
Buddy Guy on Lettsworth, making instruments from nothing, what the radio meant in rural Louisiana, why he left, why the leaving matters, and what's still in the soil down here.

### Wendell Pierce

**Why he's a fit:** New Orleans native. Played Bunk Moreland on The Wire, Antoine Batiste on Treme. Treme is literally a show about music, culture, and survival in the corridor's southern anchor. He's also an investor and businessman — he tried to build affordable housing in the Pontchartrain Park neighborhood after Katrina. He understands the economics of place, not just the culture.

**Connection points:**
- Treme — the most corridor-relevant show ever made (music, food, preservation, gentrification, race)
- His Pontchartrain Park housing project (the business side of community investment)
- Vocal advocate for New Orleans culture and Black business ownership
- Published memoir: "The Wind in the Reeds" — about family, New Orleans, and what Katrina destroyed

**Outreach strategy:** Pierce is active on social media and responsive to genuine cultural projects. He's represented by UTA. The pitch: corridor economics, not celebrity interview. "You tried to build housing in Pontchartrain Park. We're trying to build an economy in Natchez. Same fight, different town. Come talk about it."

**Episode concept: "Treme Was a Documentary"**
Wendell Pierce on what Treme got right, what it missed, the economics of cultural preservation, and whether a corridor town can build wealth without losing its soul.

She's written for the New York Times about Mississippi, race, and memory. Her work explores the dark underbelly of seemingly proper Southern communities — exactly the kind of storytelling the corridor needs.

**Connection points:**

### Jesmyn Ward

**Why she's a fit:** Two-time National Book Award winner. Born and raised in DeLisle, Mississippi — Gulf Coast corridor. "Salvage the Bones," "Sing, Unburied, Sing," and "Men We Reaped" are the most important American novels written about Mississippi in the last 20 years. She writes about Black poverty in Mississippi with a clarity that doesn't beg for sympathy. She teaches at Tulane in New Orleans.

**Connection points:**
- DeLisle, MS — grew up in poverty on the Gulf Coast
- "Sing, Unburied, Sing" follows a family on a road trip through Mississippi — literally a corridor story
- "Men We Reaped" — memoir about the five young men she lost in her community
- Professor at Tulane (New Orleans end of the corridor)
- MacArthur Fellow

**Outreach strategy:** Through her agent at Janklow & Nesbit or through Tulane's English department. She's selective about press but passionate about Mississippi. The pitch has to be specific: "We're not asking you to explain Mississippi to outsiders. We're asking you to talk to people who live here about what you see."

**Episode concept: "Unburied"**
Jesmyn Ward on what Mississippi owes its people. The difference between a place that remembers and a place that memorializes. Why she stays.

### William Ferris

**Why he's a fit:** Former chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities. Born in Vicksburg, Mississippi. Has spent his entire career documenting Southern culture — blues, storytelling, foodways. His book "The South in Color" and documentary work are foundational. He's the academic who actually goes into the field. Now at UNC-Chapel Hill but Mississippi is in his bones.

**Connection points:**
- Vicksburg native (corridor city)
- Filmed and recorded blues musicians, storytellers, and preachers across Mississippi starting in the 1960s

**Outreach strategy:** Direct email through UNC Center for the Study of the American South. He's approachable and would likely be enthusiastic. The pitch: "You've been documenting these stories for 60 years. We want to make sure the next generation hears them."

**Episode concept: "The Field Recordings"**
William Ferris on what he found when he pointed a camera at Mississippi in the 1960s and what's still there.

## Tier 2: Historians & Scholars (Content Partners)

These are the people who make the show credible. They're not just guests — they're potential recurring contributors and content partners.

### 1. Ser Seshsh Ab Heter-CM Boxley
**Affiliation:** Natchez Museum of African American History & Culture, Director/Curator
**Specialty:** Black history of Natchez, enslaved communities, Forks of the Road slave market, African American material culture
**Why he'd be good:** He runs the primary institution in Natchez dedicated to telling the Black story of the city. He knows the granular, specific history — names, places, family lines. This isn't abstract for him.
**Pitch angle:** "You're already doing this work. Radio Big Muddy puts a microphone in front of it and sends it to 600,000 tourists a year who currently hear only half the story. We want you as a recurring voice."

### 2. Jim Barnett
**Affiliation:** Retired Director, Historic Preservation Division, Mississippi Department of Archives and History
**Specialty:** Natchez Trace history, Native American history of the lower Mississippi, colonial-era Natchez
**Why he'd be good:** He wrote "The Natchez Indians: A History to 1735" — the definitive work on the Natchez Nation. He knows the pre-European corridor better than almost anyone alive.
**Pitch angle:** "Before there was a Trace, before there were plantations, the Natchez people built a civilization on these bluffs. You wrote the book. Let's put it on the radio."

### 3. Miki Crespi (or current NPS Natchez historian)
**Affiliation:** National Park Service, Natchez National Historical Park
**Specialty:** Forks of the Road, Melrose estate, William Johnson (the Barber of Natchez), enslaved community life
**Why they'd be good:** NPS Natchez manages both the Forks of the Road slave market site and Melrose. Their interpretive rangers have deep, specific knowledge and are trained communicators.
**Pitch angle:** "The Forks of the Road was the second-largest slave market in the deep South. Most tourists in Natchez never visit the site. We want to change that, starting with telling the story on air."

### 4. Dr. Robert Luckett
**Affiliation:** Jackson State University, Director of the Margaret Walker Center
**Specialty:** Civil rights history, Mississippi Freedom Movement, Black intellectual history
**Why he'd be good:** The Margaret Walker Center is a major civil rights archive. Luckett connects the dots between civil rights history and present-day Mississippi. He's a public intellectual, not just an academic.
**Pitch angle:** "The corridor isn't just blues and antebellum homes. It's freedom rides, voter registration, and the Klan. That history lives at Jackson State. Let's put it on the radio."

### 5. Dr. Luther Brown
**Affiliation:** Delta State University, Director of the Delta Center for Culture and Learning (Mississippi Delta National Heritage Area)
**Specialty:** Delta cultural landscape, blues heritage tourism, foodways, community development
**Why he'd be good:** He runs the Mississippi Delta National Heritage Area — the federal designation that covers the Delta portion of the corridor. He thinks about heritage tourism the same way we think about coordination economics.
**Pitch angle:** "You're trying to make the Delta's heritage pay the Delta's bills. We're trying to do the same thing in Natchez. Let's compare notes on air."

### 6. Dr. Deirdre Cooper Owens
**Affiliation:** University of Connecticut (formerly University of Mississippi)
**Specialty:** History of medicine and slavery, specifically the gynecological experiments performed on enslaved women by J. Marion Sims in Alabama and the medical exploitation of Black bodies in the antebellum South
**Why she'd be good:** Her book "Medical Bondage" covers a brutal chapter of Southern history that connects directly to the corridor's plantation economy. This is the kind of story that doesn't get told on tourism brochures.
**Pitch angle:** "The antebellum economy wasn't just cotton and sugar. It was the commodification of human bodies in every possible way, including medicine. That's a corridor story."

### 7. Dr. Robbie Ethridge
**Affiliation:** University of Mississippi, Department of Sociology and Anthropology
**Specialty:** Southeastern Native American history, the Natchez people, Choctaw and Chickasaw nations, the Indian slave trade
**Why she'd be good:** She's one of the leading scholars on the Indigenous peoples of the lower Mississippi. Her work on the "Mississippian shatter zone" — the destruction of Native societies by European contact — is essential corridor history.
**Pitch angle:** "The Natchez Trace is named for a nation that was nearly erased. Before we tell the story of the road, we need to tell the story of the people."

### 8. Dr. John Marszalek
**Affiliation:** Mississippi State University (emeritus), Executive Director of the Ulysses S. Grant Presidential Library
**Specialty:** Civil War history, the Vicksburg Campaign, Grant's Mississippi operations
**Why he'd be good:** Vicksburg is a corridor city, and the siege is one of the most consequential military operations in American history. Marszalek makes it vivid and connects it to the broader story of the river.
**Pitch angle:** "Grant took the river. That changed the war. That changed the corridor. And the people of Vicksburg ate rats for 47 days. Let's talk about what that did to a city's memory."

### 9. Dr. Amy Tikkanen (or current Southern Foodways Alliance director)
**Affiliation:** Southern Foodways Alliance, University of Mississippi
**Specialty:** Southern food history, oral histories of food workers, the economics of Southern food culture
**Why they'd be good:** SFA has the best oral history archive on Southern food in existence. They interview the cooks, the farmers, the people who actually make the food — not just the chefs with restaurants.
**Pitch angle:** "Every corridor city has a food story. Natchez has Regina Charboneau. Clarksdale has the tamale trail. Memphis has barbecue. New Orleans has everything. But the people who cook this food are rarely the ones who profit from it. That's our beat."

### 10. Dr. Charles Reagan Wilson
**Affiliation:** University of Mississippi (emeritus), Center for the Study of Southern Culture
**Specialty:** Southern religion, Southern identity, the "Lost Cause" mythology
**Why he'd be good:** He co-founded the Encyclopedia of Southern Culture. He understands how the South tells stories about itself — including the lies it tells. That critical perspective is essential for a show that claims to tell "the stories that don't get told."
**Pitch angle:** "The South has a story it tells tourists and a story it tells itself. Neither one is the whole truth. You've spent your career finding what's in between."

### 11. Dr. Kellie Carter Jackson
**Affiliation:** Wellesley College
**Specialty:** African American history, political violence, the history of Black resistance
**Why she'd be good:** Her work on force and freedom connects directly to the corridor's history of enslaved resistance, maroon communities, and the Natchez Revolt of 1811. She's also a strong public communicator — frequent media appearances.
**Pitch angle:** "The corridor has a resistance history that's as deep as its blues history. Natchez had armed revolt. The river had runaways. Let's tell the part of the story where people fought back."

### 12. Ian Brown
**Affiliation:** Alabama Museum of Natural History (or independent researcher)
**Specialty:** Lower Mississippi Valley archaeology, Natchez and Tunica peoples, pre-Columbian mound-builder cultures
**Why he'd be good:** The lower Mississippi Valley was one of the most complex pre-Columbian civilizations in North America — mound-builders, trade networks, agricultural societies. Brown's archaeological work gives the corridor a 2,000-year timeline, not a 300-year one.
**Pitch angle:** "People were building cities on these bluffs a thousand years before the French showed up. The mounds are still here. Let's start at the beginning."

### 13. Dr. Mary Niall Mitchell
**Affiliation:** University of New Orleans, Midlo Center for New Orleans Studies
**Specialty:** Slavery, race, and childhood in the antebellum South; visual culture of slavery
**Why she'd be good:** Her work on the visual representation of enslaved people — photographs, illustrations, advertisements — adds a visceral dimension to corridor history. She's also based in New Orleans, the southern anchor.
**Pitch angle:** "There are photographs of enslaved people taken in the corridor. Daguerreotypes. Actual faces. What do we do with those images now? That's a conversation we need to have."

### 14. Greg Iles
**Affiliation:** Author, Natchez native
**Specialty:** Novels set in Natchez — the Penn Cage series deals directly with Natchez history, race, the Klan, and the murders that shaped the city
**Why he'd be good:** He was born in Natchez. His novels are bestsellers that put Natchez on the map for millions of readers. The Penn Cage books deal with real events — the murders of Black citizens by the Klan, the Silver Dollar Group, and the unresolved violence of the civil rights era in Natchez.
**Pitch angle:** "You fictionalized Natchez's darkest history and millions of people read it. Now let's talk about the real version."

## Tier 3: Local Voices (Community)

These are the people who make the show matter to the community, not just to listeners. They live here. They built this.

### Museum Curators & Institutional Voices

- **Natchez Museum of African American History & Culture** — Director/curator and docents. The museum is small, underfunded, and essential. Getting their voices on air is both good radio and good advocacy.
- **Delta Blues Museum, Clarksdale** — Curators who can speak to the physical artifacts of blues history: instruments, clothing, recordings, photographs. What does a museum do when the culture it preserves is still alive in the juke joints down the street?
- **B.B. King Museum, Indianola** — The museum is excellent and undervisited. B.B. King's story is a corridor story: born in Berclair, MS, raised near Indianola, moved to Memphis, conquered the world. Came back.
- **Grammy Museum Mississippi, Cleveland** — The Delta outpost of the Grammy Museum. Their programming connects national music history to Mississippi origins.
- **Historic Natchez Foundation** — Preservation advocacy, architectural history, the stories behind the houses that the Pilgrimage tours tell (and the ones they skip).
- **Longwood docents** — Longwood is the unfinished octagonal mansion in Natchez. The workers left when the Civil War started. The upper floors are still raw brick and exposed timber. The docents who tell that story daily have refined it into something powerful.

### Church Elders & Community Historians

- **Zion Chapel A.M.E. Church, Natchez** — One of the oldest Black congregations in Natchez. The elders carry oral history that isn't written down anywhere. This is urgent work — these voices won't be here forever.
- **Holy Family Catholic Church, Natchez** — Historic Black Catholic parish. The intersection of Catholicism and Black Southern life is a story almost nobody tells.
- **Community elders in the neighborhoods east of downtown Natchez** — The historically Black neighborhoods. People who remember segregation, who remember the Rhythm Nightclub Fire (1940 — 209 people killed at a Black dance hall, the deadliest fire in Mississippi history), who remember what the town was before tourism.

### Working Musicians Along the Corridor

- **Blues Room regulars** — The musicians who play the Big Muddy Inn's 40-seat room. They're the heartbeat. Record conversations with them before or after sets.
- **Clarksdale juke joint musicians** — Red's Lounge, Ground Zero, New Roxy. The players who work the Delta circuit.
- **Memphis session musicians** — The surviving studio players from Royal, Stax, Sun. Their stories are being lost as they age out.
- **New Orleans brass band musicians** — Hot 8, Rebirth, Treme Brass Band. The living tradition of second lines and brass bands.
- **Natchez street musicians and church musicians** — The people who play for free, who play for God, who play because they can't not play.

### Amy Allen (Big Muddy Inn Team)

Amy is already part of Radio Big Muddy — she's on Episode 1, she plays piano at the Inn, she's family. Her song about the Rhythm Nightclub Fire is a centerpiece waiting to happen. An episode built around Amy performing and discussing the song, intercut with historical context and community voices who remember or carry the memory of the fire, could be one of the most powerful things the show ever produces.

**Episode concept:** Amy performs "The Fire" (working title) live at the round table. Between verses, we hear from community members, historians, and descendants. The episode becomes both performance and memorial.

### Preservation & Heritage Societies

- **Natchez Pilgrimage Garden Club** — They run the Spring and Fall Pilgrimages. They're the establishment. A frank conversation about what the Pilgrimage tells and what it omits would be extraordinary radio. Also a partnership for the book project.
- **Natchez National Historical Park volunteers and rangers** — NPS staff who interpret Melrose and Forks of the Road daily.
- **Natchez Heritage Tourism Bureau** — The official tourism voice. Good to have on record, and useful for the show's relationship with the broader tourism economy.
- **Mississippi Heritage Trust** — Statewide preservation advocacy. They identify endangered properties — every one of those is a story.

## Tier 4: Adjacent Voices

### Food Writers & Southern Foodways

- **John T. Edge** — Founding director of the Southern Foodways Alliance at Ole Miss. He's the most prominent voice in Southern food writing. Books include "The Potlikker Papers." He'd connect food to economics, race, and culture. Likely accessible through SFA.
- **Michael Twitty** — Author of "The Cooking Gene," which traces African American food history through DNA and recipes. He literally cooked his way through the antebellum South. Essential voice.
- **Lolis Eric Elie** — New Orleans food writer, screenwriter on Treme. He bridges food, culture, and storytelling. Co-wrote the "Treme" cookbook.
- **Regina Charboneau** — Already in the Big Muddy orbit (she's in Episode 1, the biscuit class connection). Natchez-based, nationally known. She's Tier 3 by geography but Tier 4 by reach. A recurring presence.

### Photographers Documenting the Region

- **Maude Schuyler Clay** — Mississippi Delta photographer. Her book "Delta Land" is a definitive visual document of the landscape. Born in Greenwood, MS.
- **Birney Imes** — Photographer who documented Mississippi juke joints in the 1980s and 90s. His book "Juke Joint" is iconic. These spaces are disappearing.
- **William Eggleston** — Memphis-based, one of the most important photographers alive. His color photography of the everyday South changed art. Long shot but worth asking.
- **David Wharton** — Photographer and author of "The Soul of a Small Texas Town" — his approach to documenting small-town life aligns with what Big Muddy is doing visually.

### Authors with Corridor Settings

- **Greg Iles** — (also in Tier 2) Natchez native, Penn Cage series
- **Tom Franklin** — Born in Dickinson, AL. "Crooked Letter, Crooked Letter" is set in rural Mississippi. Crime fiction rooted in race and place.
- **Michael Farris Smith** — Lives in Columbus, MS. "Desperation Road" and other novels set in Mississippi. He writes about the people the economy left behind.
- **Jamie Quatro** — "Fire Sermon" and short stories set in the small-town South. Her work explores faith, desire, and the tension between them in Southern life.
- **Kiese Laymon** — From Jackson, MS. "Heavy" is a memoir about growing up Black in Mississippi. "Long Division" is a novel about Mississippi across time. National Book Award winner.

### Podcasters for Cross-Promotion

- **Reveille (Mississippi Today podcast)** — Mississippi's best investigative journalism outlet. They cover the state seriously. Cross-promotion would give both shows credibility.
- **BackStory / The Past Is Never (general history podcasts)** — Shows that do deep-dive American history. A corridor episode collaboration.
- **Gravy (Southern Foodways Alliance podcast)** — SFA's podcast covers food and culture in the South. Natural cross-promotion partner.
- **Oxford American magazine's podcast programming** — The Oxford American is the premier Southern literary and music magazine. They're based in Arkansas and would be a prestige cross-promotion.
- **Gonna Be On Natchez (local Natchez podcast)** — If it exists or when it exists, cross-promotion with any local voice that's telling Natchez stories.
- **Mississippi Folklife podcast / Mississippi Arts Commission** — State-funded arts programming. They want to amplify Mississippi voices — that's what we do.

## Episode Series Concepts

### Series 1: "Before the Forks" — Indigenous and Black History of the Natchez Region
**Episodes:** 6
**Thesis:** Before the antebellum mansions, before the slave market, before the Trace was a road, the Natchez bluffs were home to one of the most sophisticated civilizations in North America. This series starts at the beginning.

1. **"The Grand Village"** — The Natchez Nation: their civilization, their mound-building, their social structure, and the Grand Village site that still exists in Natchez today. Guest: Jim Barnett or Robbie Ethridge.
2. **"The French War"** — The Natchez Revolt of 1729 and the French destruction of the Natchez Nation. One of the most consequential events in corridor history that almost nobody knows. Guest: Jim Barnett, Ian Brown.
3. **"The Trace Before the Road"** — The Natchez Trace as a Native American trade route, not a pioneer road. What it connected, who walked it, what they carried. Guest: NPS Natchez Trace historian.
4. **"Forks of the Road"** — The slave market. What happened there, how many people passed through, where they came from and where they went. The site today — a roadside marker and an empty field. Guest: NPS Natchez interpreter, Ser Seshsh Ab Heter-CM Boxley.
5. **"The Builders"** — The enslaved craftsmen who built the mansions. Their names, their skills, their lives. The irony that Pilgrimage tours celebrate the houses and rarely name the people who built them. Guest: Natchez Museum of African American History curator, Greg Iles.
6. **"The Choctaw Removal"** — The Trail of Tears passed through Mississippi. The Choctaw were removed from their land along the corridor. Some stayed. Their descendants are still here. Guest: Robbie Ethridge, Choctaw Nation cultural representative.

### Series 2: "The Fire" — The Rhythm Nightclub Fire
**Episodes:** 5
**Thesis:** On April 23, 1940, 209 Black men and women died in a fire at the Rhythm Night Club in Natchez, Mississippi. It was the deadliest fire in Mississippi history and one of the deadliest in American history. It has been almost entirely forgotten outside of Natchez. This series refuses to let that stand.

1. **"The Dance"** — What the Rhythm Night Club was. A wooden dance hall in the Black community. Who went there, what the music sounded like, what the night of April 23 was supposed to be. Walter Barnes and his Royal Creolians were performing. Set the scene. Guest: community elders, local historian.
2. **"The Fire"** — What happened. The boarded-up windows. The single exit. The Spanish moss used as decoration catching fire. Two hundred and nine people. The community response in the immediate aftermath. Guest: descendants, community historian, fire historian.
3. **"The Aftermath"** — The funerals. The mass graves. The community trauma. How Natchez's Black community rebuilt — and how white Natchez largely ignored it. Guest: church elders, Zion Chapel A.M.E. Church.
4. **"The Song"** — Amy Allen's original song about the fire. She performs it live at the round table. Between sections, we hear from community members and descendants. The episode is part performance, part memorial. Guest: Amy Allen, community voices.
5. **"The Memorial"** — The fight to memorialize the fire. The marker that exists now. What a proper memorial would look like. How a community remembers what the country forgot. Guest: preservation advocates, descendants, city officials.

### Series 3: "The Corridor" — Memphis to New Orleans, City by City
**Episodes:** 6
**Thesis:** The Mississippi corridor isn't one place — it's a chain of places connected by the river, the road, the railroad, and the music. Each city has its own economy, its own culture, and its own version of the same problem: how do you build wealth in a place that the national economy forgot?

1. **"Memphis: The Bluff City"** — Beale Street, Sun Studio, Stax, the sanitation workers' strike, the assassination of MLK. Memphis is the corridor's northern anchor and its most complicated city. Guest: Memphis historian, Stax Museum representative.
2. **"The Delta: Clarksdale to Indianola"** — Ground Zero, the Delta Blues Museum, B.B. King Museum, the Crossroads. The most important music landscape in America, and one of the poorest. Guest: Luther Brown, Delta musician.
3. **"Vicksburg: The Siege City"** — 47 days. The siege that gave the Union the river. What that did to a city's identity for 160 years. Vicksburg didn't celebrate the Fourth of July until 1945. Guest: John Marszalek, Vicksburg historian.
4. **"Natchez: The Proof of Concept"** — Our city. The antebellum homes, the bluff, the river, the 600,000 tourists, the 34% poverty rate. What Big Muddy is trying to build and why. Guest: local voices — Amy, Tracy, community members.
5. **"The River Road: Baton Rouge to New Orleans"** — Plantation country. The Great River Road. Cancer Alley. The most visible and the most toxic stretch of the corridor. The mansions tourists visit and the petrochemical plants they drive past. Guest: environmental justice advocate, River Road historian.
6. **"New Orleans: The Mouth"** — Where the corridor meets the Gulf. The music, the food, the flooding, the gentrification. What New Orleans teaches every other corridor city about the price of being a cultural destination. Guest: Wendell Pierce, Lolis Eric Elie.

### Series 4: "The Builders" — The Enslaved Craftsmen Who Built the Antebellum South
**Episodes:** 5
**Thesis:** Every antebellum mansion in the corridor was built by enslaved people. Brick masons, carpenters, ironworkers, plasterers — master craftsmen whose names were rarely recorded and whose skills are still visible in every room the tourists walk through. This series names them.

1. **"The Hands"** — The skills required to build a Natchez mansion. Brick-making, carpentry, plastering, ironwork. Where enslaved craftsmen learned these trades and how their work compares to any master builder in Europe. Guest: architectural historian, preservation specialist.
2. **"Stanton Hall"** — One house, told through the people who built it. Who were the enslaved workers at Stanton Hall? What records exist? What can the building itself tell us about the people who made it? Guest: Historic Natchez Foundation, Natchez Museum of African American History.
3. **"Longwood"** — The unfinished octagonal mansion. The Northern workers left when the war started. The enslaved workers stayed. The upper floors are still raw — you can see the construction methods frozen in time. Guest: Longwood docent, architectural historian.
4. **"The Ironworkers"** — The decorative ironwork of Natchez and New Orleans was largely made by enslaved and free Black craftsmen. The fences, balconies, and gates that define the Southern aesthetic were Black art. Guest: ironwork historian, New Orleans preservation specialist.
5. **"Still Building"** — Black craftsmen today. The tradition of skilled trades in the Black community along the corridor. Who's still doing this work? How do you carry forward a tradition that was born in bondage? Guest: contemporary Black craftsmen, trade school leaders.

### Series 5: "Still Here" — Living Voices, Present-Day Corridor Culture
**Episodes:** 6
**Thesis:** The corridor isn't a museum. People live here. They work, cook, play music, raise kids, argue about parking, go to church, and try to figure out how to stay in a place that the economy keeps trying to leave behind. This series is about right now.

1. **"Sunday Morning"** — Church along the corridor. What happens inside Black churches in Natchez, Clarksdale, Vicksburg, and Memphis on a Sunday morning. The music, the preaching, the community, the economics of a church in a town where the median income is $29,000. Guest: pastors, choir directors, church elders from multiple corridor cities.
2. **"The Kitchen"** — Food as culture, food as economics, food as survival. The cooks — not the chefs, the cooks — who feed the corridor. Plate lunch spots, tamale vendors, the woman who caters funerals. Guest: corridor cooks, John T. Edge or Michael Twitty.
3. **"The Set"** — A live music episode. Recording musicians playing at the Blues Room, at Red's Lounge in Clarksdale, at a house concert in Indianola. Between songs, conversation about what it means to play the blues in 2026, where the money goes, and why they stay. Guest: working musicians.
4. **"The Porch"** — A conversation about neighborhoods. The residential streets behind the tourist districts. Who lives there, what they see, what they wish the tourists knew. Recorded on an actual porch. Guest: neighborhood residents in Natchez and Clarksdale.
5. **"The Young Ones"** — People under 30 who stayed in the corridor or came back. Why? What do they see that their parents don't? What would make them leave? What would make them stay? Guest: young entrepreneurs, artists, teachers along the corridor.
6. **"The Table"** — A round-table episode at the Big Muddy Inn with six people from six different corridor cities. No agenda. Just: "What does this place mean to you, and what does it need?" Guest: one voice each from Memphis, Clarksdale, Vicksburg, Natchez, Baton Rouge, and New Orleans.

## Production Notes

**Recording location:** The round table in the parlor of The Big Muddy Inn, 411 North Commerce Street, Natchez, MS. Remote recording available for guests who can't travel.

**Format:** Long-form conversation, 45-90 minutes per episode. No ads (for now). No script. A few prepared questions, then let it run.

**Distribution:** Podcast (Apple, Spotify, YouTube), live stream via Restream, clips cut for social (TikTok, Instagram, Twitter/X).

**Priority booking order:**
1. Local voices (Tier 3) — build the show's credibility and community relationship first
2. Historians (Tier 2) — establish the show as serious and substantive
3. Adjacent voices (Tier 4) — expand the audience through cross-promotion
4. Dream guests (Tier 1) — when the show has 20+ episodes and a body of work to point to

You don't pitch Billy Bob Thornton with zero episodes. You pitch him with a catalog that proves you're the real thing. Build the table first. The dream guests come to a table that's already full.
