# Natchez History Content Infrastructure

**Big Muddy Media — The Definitive Regional History Repository**

Natchez is not a museum. It's a town that has been continuously inhabited longer than almost any place on the Mississippi River. The history here isn't behind glass — it's in the soil, the street names, the architecture, the music, and the people who never left.

Big Muddy's job is to tell these stories better than anyone else. Not as nostalgia tourism. Not as Lost Cause mythology. As the real, documented, complicated truth of what happened here — and what it means for the people who live here now.

Every piece in this repository is designed to publish across all Big Muddy brands: Magazine, Radio, social media, podcast, and the Inn's guest library. One research investment, infinite distribution.

## Upcoming Content (Planned)

### 6. The Pilgrimage: How Natchez Sold Itself
The Spring and Fall Pilgrimages, the Garden Club women, the Confederate pageantry, and the slow reckoning with what was actually being celebrated. How the tourism industry was built on a selective telling of history — and how that's changing.

### 7. William Johnson: The Barber of Natchez
A free Black man who kept a detailed diary from 1835-1851. One of the most important primary source documents of antebellum life in the South. His house is a National Park site on State Street.

### 8. The Devil's Backbone
Natchez-to-Nashville overland routes, the history of banditry, and the communities that formed along the ridgeline. Overlaps with Trace content but focuses on the outlaw economy.

### 9. River Commerce and the Steamboat Era
Natchez as one of the wealthiest cities in America before the Civil War. The cotton economy, the steamboat races, and the crash that followed the war.

### 10. The Civil Rights Movement in Natchez
The Wharlest Jackson assassination (1967). The bombing of George Metcalf. The Deacons for Defense. Natchez's civil rights history is underreported compared to Jackson and the Delta.

### 11. The Great Migration and Natchez
Who left, who stayed, and what happened to the town when thousands of Black residents moved north. The depopulation that created the Natchez of today.

## Voice Rules for History Content

- No Lost Cause framing. Ever. The mansions were built by enslaved people. Say it.
- Numbers first. How many people. How much money. What year. Specificity is credibility.
- Name people. History happened to individuals, not abstractions.
- Flag what needs verification. Better to say "this needs a primary source" than to publish something wrong.
- No hashtags. No emojis. No "Did you know?" hooks. Just tell the story.
- Southern but not folksy. Direct but not cold. This is journalism, not tourism marketing.

## Research Priorities

1. **Rhythm Nightclub Fire** — Contact the Rhythm Night Club Memorial Museum. Get primary sources, survivor accounts, photographs. This is the flagship piece.
2. **Forks of the Road** — National Park Service has been developing this site. Get the latest research from NPS historians.
3. **William Johnson diary** — Available through the LSU Press publication. The NPS site on State Street has interpretive materials.
4. **Civil rights history** — The Wharlest Jackson story needs a dedicated investigative piece. The FBI files may be partially declassified.
5. **Indigenous Natchez** — The Grand Village of the Natchez is a state historic site. Their interpretive staff would be key contacts.
