# Before the Forks: Indigenous and Black History of the Natchez Region

**The history they don't put on the Pilgrimage brochure.**

---

## The Natchez People

Before Natchez was a city, Natchez was a people.

The Natchez Nation was one of the most sophisticated indigenous societies in the lower Mississippi Valley. At their peak, they numbered somewhere between 4,000 and 6,000 people and controlled territory along the eastern bank of the Mississippi River in present-day southwestern Mississippi. Their principal settlement complex was centered at the Grand Village, near present-day Natchez — now a state historic site you can visit on Jefferson Davis Boulevard.

What made the Natchez distinct:

- **Mound-building culture.** The Natchez built and maintained large ceremonial earthen mounds, a tradition connected to the older Mississippian culture (Cahokia and its descendants). The Grand Village included at least three major mounds.
- **Stratified social system.** The Natchez had a hierarchical society with a paramount chief called the Great Sun. Below the Great Sun were Suns, Nobles, Honored People, and Stinkards (commoners). This was unusual in its rigidity among southeastern indigenous nations.
- **Agricultural economy.** Corn, beans, squash, supplemented by hunting and fishing. The river valley bottomlands were extraordinarily fertile.
- **Matrilineal descent.** Leadership and social rank passed through the maternal line.
- **Solar worship.** The Great Sun maintained a perpetual sacred fire in the main temple. French observers compared the Natchez religion to sun worship traditions they knew from the Old World, though the comparison was reductive.

The French established Fort Rosalie near the Grand Village in 1716. The relationship between the French and the Natchez deteriorated over the next thirteen years as French settlers demanded more land and labor. In November 1729, the Natchez launched a coordinated attack on Fort Rosalie, killing approximately 230 French settlers and taking hundreds of women, children, and enslaved Africans as captives.

The French response was total war. With Choctaw allies, the French attacked the Natchez over the following years. By 1731, the Natchez Nation as a sovereign political entity was effectively destroyed. Survivors scattered — some were sold into slavery in the Caribbean, some took refuge with the Chickasaw, the Creek, and the Cherokee. Some remained in the region, absorbed into other communities.

The city that bears their name was built on their land, over their mounds, after their destruction. That's where the story of Natchez begins, whether the tourism board acknowledges it or not.

## The Black-Indigenous Connection

This is the part that most histories skip, and the part that needs the most careful research.

### Documented Connections Between Escaped Enslaved People and the Natchez Nation

French colonial records document interactions between escaped enslaved Africans and the Natchez people as early as the 1720s. This is not speculative — it's in the colonial archives.

Key points:

- **The 1729 revolt.** When the Natchez attacked Fort Rosalie, they took approximately 150-300 enslaved Africans from the French settlement. The exact fate of these captives is debated by historians. Some were likely adopted into Natchez communities. Some may have been traded. Some were recaptured by the French in subsequent military actions.
- **Maroon communities.** Throughout the colonial period, enslaved people escaped into the forests and swamps of the Natchez region. Some formed independent maroon communities. Others sought refuge with indigenous nations — the Natchez before their dispersal, and later the Choctaw and Chickasaw.
- **French records from the 1720s** mention escaped enslaved people living among the Natchez. [NEEDS PRIMARY SOURCE VERIFICATION — check the *Mississippi Provincial Archives: French Dominion* series, published by the Mississippi Department of Archives and History]
- **Intermarriage and mixed communities.** By the late colonial and early American period, there were documented individuals and families in the Natchez region whose ancestry included both African and indigenous lines. The rigid racial categories of later American law didn't map onto the social reality of the 18th-century lower Mississippi Valley.

### The Question

Were some Black families in the Natchez region descended from indigenous populations — not solely from the transatlantic slave trade?

The answer, based on available evidence, is almost certainly yes. But the documentation is fragmentary, and the subject requires careful handling:

- **Colonial census records** sometimes classified people as "free people of color" without distinguishing between African, indigenous, and mixed ancestry.
- **The one-drop rule** and later American racial classification systems erased indigenous ancestry among people classified as Black.
- **Oral histories** in some Natchez-area Black families include references to indigenous ancestry, but oral tradition alone doesn't satisfy academic standards without supporting documentation.
- **DNA evidence** from modern genetic studies has shown that many African Americans in the lower Mississippi Valley carry some indigenous genetic markers, though this could reflect mixing at various points across centuries.

This is a story that needs more research, not less. Big Muddy's role is to ask the question publicly, point people toward the existing scholarship, and support further investigation — not to make claims that outrun the evidence.

## Free People of Color in Natchez

By 1801 — before Mississippi became a state, while the territory was still under shifting Spanish, French, and American authority — there were at least 150 documented free people of color living in the Natchez District.

This is a remarkable number for several reasons:

- **The Natchez District was a slaveholding region.** By 1800, enslaved people outnumbered free people in the district. The presence of 150+ free Black people in this context reflects a complex social reality that doesn't fit the simple binary of "enslaved or free, Black or white."
- **Spanish colonial law** (Natchez was under Spanish control from 1779 to 1798) was comparatively more permissive toward manumission than Anglo-American law. Under the Spanish system, enslaved people could purchase their own freedom (*coartacion*), and slaveholders could free enslaved people with fewer legal obstacles. This partially explains the relatively large free Black population.
- **Free Black landowners and businesspeople.** Some free people of color in the Natchez District owned land, operated businesses, and — in some cases — owned enslaved people themselves. William Johnson, the famous "Barber of Natchez" who kept a detailed diary from 1835-1851, was a free man of color who owned property and enslaved people. His story is complicated and important.
- **After American control.** When Mississippi became a U.S. territory (1798) and then a state (1817), the legal status of free people of color progressively worsened. American racial law was less flexible than Spanish colonial law, and the antebellum period brought increasing restrictions on free Black people — requirements to register, limits on movement, prohibitions on certain occupations and activities.

### What This Means

The existence of 150+ free people of color in 1801 Natchez means that Black freedom in this region predates the United States' control of the territory. It predates the cotton boom. It predates the antebellum mansions.

When tourists visit Natchez and see the "history," they see the mansions (built 1820s-1850s). The actual history goes back further, involves more people, and is far more complex than the Pilgrimage tour suggests.

## Forks of the Road

The Forks of the Road was the second-largest slave market in the American South, after New Orleans.

Located at the intersection of Liberty Road and St. Catherine Street (now the intersection of Liberty Road and D'Evereaux Drive), the Forks operated from the early 1800s through the Civil War. At its peak in the 1830s, thousands of enslaved people were sold here every year.

### The Market

- **Location.** The Forks sat at a literal fork in the road — where the road from the river (and from Under-the-Hill) split toward the interior. Traders coming upriver from New Orleans or overland from the East brought enslaved people to this junction for sale.
- **Scale.** Isaac Franklin and John Armfield, the largest domestic slave traders in America, operated at the Forks. Their firm moved thousands of enslaved people from the Upper South (Virginia, Maryland, the Carolinas) to the Deep South (Mississippi, Louisiana) in the 1830s alone.
- **The process.** Enslaved people were held in pens and yards at the Forks, inspected by buyers, and sold. Families were routinely separated. The physical conditions of the holding pens were documented by contemporary observers as brutal.
- **The economics.** An able-bodied enslaved man in his twenties could sell for $1,000-$1,500 at the Forks in the 1830s — equivalent to roughly $35,000-$50,000 today. The cotton boom created insatiable demand for labor, and the Forks was one of the primary supply points.

### The Site Today

The National Park Service acquired the Forks of the Road site as part of the Natchez National Historical Park. As of this writing, the site includes interpretive markers but is still being developed as a full interpretive facility.

## The Builders

Every antebellum mansion in Natchez was built by enslaved people.

This is not a controversial claim. It is a basic historical fact. The brickwork, carpentry, ironwork, plastering, and finish work in the mansions that draw 600,000 tourists a year was performed by enslaved craftsmen.

Some specifics:

- **Skilled trades.** Enslaved people in Natchez included highly skilled carpenters, brickmasons, blacksmiths, plasterers, and painters. Some were trained specifically in these crafts; others learned through apprenticeship systems within the enslaved community.
- **Hired-out labor.** Some slaveholders "hired out" their skilled enslaved workers to other plantations and businesses, collecting the wages. In some cases, enslaved craftsmen were allowed to keep a portion of their earnings — a practice that occasionally enabled self-purchase of freedom.
- **Named individuals.** Some of the craftsmen are identifiable in historical records — particularly in the construction accounts of specific mansions. [NEEDS RESEARCH — go through the construction records for major Natchez mansions held at MDAH to identify specific enslaved craftsmen]
- **Architectural legacy.** The craftsmanship visible in houses like Stanton Hall, Longwood, Melrose, and Rosalie was produced by enslaved hands. Every tourist who admires the ironwork, the millwork, the brickwork is admiring the product of forced labor by people whose names are mostly lost.

When Big Muddy talks about the antebellum mansions — and we will, because they're a major tourism draw and our guests visit them — we tell the whole story. Not as an addendum. Not as a "by the way." As the story.

"This house was built by enslaved craftsmen. Here's what we know about who they were."

That's not politics. That's history. The house doesn't make sense without the people who built it.

## Academic Sources and Further Research

### Published Works
- **Daniel H. Usner Jr., "Indians, Settlers, and Slaves in a Frontier Exchange Economy"** — covers the lower Mississippi Valley's multiracial colonial economy. Essential background.
- **James F. Barnett Jr., "The Natchez Indians: A History to 1735"** — the definitive academic history of the Natchez people through French contact.
- **Ronald L.F. Davis, "The Black Experience in Natchez"** — NPS-commissioned study. [NEEDS VERIFICATION — check if this is publicly available or NPS internal]
- **William Johnson's Natchez: The Ante-Bellum Diary of a Free Negro** — edited by William Ransom Hogan and Edwin Adams Davis, published by LSU Press. Primary source.
- **Walter Johnson, "Soul by Soul: Life Inside the Antebellum Slave Market"** — covers the domestic slave trade including the Forks of the Road.
- **Edward Ball, "Slaves in the Family"** — traces the descendants of slaveholders and enslaved people. Model for the kind of research needed in Natchez.
- **Mississippi Provincial Archives: French Dominion** — primary source collection for colonial-era records including interactions between French, Natchez, and enslaved Africans.

### Archives and Institutions
- **Mississippi Department of Archives and History (MDAH)** — Jackson, MS. The primary archive for Mississippi historical records.
- **Grand Village of the Natchez Indians** — state historic site in Natchez. Interpretive staff are contacts for Natchez Nation history.
- **Natchez National Historical Park (NPS)** — manages the Forks of the Road, Melrose, and William Johnson House. NPS historians are key contacts.
- **Natchez Museum of African American History and Culture** — local museum on St. Catherine Street.
- **Historic Natchez Foundation** — preservationist organization. Has architectural records that may include construction details (and therefore enslaved craftsmen records).
- **Alcorn State University** — HBCU in Lorman, MS (30 miles from Natchez). Faculty in history and African American studies may have relevant research.
- **University of Southern Mississippi** — Center for Oral History and Cultural Heritage may have relevant collections.
