# The Rhythm Nightclub Fire — April 23, 1940

**209 dead. One building. One night. The deadliest fire in Mississippi history.**

---

## The Night

Tuesday, April 23, 1940. A warm spring evening in Natchez, Mississippi.

The Rhythm Night Club sat at 607 St. Catherine Street, in the heart of Natchez's Black commercial district. It wasn't much to look at — a one-story wooden building, roughly 120 feet long and 38 feet wide, with corrugated tin walls and a wooden roof. One front entrance. Windows that had been boarded up. No rear exit.

That night, the Walter Barnes Orchestra was playing. Barnes was a big draw — a Chicago-based bandleader whose territory stretched across the South. He'd been touring the "chitlin' circuit," playing one-nighters in Black dance halls from Texas to the Carolinas. The Rhythm Club was just another stop.

By most accounts, somewhere between 250 and 300 people packed into the building that night. Some estimates run higher — possibly 400. The fire marshal's legal capacity for a building this size would have been far less. But there was no fire marshal inspection. There was no code enforcement in Black establishments in 1940 Mississippi.

The fire started sometime around 11:30 PM. The most widely accepted account is that someone — possibly a patron trying to keep mosquitoes away — lit a match near the Spanish moss that had been draped from the ceiling and walls as decoration. Dry Spanish moss is essentially tinder. It caught instantly.

Within minutes, the entire ceiling was a sheet of flame.

The crowd surged toward the single front entrance. The door opened inward. The crush of bodies made it impossible to open. People stacked against the door, against the boarded windows, against the tin walls. The fire consumed the wooden structure with terrifying speed.

By the time the Natchez Fire Department arrived, the building was fully engulfed. Firefighters had to pull bodies away from the entrance to get water on the flames.

**209 people died.** Some sources say 210 or 212 — the count has never been perfectly settled because some victims were so badly burned that identification was impossible. More than 200 others were injured, many severely.

Walter Barnes died in the fire. So did most of his orchestra members. According to multiple accounts, Barnes continued playing as long as he could, trying to prevent panic. It didn't work. There was nowhere to go.

---

## The Building

The Rhythm Night Club was owned by Ed Frazier, a Black businessman. The building had originally been a church, then was converted to a dance hall. Key details about the structure:

- **One entrance/exit.** The front door was the only way in or out.
- **Boarded windows.** The windows had been nailed shut with boards — some accounts say to prevent people from sneaking in without paying, others say it was simply the state of the building.
- **No fire escapes.** No rear exit. No emergency exits of any kind.
- **Corrugated tin exterior walls.** People trapped inside couldn't break through.
- **Decorative Spanish moss on ceiling and walls.** Highly flammable. This was the accelerant.
- **No fire inspection on record.** Black commercial establishments in Natchez were not subject to the same code enforcement as white businesses. [NEEDS VERIFICATION — check city records]

The building was a death trap before anyone lit a match.

## The Walter Barnes Orchestra

Walter Barnes was born in Vicksburg, Mississippi, in 1905. He moved to Chicago as a young man and became one of the premier Black bandleaders of the 1930s. His Royal Creolians played the Savoy Ballroom and other major Chicago venues before Barnes shifted to a grueling touring schedule through the South.

By 1940, Barnes was playing 300+ dates a year, mostly in small-town dance halls across Mississippi, Alabama, Louisiana, and Georgia. The money was in volume — you played every night, drove to the next town, played again. The Rhythm Club in Natchez was a routine booking.

Barnes and at least nine of his band members died in the fire. [NEEDS VERIFICATION — exact number of band member casualties varies by source from 7 to 12]

The Chicago Defender, the nation's most influential Black newspaper, covered the fire extensively. Barnes was well-known in Chicago's Black community, and his death elevated the story beyond a local tragedy.

## The Aftermath

### The Immediate Response

The white community in Natchez and the city government offered condolences. The Red Cross set up emergency relief. But the response was shaped by the same segregation that contributed to the death toll:

- **Hospitals were segregated.** Injured survivors were treated at the "colored" facilities, which were grossly under-resourced for a mass casualty event.
- **Funeral homes were overwhelmed.** Black funeral homes in Natchez could not handle 209 bodies. Some victims were buried in mass graves.
- **The investigation was cursory.** No criminal charges were ever filed. No building owner, no city official, no fire inspector was held accountable.

### Insurance and Compensation

Most victims' families received nothing. The building owner carried minimal insurance, if any. There was no wrongful death litigation of the kind that would follow such an event today — Black families in 1940 Mississippi had effectively no access to the civil justice system for claims against the structural conditions created by segregation.

### Segregation's Role in the Death Toll

This is the part of the story that cannot be separated from the rest:

The Rhythm Nightclub Fire killed 209 people because of specific, identifiable structural conditions — boarded windows, one exit, no fire inspection, flammable decorations. Every one of those conditions existed because Black commercial establishments in Jim Crow Mississippi operated entirely outside the regulatory infrastructure that (theoretically) protected white establishments.

- No code enforcement meant no one checked for exits.
- No fire marshal inspections meant no one flagged the Spanish moss.
- Boarded windows reflected the economics of running a Black business in a system designed to keep you from accumulating capital — you couldn't afford to lose a single paying customer, so you boarded the windows.
- The building itself — a converted church with tin walls — reflected what was available to Black entrepreneurs. You didn't build new. You used what you had.

209 people didn't die because of one match. They died because of a system that guaranteed the match would be catastrophic.

### The National Impact

The Rhythm Nightclub Fire was, at the time, the second-deadliest single-building fire in American history (after the Iroquois Theatre fire in Chicago in 1903, which killed 602). It remains the deadliest nightclub fire in American history, surpassing the Cocoanut Grove fire in Boston (1942, 492 dead) only in the ratio of dead to capacity — the Rhythm Club had a near-total fatality rate among those trapped.

Despite this, the fire received relatively limited coverage in the white press. The *New York Times* ran a brief wire story. The *Chicago Defender* and other Black newspapers covered it extensively. But in the broader national memory, the Rhythm Nightclub Fire has been largely forgotten — a pattern consistent with how disasters in Black communities are treated in American historical memory.

## Modern Memorial and Museum Efforts

### The Rhythm Night Club Memorial Museum

A memorial museum has been developed in Natchez to preserve the history of the fire. The effort has been led by local descendants and community members, including Roscoe Barnes III, the grandnephew of Walter Barnes.
