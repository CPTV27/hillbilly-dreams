# Under-the-Hill: The Notorious Riverfront

**Natchez's original sin — and its best bar.**

---

## The Geography

Natchez sits on a bluff 200 feet above the Mississippi River. The bluff is the postcard — the mansions, the historic downtown, the respectability. But for most of the city's history, the real action was at the bottom.

Under-the-Hill is the narrow strip of land between the base of the bluff and the river. Silver Street descends steeply from Broadway (the main downtown street) down to the waterfront. At the bottom: the landing where every flatboat, keelboat, and steamboat on the Mississippi docked from the late 1700s through the Civil War.

The geography created a natural social division. The bluff was polite society. Under-the-Hill was everything polite society needed but wouldn't claim. The respectable families lived at the top. The money was made at the bottom.

## The History

### The Riverboat Landing Era (1790s-1860s)

Under-the-Hill was one of the most notorious districts on the entire Mississippi River — and in the early 1800s, the Mississippi River was the most important commercial corridor in North America.

**What was there:**

- **Slave trading.** Before and alongside the Forks of the Road market on the hill, enslaved people were bought and sold at the river landing. Traders arriving by boat unloaded human cargo at Under-the-Hill before marching them to the Forks for sale. The landing was the first point of entry into the Natchez slave economy.
- **River commerce.** Cotton, tobacco, sugar, lumber — the agricultural products of the entire lower Mississippi Valley moved through landings like Under-the-Hill. Flatboats loaded with goods from Kentucky, Ohio, Indiana, and Illinois docked here. The landing was a commercial hub of continental significance.
- **Gambling.** Under-the-Hill was legendary for its gambling operations. Professional gamblers worked the steamboat passengers and rivermen. Card games, dice, shell games — every form of betting that existed. The concentration of cash moving through the landing made it irresistible.
- **Prostitution.** Under-the-Hill had one of the most extensive commercial sex districts in the antebellum South. Brothels, boarding houses, and streetwalkers served the rivermen, travelers, and anyone else with money and time to kill between boats.
- **Saloons.** Dozens of taverns and saloons lined Silver Street and the waterfront. These were rough establishments — fights, murders, and robberies were common enough that Under-the-Hill's reputation spread across the country.
- **The Kaintucks.** The flatboatmen from Kentucky and the Ohio Valley — known as Kaintucks — were the primary clientele. They floated goods downriver, sold the goods and the boat for lumber, then spent their earnings Under-the-Hill before walking home via the Natchez Trace. (See: [the-trace.md](the-trace.md))

### The Reputation

Under-the-Hill was widely considered one of the most dangerous and debauched places in America in the early 1800s. Contemporary accounts describe:

- Murder as a semi-regular occurrence. Rivermen carrying large amounts of cash were targets.
- Organized crime before the term existed — networks of thieves, counterfeiters, and fences operated from the district.
- Racial mixing that was unthinkable on the bluff. Black and white, free and enslaved, rich and poor — Under-the-Hill was socially chaotic in ways that the bluff-top society found threatening.
- Disease. Yellow fever, cholera, and other waterborne illnesses hit the low-lying riverfront hard. Periodic epidemics devastated the population.

The bluff-top citizens periodically cracked down on Under-the-Hill — the most famous incident being the 1835 expulsion of professional gamblers, during which a mob lynched several men accused of gambling and associated crimes. But the district always rebuilt. The money flowing through the landing was too good.

### Physical Destruction

Under-the-Hill didn't just decline — much of it literally fell into the river. The Mississippi River's channel shifted over the decades, and erosion ate away at the riverbank. Buildings that once sat on solid ground collapsed into the water. By the late 1800s, a significant portion of the original Under-the-Hill district had been lost to the river.

Floods also took their toll. The periodic Mississippi floods inundated the low-lying district, destroying structures and discouraging rebuilding.

Between erosion, floods, fire, and neglect, Under-the-Hill shrank from a sprawling vice district of dozens of buildings to a handful of survivors clinging to what remained of the bank.

## The Silver Street Saloons

Silver Street was the main drag of Under-the-Hill. Named for the silver that (allegedly) flowed through it in the form of river commerce payments, Silver Street connected the bluff to the river landing.

At its peak, Silver Street hosted:

- Multiple saloons and taverns
- Gambling houses
- Brothels
- Boarding houses for rivermen
- Trading posts
- Warehouses for goods in transit

The street's character changed with the river traffic. During high-water season, when steamboats ran frequently, Silver Street was packed. During low water, it was quieter — but never quiet. There was always someone with money to spend and someone willing to take it.

## Modern Under-the-Hill

Today, Under-the-Hill is a fraction of its former self. The surviving structures include:

### The Under-the-Hill Saloon
The last remaining saloon on Silver Street. It operates as a bar and live music venue, with a heavy tourist and local crowd. The building dates to the 1800s (exact construction date varies by source). It's the most recognizable establishment Under-the-Hill and a key stop for any visitor.

The Saloon books live music — blues, country, rock, whatever works. On any given night, you can sit at the bar, look out at the river, and drink in a room where people have been drinking for close to two centuries. That's not a marketing line. That's just true.

The difference: the antebellum gamblers were independent operators who lived in the community. The casino is a corporate extraction machine that sends the profits to a headquarters somewhere else. Same activity. Different plumbing.

### The Bluff and the Bridge

The view from Under-the-Hill looking up at the bluff is one of the most dramatic in Natchez. The bluff towers above, the old brick buildings of Silver Street sit at the base, and the Mississippi-Louisiana bridge spans the river behind you.

The bridge (the Natchez-Vidalia Bridge, connecting to Vidalia, Louisiana) changed Under-the-Hill's function. Before the bridge, you crossed the river by ferry from the landing. The bridge moved the crossing point and reduced Under-the-Hill's role as a transportation hub.

### What Under-the-Hill Means for Big Muddy

Under-the-Hill is where the history is most visible and most visceral. It's the place where you can stand in a building from the 1800s, drink a beer, and feel the weight of what happened in that space — the commerce, the slavery, the gambling, the violence, the music, the survival.

For Big Muddy's purposes:
- **The Saloon** is a venue partner. Live music at the Saloon feeds the same ecosystem as the Blues Room.
- **The physical space** is a tourism asset. Walking down Silver Street is an experience you can't replicate.
- **The history** is content. Every wall, every brick, every view has a story.
- **The market opportunity** (see the Under-the-Hill Maker & Food Market concept in [natchez.md](../natchez.md)) uses the foot traffic that already exists.
