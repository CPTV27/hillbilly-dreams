A Report from the Ground

Persimmon Hollow

On a patch of land outside Natchez, a small group of builders, musicians, and growers is doing something quietly unusual — and getting away with it.

By Harper Lane · Photographs for Big Muddy · 14 min read

The driveway is dirt for a quarter mile before it opens into a clearing, and the first thing you see is not the barn. It's the pond. Someone — and a backhoe, and a week — has shaped the low end of the property into a small body of water with a willow at the north edge and a flat spot at the south for sitting. Beyond that, a 2,400-square-foot barn is framed out and half-clad in corrugated metal the color of weathered tin. Beyond the barn, stepping back toward the tree line in a loose arc, are four small structures that would read as cabins if they weren't quite so carefully made.

This is Persimmon Hollow. It is not a commune. It is not a retreat. It is not, its founders are careful to say, an experiment in anything — which is how you can tell it's working.

"It's a working compound," says one of the organizers, looking out across the pond from the porch of the central barn. "Some people live here. Some people come for a week. Some people come for a season. The people who live here work. The people who visit work too, usually, because that's why they come."

What they work on depends on who they are. There's a recording studio in one corner of the barn, treated and quiet, with an Avalon preamp on the rack and a Hammond B3 in the live room. There's a woodshop in another corner of the barn, where a resident builder is framing the fifth tiny house of a run that will ultimately be twelve. There's a garden — two acres of it — run by a woman who spent fifteen years cooking in other people's kitchens and now cooks mostly in her own. There's a dog training operation in an outbuilding toward the southwest corner of the property, three kennel runs and a thousand square feet of indoor working space, run by a man whose day job for twenty years was recording drums.

The Idea

Persimmon Hollow started with a problem. Artists and craftspeople working on the Mississippi corridor — musicians tied to the Blues Room, writers tied to Big Muddy Magazine, builders and growers with skills but no equity — kept running into the same wall. Natchez is affordable by Hudson Valley standards, but the housing stock is aging and the rental market rewards tourists over locals. A thirty-two-year-old songwriter with a steady touring calendar can't find a place to live that doesn't eat half her income. A drummer with a mother who needs ADA-accessible housing can't find a unit that works for her at any price. A garden manager with fifteen years of restaurant experience can't afford the kind of kitchen she wants to work in.

"We kept having the same conversation," one organizer says. "What if we just built it? Not for investors. For the people who are actually doing the work on the corridor."

The math they landed on is unusual. The compound is organized as a hybrid cooperative. The first ten residents are founders. They put in capital — either cash, or sweat equity for people who are building the thing themselves — and they get a partial stake and voting rights. After ten, new residents are renters. No stake, no vote, but also no capital lockup. The rent is $900 a month, which is under market, which is the point.

The tiny houses themselves are built on site. Two hundred and fifty square feet, off-grid capable with solar and battery, shippable on a trailer if need be. The build cost is aggressive — a target of twenty-five to thirty-one thousand dollars per unit, which is well under what Boxabl or any of the other prefab operators are doing at scale. How they get there is by building them themselves, mostly with regional timber from El Dorado and hardware from the Natchez Building Supply, with a small paid labor budget and a lot of bartered trade.

It's a working compound. The people who live here work. The people who visit work too, usually, because that's why they come.

The Barn

The central structure is a 2,400-square-foot barndominium, which is a word that either needs explaining or doesn't, depending on whether you've spent time in the rural South recently. It is exactly what it sounds like — a barn that you also live in. Kit-built from a Mississippi supplier, shell up in six days, slab poured by a local contractor, interior finished by the owners themselves over the course of a couple of months.

The barn houses everything the compound needs that isn't a bedroom. There's a 600-square-foot apartment at one end, which is where the founder who's doing the day-to-day organizing lives. A workshop runs the length of the middle, with tools and a lumber rack and a dust collection system. There's a 400-square-foot recording studio at the back, treated with blankets and rockwool and the kind of acoustic dampening that doesn't look pretty but works beautifully. And there's a bay at the far end that's big enough to hold a Prevost coach — which matters, because Big Muddy Touring's tour bus lives here when it isn't out on the corridor.

The building also houses the compound's internet, which comes in via Starlink and fans out across the property via a mesh network that the organizer cobbled together out of parts. There's a rack in the corner with three machines quietly humming. One runs the compound's internal services. One runs the radio station's broadcasting software. One is a photo archive with more than 50,000 images in it, mostly of the corridor itself — the rivers, the venues, the people, the light.

The Garden

"When I was cooking in restaurants," the garden manager says, "I could never source what I actually wanted. I'd order heirloom tomatoes and get whatever the distributor had. I'd put okra on the menu and spend an hour on the phone trying to find some that wasn't shipped in from three states away."

Now she orders from herself. The garden produces the compound's vegetables for most of the year, sells surplus at a farmers market in Natchez on Saturdays, and runs a small CSA that delivers to a few restaurants in town. She keeps chickens, which supply the eggs. She keeps bees, which supply the honey. She doesn't keep goats — she's thought about it — but she has let a neighbor graze sheep on the back pasture in exchange for mutton in the freezer.

She runs the hospitality side of the Inn in her other role — Big Muddy Inn has a kitchen garden in Natchez, and she oversees both. On the weekends when there's a show at the Blues Room, she's the one who sets up the hospitality rider for the touring act. On the mornings when there are guests at the Inn, she's the one making sure the coffee is where it's supposed to be by seven. On the afternoons when she's at the compound, she's usually in the garden, in a wide-brim hat, watching the clouds.

The Residents (Founder Circle)

Who lives at Persimmon Hollow

The Organizer — lives in the barn apartment, runs the compound day to day, photographs everything, builds the platform software that holds the whole Big Muddy family together.

The Grower — runs the garden and the Inn hospitality side. Fifteen years in restaurant kitchens before this. Lives in a farmhouse on the north end of the property.

The Engineer — runs the studio, trains dogs on the side. Twenty years as a session drummer in Muscle Shoals before relocating. Lives in a tiny house at the east edge; his mother, who has mobility needs, lives in an ADA-accessible unit built to her spec right next door.

The Builder — frames the tiny houses, manages the shop, handles most of the skilled trades on the property. Lives in a tiny house of his own making.

Six more seats — to be filled with musicians, writers, and craftspeople over the next eighteen months.

The Studio

The recording studio is the part of the compound that surprises people most. It's small — 400 square feet — but it's built to a professional standard. The live room is floated, the control room is separate, the monitoring is Focal, the preamps are a mix of vintage and new. The resident engineer spent two decades recording drums in other people's studios and decided, finally, to build one of his own in a place that wasn't Muscle Shoals and wasn't Nashville and wasn't Memphis.

"I wanted to be on the corridor," he says. "The river does something to the music. Everyone who records here notices it, and nobody can explain it, and that's fine."

The studio operates on a day-rate basis — five hundred dollars, which is below the regional competitive rate, which is the point. Seventy percent of that goes to him. Thirty percent goes to the cooperative. The sessions are booked primarily through Big Muddy Records, which uses the studio for label work, but outside clients are welcome and increasingly common. A songwriter from the Delta tracked her EP here last month. An Austin blues band did a two-day run in February.

The same engineer also runs a dog training operation — a separate business, housed in its own outbuilding, twenty percent to the cooperative, eighty percent to him. The two things don't seem related, and the fact that they're run by the same person doesn't seem to bother anyone.

The river does something to the music. Everyone who records here notices it, and nobody can explain it, and that's fine.

The Economics

The question people ask first is whether it pencils. It does, but slowly.

The first phase of the compound — land, barn, site development, the model tiny house — comes in around $627,000 fully loaded. That's funded by a mix of partner equity, founder buy-ins, a small rural construction loan, and an expected settlement from an unrelated legal matter. Phase one is self-contained; phase two is the eight tiny houses that fill out the founder circle plus the additional infrastructure those units require.

Each tiny house, at a build cost of roughly $31,000 and a monthly rent of $900 at 90% occupancy, yields about 31% annually on build cost. Simple payback sits around 3.2 years per unit. That's aggressive as real estate goes, but it's the math that makes the compound work without outside investors. Founder buy-ins cover early operating costs. Rental income covers the rest once the units are built out.

The compound also generates revenue from the studio, from the dog training operation, from occasional tiny house sales to outside buyers (friends and neighbors first, at around $38,000 per unit installed within fifty miles), and from a small garden CSA. None of these are the main business. The main business is people making things, in a place that supports them making things, and the rest of it is plumbing.

What's Next

The organizers are careful about pace. "We don't want to build everything in year one and then spend five years running a property management company," one says. "The point is the work. The compound exists so the work can happen."

Year one is the barn, the model tiny house, the garden, the dog operation, and the first four residents. Year two is three or four additional tiny houses and the rest of the founder circle. Year three brings the cap to ten founders, fills out the rental pool with another handful of units, and starts selling extra builds to outside buyers on the corridor who want a small house and don't want to wait for Boxabl to actually ship one.

By year five, the compound is a closed system. Ten founders. Eight to ten rental units. A working studio, a working garden, a working dog operation, a working shop. A place where the tour bus can park between runs. A place where a magazine writer can stay for a week to work on a piece. A place where a songwriter can cut an EP with a drummer whose day job involves golden retrievers.

That's the plan, anyway. It's not a commune. It's not a retreat. It's not an experiment in anything. It's a compound, and the people who live there work, and that's really all it is.

Somewhere out past the pond, the willow is coming into leaf.

At a glance

Persimmon Hollow

Location: Twelve acres outside Natchez, Mississippi, 25 minutes from downtown

Structure: Hybrid cooperative — ten founders, then rental

Housing: Central barndominium (2,400 sqft) + tiny houses (250 sqft each) built on site

Rent: $900 per month per tiny unit

Founder buy-in: $35,000 cash, or sweat equity for builders and key residents

Services on site: Recording studio, dog training, garden, woodshop, tour bus bay

Parent entity: Big Muddy Land Co. LLC, under MBT
