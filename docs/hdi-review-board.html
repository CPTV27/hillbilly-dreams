What Big Muddy Is.

Big Muddy is a music and media company in Natchez, Mississippi. We run an inn, book shows across the Mississippi corridor, publish a magazine, produce a video podcast, release records, and sell publicity packages to local businesses.

Three partners own the company equally: Chase Pierson, Tracy Alderson-Allen, and Amy Alderson-Allen. Hillbilly Dreams Inc is the operating company. The center of gravity is a six-suite inn in downtown Natchez with a bar on the ground floor and a small stage in the back.

From there it spreads out. A Sprinter van for hauling bands up and down the corridor. A magazine with a print run and a website, publishing profiles, show reviews, and music-industry stories from the region — venues old and new, bands touring through, interviews with artists, studios, and the historical figures that built the Delta sound. A video podcast produced at the Inn, published across Spotify, SoundCloud, YouTube, and TikTok. A small record label that releases the artists who pass through. And a directory of every music venue, restaurant, bar, and hotel between Natchez and Memphis, with publicity packages sold on top of it.

A small media company can do for a Main Street what the big ones never bothered to. The Operating Thesis

The parts feed each other. A band plays the bar on Friday. The magazine writes about the show on Saturday. The podcast features the band in the next episode — published to Spotify, SoundCloud, YouTube, and TikTok. The venue listing shows a live-music badge. A tourist books a room at the Inn. The band sells records at the merch table. Weddings come in on the weekends. None of these is a big business on its own. Together they are.

Hospitality

The Inn, the Bar, the Weddings.

Six suites. A stage downstairs. Twenty weddings a year at full run rate.

The Big Muddy Inn is six suites above a bar in downtown Natchez. Each suite sleeps two to four. Occupancy sits at 18% today. The plan lifts it to 28% by year-end, which at a $180 average nightly rate is roughly $120,000 in room revenue.

The Bar is the ground-floor room. Twelve show nights a month, local pours, a small kitchen, and the stage at the back. Show-night margins on beer and spirits carry the room. The bar is also where out-of-town musicians end up after their sets, which is where magazine interviews happen.

Weddings are the highest-margin line in the building. Each package is $14,100 and books the inn, the bar, the stage, and a full-day photography shoot. Eight in Year One, sixteen in Year Two, twenty in Year Three. Weddings turn a small inn into a real company.

Entertainment

Touring, Magazine, Radio, Records.

The production arm. The Sprinter van is on the road this week. The magazine publishes weekly. The video podcast releases new episodes across Spotify, SoundCloud, YouTube, and TikTok. The label books studio time in Memphis.

Big Muddy Touring books shows up and down the Mississippi corridor — Natchez to Vicksburg, Clarksdale, Memphis, and the side routes through Greenville and Helena. We handle the booking work. The Sprinter van hauls artists and gear. Corridor routing pays for itself when the Inn fills on the back end of a tour.

Big Muddy Magazine publishes profiles, show reviews, corridor travel features, and music-industry stories from the region — coverage of the venues that built the Delta sound, bands old and new, interviews with artists, studios, and the historical figures behind the music. AI drafts, human editors finish. Print runs on a quarterly cadence; the website runs weekly. Sponsor placements inside the magazine are a real revenue line.

Big Muddy Radio is a video podcast produced at the Inn. Every episode features the musicians we work with — the artists on the corridor tour, the bands playing the bar, the label roster. New episodes publish to Spotify, SoundCloud, YouTube, and TikTok. When a band signs with us, when a record comes out, when a tour rolls through — they end up on the podcast, in front of an audience that spans four platforms. The show is how every artist we work with gets showcased. A live linear audio and video format launches in Q4 once the podcast audience is at scale.

Big Muddy Records is a small label. Three releases in Year One, all 50/50 NET splits with the artist. The label pays for recording at a Memphis studio and funds the physical release. The artist gets a full promotional push through the magazine, the podcast across Spotify/SoundCloud/YouTube/TikTok, and the touring circuit.

Local Marketing

The Deep South Directory.

A directory of venues, bars, restaurants, hotels, and musicians across the Mississippi corridor. Free listings for everyone. Publicity packages sold on top. That's the revenue line.

The Directory is at deepsouthdirectory.com. Free listings in five categories pull the audience in — a venue owner lists her room, a musician lists his profile, a restaurant lists its Friday-night jazz. The directory becomes the search layer for the corridor.

On top of the free listings, we sell publicity packages. A musician wanting a feature in the magazine pays $500 to $3,000 for a spotlight. A venue wanting consistent promotion pays $200 a month for ongoing placement. A hotel running a weekend special pays $250 to $300 a month for dedicated media. A brand sponsoring a tour pays $500 to $1,200 for a press run.

These packages sell into media the company owns — the magazine, the podcast and its Spotify/SoundCloud/YouTube/TikTok channels, the directory itself, the Big Muddy Touring tour pages. The audience is real and growing. The placements are traceable. The money is straightforward.

The Catalog

Venues, Musicians, Industry Intelligence.

Our working directory of the music and entertainment industry on the Mississippi corridor. Venues, musicians, press, studios, historical context — the intelligence Big Muddy runs on.

The Catalog is an internal operating resource. When a booker needs a Tuesday-night act, the Catalog is where the team looks. When a magazine writer needs a band to profile, it's where they go. When Big Muddy Touring plans a corridor run, the venue data is in here. It's a CRM for the music industry on the corridor, maintained by the people who work the corridor every day.

Musicians add themselves through a five-minute QR card at a show — scan, fill out five fields, upload a photo and a track. Those profiles feed our booking, podcast, and magazine work. For the musician, it's a free discovery page. For us, it's an entry in our directory. Over time the Catalog may become a place where musicians network with each other; that's a side benefit, not a separate product we're building.

The Catalog also holds industry intelligence: venue capacities and contacts, press relationships, studio directories, historical references to the people and places that shaped the Delta sound. It grows as we work the corridor.
