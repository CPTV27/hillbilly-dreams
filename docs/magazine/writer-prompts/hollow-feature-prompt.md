## Prompt For Writer: Fake NYT-Style Feature

You are writing a feature article in the style and tone of the New York Times "Style" or "Arts & Leisure" section. It should read like a real reported piece, not marketing copy, and should be plausible enough that a working musician or arts-adjacent professional doesn't immediately spot it as fake.

Write a 1,500–2,000 word article about a small, semi-secret rural artist community and studio project that sounds like a cross between:

- A tiny-house homestead
- A working recording studio and rehearsal compound
- A modern agrarian arts colony

Follow these instructions exactly:

### 1. Basic Setup

- Dateline it like a NYT piece: CITY, State — with month/day/year.
- Place it somewhere in the rural South, within 30–45 minutes of a known small city (e.g., Natchez, Asheville, Chattanooga, etc.).
- Use a fictional name for the community (e.g., "Persimmon Hollow") and treat it like a real place that prefers to remain low-key and off the map.
- Make it clear the community is:
  - 10–25 acres of land
  - 7–15 people living there full-time
  - A mix of tiny houses, one central "barndominium" or shared building, gardens, and a small pond or similar landscape feature.

### 2. Core Characters

Invent 3–4 key characters with first and last names:

- A photographer / media person in their 40s who is one of the founders.
- A gardener / hospitality lead (late 20s to 40s) who runs the food side and guest experience.
- A musician/engineer (late 30s to 40s) who is the "resident studio brain," also plays multiple instruments, and looks after gear, vehicles, and dogs.
- Optionally, a visiting or part-time resident (like a drummer or songwriter) who is clearly a stand-in for a specific type of working musician.

Write them like real people: small details, quotes, and quirks, but no melodrama.

### 3. The Place And Infrastructure

Describe the physical setup with concrete but believable detail:

- Number and size of tiny houses (all under 400–500 square feet)
- The central building that houses:
  - A woodshop
  - A shared kitchen and common room
  - A recording studio / control room
- Power: primarily solar, with a small generator that is rarely used.
- Internet: Starlink or similar satellite, shared local network.
- Cell service: very poor or nonexistent on the property.
- Food: a working vegetable garden, chickens, possibly goats. Residents share food.

Make the description tactile: what it smells like, what it sounds like, how quiet it gets at night, what the drive in feels like.

### 4. Economics And Ownership

This is the "outsider economics" section. It should sound idealistic but plausible.

Explain:

- The land was purchased cheaply for the region (give an approximate but believable number).
- The founding members pooled savings or sold city real estate / gear to buy it outright or with a small loan.
- They have a "founding circle" or similar model:
  - Up to about 10 people who contribute money, skills, or labor in exchange for a share of land ownership and a permanent right to live there.
  - After that, additional residents are renters with access to shared facilities but no equity.

Give believable numbers:

- Rent for tiny houses in the $700–$1,200 range, including utilities and access to garden, shop, and studio.
- Cost of building each tiny house (materials only) around the mid-teens to low twenties in thousands of dollars.
- Studio income: the community takes a percentage (e.g., 30%) on commercial sessions; the rest goes to the engineer(s).

Do NOT explain legal structure in detail. Use plain language (e.g., "a co-op-like agreement" or "a private partnership that functions like a land trust") and leave specifics fuzzy like a normal newspaper article would.

### 5. Why It Exists

Make the emotional "why" very clear:

- They're trying to opt out of the constant hustle of city art and music economies (e.g., Nashville, New Orleans, Brooklyn, etc.).
- They want to create a stable base for working musicians, craftspeople, and artists who are tired of gig-to-gig survival.
- They see land, shared infrastructure, and cooperation as a way to gain freedom and creative time.

Use quotes from residents about:

- Burnout from touring and bar gigs
- Rising rent and cost of living in cities
- Wanting to "stop performing" economically and actually live.

### 6. Media And Storytelling

Include a section that hints at:

- The community documenting their own story: building the place, recording, gardening, and day-to-day life.
- Some kind of media arm or "small studio" that records music and produces content, without making it sound like influencer culture.
- Their tension between wanting privacy and wanting to share the blueprint with other artists.

Make it clear they keep the exact location vague, don't push hard on social media, and rely on friends-of-friends and word of mouth for visitors.

### 7. Tone And Style

Write in polished, neutral, NYT-like prose:

- Third-person reporting voice.
- Mix of scene-setting, quotes, and factual paragraphs.
- A few short, punchy pull-quote lines that could be set off typographically (you can mark them with > like block quotes).
- No slang, no clickbait, no direct sales language.

The article should:

- Open with a vivid scene (arrival, a meal, a rehearsal, or a day in the workshop).
- Then zoom out into backstory, economics, and philosophy.
- Close with something small but symbolic (e.g., a late-night rehearsal, planting something, or talking about what they still need — like "a good drummer").

### 8. Constraints And Red Lines

- Do NOT reference any real people by name; all names should be invented but plausible.
- Do NOT include a URL for the community or social media handles.
- Do NOT make any claims that would obviously require public records (no precise parcel IDs, no detailed corporate structures).
- Everything should be "soft" enough that, if someone wanted to visit, they'd still have to be invited.

### 9. Output Format

- Include a headline in NYT style.
- Include a short italic dek/subhead under the headline.
- Begin with: "By [WRITER NAME]" and a plausible dateline like "April 15, 2026".
- Use section breaks (blank lines) between natural chunks of the story.
- No bullet points in the final article; all prose.

Now, using all of these instructions, write the full article.
