**Caption:**
Why we built a media company for small-town businesses.

I moved to Natchez, Mississippi -- population 16,000, median household income $35,000 -- and started a media company. Not a marketing agency. Not a consulting firm. A media company. Magazine, record label, radio station, photo studio, touring guide, business directory, and an inn. All serving the same 200-mile corridor between Memphis and New Orleans.

The conventional wisdom says this is insane. The demographics don't support it. The market is too small. The addressable audience is too niche. Every MBA case study says you go where the people are, and the people are in Nashville and Austin and the other cities that already have too many media companies fighting for the same audience.

Here's what the conventional wisdom misses: small towns don't need less media infrastructure. They need more. They need it worse than cities do. A restaurant in Natchez can't buy a billboard on Broadway. A musician in Clarksdale can't get a feature in Rolling Stone. A flower shop on Main Street can't afford a digital marketing agency.

But they can be part of a network. A network where the Magazine features the restaurant, the Radio plays the musician, the Directory lists the flower shop, the Touring guide sends the travelers, and the whole system runs on shared infrastructure that no single small business could build alone.

That's the model. Every client gets treated like a brand AND a media company. Same infrastructure, different voice per brand. The recording artist who plays our venue on Friday gets a Magazine feature by Tuesday, a Radio session by the following week, and a Directory listing that connects them to every other business in the network.

We're not competing with Nashville media companies. We're building the media infrastructure that the 16,000-person towns never had. And it turns out there are a lot of 16,000-person towns between Memphis and New Orleans, and they all have restaurants and musicians and flower shops that deserve better than a Yelp page and a prayer.

The coffee table book comes out this year. The field manual (Outsider Economics) is in production. The Directory just launched with Stripe Connect. The recording pipeline is live across three venues.

None of this pencils out on a spreadsheet the way a VC would want it to. All of it works in the room where it happens.
