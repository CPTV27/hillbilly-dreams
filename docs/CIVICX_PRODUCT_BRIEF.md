## CRITICAL FLAG: ARPA Obligation Deadline Already Passed

**The ARPA obligation deadline was December 31, 2024.** All funds had to be committed through binding contracts by that date. New obligations cannot be created.

This means we **cannot** pitch: "You have unspent money — contract with us." That window closed 15 months ago.

**What we CAN do:**
1. **Subcontract under existing obligations** — If a municipality obligated ARPA funds for "technology modernization" or "digital services" but hasn't selected a vendor yet (or their vendor fell through), we can fulfill that existing obligation
2. **Substitute vendor** — Treasury allows recipients to substitute personnel/vendors on already-obligated projects
3. **Revenue replacement pathway** — Cities that elected the standard allowance ($10M) can spend on government services broadly, and if they obligated under that category, digital infrastructure qualifies
4. **Non-ARPA funding** — CDBG, USDA Rural Development, and municipal IT budgets are evergreen

**The real pitch is:** "You already committed federal money to technology. We can deploy before your December 2026 expenditure deadline. Your current vendor can't."

### Mississippi HB 1571 Makes This Urgent
Signed March 24, 2026 — the state will redirect unspent local ARPA funds by **September 30–October 15, 2026**. Cities that haven't spent obligated funds lose them to MDOT and the state insurance fund. This is a 6-month window, not 9.

## 2. ARPA Qualification Pathways

### Path A: Revenue Replacement (Standard Allowance) — BEST FIT
- Cities that elected the standard allowance (up to $10M) can spend on **any government service**
- Treasury explicitly includes: "improving access to and user-experience of government IT systems, including upgrades to hardware and software as well as improvements to public-facing websites"
- Most NEU allocations are well under $10M — this is the simplest path
- **Simplified reporting** — no need to demonstrate revenue loss

### Path B: Negative Economic Impacts (Tourism)
- Tourism, travel, and hospitality are **presumptively impacted industries**
- Technology supporting tourism recovery qualifies
- Small business technical assistance and digital tools qualify
- This covers the tourism/directory component of CivicX

### Path C: Broadband/Digital Inclusion
- Digital literacy training, device provision, and programs promoting internet access are eligible
- The kiosk component (public access terminal) fits here
- "Lack of affordable broadband" qualifies areas for investment

### Path D: Government Services (Direct)
- Capital expenditures for technology to adapt government operations
- Video conferencing, case management, data sharing systems
- Public-facing website improvements

### NEU-Specific Rules (Cities <50K)
- Funds distributed through the state, not directly from Treasury
- **75% budget cap** — total award capped at 75% of annual operating + capital budget
- Same eligible uses as larger cities
- Simplified annual reporting (Tier 5)

## 8. The $826M Number — Correction Needed

The $826M figure in our existing docs appears to be the **total local government ARPA allocation** (~$900M direct to MS cities/counties), not the unspent balance. Verified data shows:
- $305M+ remains unspent from water/sewer grants alone (as of Feb 2026)
- 44% of city/county MDEQ grants are unspent
- 31% of rural water association grants are unspent

**Recommended language:** "Hundreds of millions in Mississippi ARPA funds remain unspent with a December 2026 deadline" rather than citing $826M specifically. Pull exact figures from Treasury before any external pitch.
