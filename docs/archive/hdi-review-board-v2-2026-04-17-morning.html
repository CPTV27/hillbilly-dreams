Plain English

What HDI Is

We run an inn, book shows, publish a magazine, broadcast a radio station, release records, and sell publicity packages to local businesses. We are three partners in Natchez, Mississippi. Everything else in this document is detail.

Hillbilly Dreams Inc — HDI — is the operating company. The center of gravity is a six-suite inn in downtown Natchez with a bar on the ground floor and a small stage in the back. From there it spreads out: a Sprinter van for hauling bands up and down the corridor, a magazine with a print run and a website, an internet radio station that broadcasts out of the building, a small record label that releases the artists who pass through, and a directory of every music venue, restaurant, bar, and hotel between Natchez and Memphis.

The point is that the parts feed each other. A band plays the bar on Friday. The magazine writes about the show on Saturday. The radio station puts a track in rotation Monday. The directory listing for the venue gets a "live music this week" badge. A tourist in Memphis sees the listing, books a room at the inn, drives down to catch the band's return date. The band sells records at the merch table. We host the wedding of two people who came down for the show. None of these things is a big business on its own. Together they are.

"Three verticals, one company. Hospitality is the anchor. Entertainment is the engine. Local Marketing is the leverage." The Operating Thesis

This document is for two audiences at once. The three of us — Chase, Tracy, Amy — need to keep working from the same map, especially as the calendar fills up and the stakes go up. Outsiders — advisors, friends in the industry, future partners, anyone Chase trusts enough to show this to — need a way to understand what we are actually doing without sitting through ten years of explanation. The hope is that one document can do both.

What follows is the structure of the company, the pieces of revenue that make it up, the case study that shows how it cashes out, the things we are doing in the next thirty days, and the things we are not doing — which is at least as important as the things we are.

The Biscuits & Blues Example

Friday night, our partner Regina Charboneau's restaurant is hosting a Biscuits & Blues showcase. A blues trio plays the upstairs room. Saturday morning, the magazine publishes a shoulder-season piece on the new Biscuits & Blues lineup. By Sunday, the radio station has the trio's single in light rotation.

Monday, the directory listing for Biscuits & Blues gets a "Live Music This Week" placement. A tourist in Memphis is searching for "blues weekend Mississippi." She lands on the directory, sees the live music tag, and clicks through to the inn page. She books two nights. The trio is back in three weeks for a return date — booked because last weekend went well. The wedding inquiry that comes in next Tuesday is from someone who heard the broadcast.

The 2:1 Multiplier

A $1,000 show generates roughly $500 downstream.

Inn bookings. Bar sales. Magazine pageviews. Radio listener growth. Record promotion. Directory traffic. Wedding inquiries. None of these line items belongs to the show on its own — but none of them happens without the show.

2 : 1
Ecosystem multiplier

This is the part of the business that takes the longest to explain to outsiders, and it's the part that matters most. Every other vertical is independently honest — the inn would still be a small inn if we did nothing else, the magazine would still be a small magazine, the directory would still be a usable directory. But the reason any of it scales is that the parts compound. Shows light up the rest. The rest brings shows back.

The Moat

We own the magazine, the radio station, the social channels, the touring circuit, and the inn that hosts the band the night of the show. No one else in our market — or any market our size — can offer the same bundle from one P&L.

That is the unfair advantage. Townsquare can't book the band. Birdeye can't write the article. The PR firm can't promise the radio rotation because they don't own the station. We can promise all of it because we built all of it.

The One-Liner

"We are not a software company. We are a media company that uses software to deliver coverage."

Said another way: most of our competitors are vendors. We are participants. Every artist we promote, we can put on stage. Every venue we list, we can fill. Every business we feature, we can send tourists to. The directory is not the product. The whole machine is.

There are also things we are not trying to be. We are not a touring agent at the William Morris scale. We are not Bandcamp. We are not Spotify. We are not a venture-backed civic tech platform. We are a profitable small media business with a regional moat, run by three partners who live where the work happens. The scale is the point.

Vertical Two · Entertainment
The Engine Room.
Chapter Four

Touring, Magazine, Radio, Records.

Four lines, one machine. Owned by Chase, with Sean Davis contributing on bookings and venue relationships across the corridor. Tracy edits the magazine; Amy hosts on the radio; the studio releases what comes through.

Entertainment is the half of the company that produces the content the rest of the business runs on. A show is a show — but a show is also a magazine feature, a radio segment, a record release window, a directory campaign, and a piece of social proof for the next venue we want to book. Big Muddy Touring

The Sprinter van runs the Mississippi corridor — Natchez, Vicksburg, Clarksdale, Memphis, with side trips to Birmingham, Little Rock, New Orleans. Tracy edits. AI drafts the long features and we human-edit them down. Print run for the showcase issues, web for everything else. The magazine doesn't try to be a national music publication — it's the editorial calendar of our own scene, which makes it the most-read thing in the room.

Big Muddy Radio

Amy's Microphone

24/7 internet stream broadcast out of the inn building, SSL secured, cleanly logo'd. Amy hosts a regular show. Bands that play the bar feed the rotation. The radio is how a tourist three states away discovers the scene before they ever book a room.

Big Muddy Records

50/50 NET

Small label. We release the artists who pass through. Costs come off the top — studio time, mastering, art, distro — then we split the rest 50/50 with the artist. Honest math, the only kind that holds up over a long catalog.

Vertical Three · Local Marketing
The Publicity Engine.
Chapter Five

Deep South Directory + Publicity Packages.

Reframed from a generic business directory to a focused music-industry database for the corridor. Five public categories. One internal Rolodex. Revenue is publicity packages — paid coverage in our own media — not subscriptions.

The Deep South Directory used to be pitched as a Yelp-for-Mississippi: pay $25 or $50 a month for a listing, get more visibility. We killed that model. The honest version is simpler. Listings are free. Revenue comes from publicity packages — campaigns of guaranteed coverage in our magazine, our radio station, our social channels, our touring circuit, our inn. We are competing with PR firms, not with Yelp.

Inside the Directory
The Musician Catalog.
Chapter Six

The MySpace Idea.

Thirty real musician profiles by July. A hundred by year's end. Free for every musician forever — no Pro tier, no subscription, no upsell. The point is curation.

The musician catalog is the part of the directory that everybody underestimates. It's not a streaming service. It's not a fan-feed app. It's not Bandcamp. It's a curated, free-forever profile + submission + discovery tool that lives inside the Deep South Directory and feeds every other vertical of the company. Bands fill out a five-minute page; we get a recommendation engine, a radio submission queue, a corridor booking pipeline, and a magazine source list. Bands get a clean public page they can hand to anyone.

The QR Onboarding Flow

Chase meets a band at a show. He hands them a card with a QR code. They scan it on the spot. Five minutes later, their page is live.

Why Top-Three Everything

Spotify gives you everything. Bandcamp gives you everything. Instagram gives you a feed. The musician catalog gives you the band's three best photos, three influences, three songs they'd play if they had to pick. Curation is the differentiator. The constraint forces a decision; the decision is the discovery signal.

What It's Not

  • Not Spotify — no streaming, no algorithmic playlists
  • Not Bandcamp — no commerce on day one
  • Not Instagram — no feed, no followers, no likes
  • It is a profile + a submission + a discovery tool

Reality Check

What Could Break.

Honest naming of the things that could derail the plan. Not catastrophizing — just listing. Each risk has a mitigation we are actually doing, not one we wish we were.

Highest risk

Chase is the bottleneck

The risk: Chase is the photographer, the developer, the publicity-package salesperson, the brand voice, the technical operator of the platform, and one of three equity partners. Any single week he is unavailable, three things stall.

Mitigation in flight: Sean takes bookings. Tracy takes the magazine. Amy takes the radio. Studio C takes production. Chase systematically ships the work that hands off, then steps back. The 30-day sprint already starts the handoffs.

Highest risk

Wedding pipeline doesn't materialize

The risk: Weddings are projected at $113K Y1 (8 weddings at ~$14K). Two are confirmed; six are inferred. If the pipeline only fills four, Y1 hospitality is short ~$60K.

Mitigation in flight: Landing page and referral kit are week-4 sprint deliverables. Pricing is fixed and competitive for the venue type. Tracy already has two confirmed inquiries from existing relationships before any marketing has shipped.

Medium risk

Bookings ramp slower than planned

The risk: Touring revenue assumes a corridor circuit cadence (~12 show weeks/year). Without an active booker dedicated to it, calendar-fill is a part-time effort and shows happen on a slower clock.

Mitigation in flight: Sean is on bookings as of this month. The Sprinter van is in service. The venue directory is being hydrated city by city as a CRM, not just a public listing. Cash flow doesn't break if shows ramp to half the projection — it just slows the multiplier.

Medium risk

Cash-flow gap before publicity sales close

The risk: Publicity packages are projected at $110K Y1 but sales motion starts in Q2. If first paid package slips past June, summer cash gets tight.

Mitigation in flight: Stripe wiring is week-2 sprint. First package is sold to a friendly artist by the end of week 4, validated end-to-end. Photography and weddings carry the bridge if needed. We are not betting the company on Q2 publicity revenue — just the upside.

Medium risk

Tracy & Amy bandwidth as occupancy lifts

The risk: Inn occupancy targets (18% → 28% Y1) plus weddings plus magazine plus bar plus radio puts both partners at higher utilization. Burnout is a real number.

Mitigation in flight: Chandra (housekeeping) is already in place. Wedding coordination can be subcontracted out of the package. AI-drafted magazine pieces let Tracy edit instead of write from scratch. We add hands when occupancy proves, not before.

Medium risk

Music licensing scales with audience

The risk: SoundExchange and PRO licensing fees scale with listener-hours. As radio reach grows, so do the fees. Today they are negligible. At 10,000+ monthly listeners, they are real.

Mitigation in flight: Radio rotation favors owned masters and indie partners on direct licensing terms. We model the cost into the radio sponsorship rate card so it is paid for by the audience that triggers it.

Lower risk

Tax liability compounds

Chapter Thirteen

Where MBT Went.

MBT is a separate venture.

Measurably Better Things — the technology platform that powers HDI's properties — is its own project. It is Chase's open source work plus a book about regional creative economies. It is funded by HDI's operating revenue (because HDI uses it) and it takes opportunistic custom builds when they make sense. It does not have a sales pipeline on the HDI plan. It does not produce HDI revenue. It does not require any of the three partners to behave like enterprise software salespeople.

The full brief is in docs/MBT_VENTURE_BRIEF.md. The short version: HDI runs on it; HDI does not sell it. That separation is intentional. It removes the bottleneck that was forming when MBT was framed as a third revenue vertical, and it preserves all the optionality of the open source codebase for the future.
