## 1. Positioning

### Artist statement (≈200 words)

Arrie Aslin sings out of Natchez, Mississippi, where the Mississippi River bends back on itself and the bar at the Big Muddy Inn fills up about an hour before showtime. The voice is the front of the room: warm, lived-in, a little hoarse where it needs to be, and built for songs about staying put when you could've left. The band leans on upright bass, a Wurlitzer that hums when you turn it on, brushed drums, and a guitar that knows when to keep its mouth shut. The repertoire moves from her own writing — small-town gospel about hospitality, marriage, and Tuesday-night work — into reworked standards from the corridor that runs from Memphis down through Clarksdale into New Orleans. She's the artist-in-residence at the Blues Room at Big Muddy Inn, hosts a weekly hour on Big Muddy Radio, and tours a regional Snowbird Circuit each winter. The records come out on Big Muddy Records. If the sound has a thesis, it's this: the South already taught everybody how to sing about the river. Arrie Aslin's job is to keep singing about the people who still live on it.

### One-sentence pitch

> Arrie Aslin sings the Mississippi corridor — Natchez voice, Big Muddy Records, Blues Room residency.

### Genre / aesthetic anchor

**Recommended primary:** **Southern soul-blues** — voice-forward, hospitality-room arrangements, Stax-meets-front-porch instrumentation. Sits naturally next to Bettye LaVette, Yola, Adia Victoria, and the Robert Mugge films of Mississippi joints.

If Amy hasn't picked yet, propose:

1. **Southern soul-blues** *(recommended)* — broad enough to cover originals + standards, anchors her in the Blues Room, lets her tour with both blues and Americana bills. Best fit for Big Muddy Records' positioning.
2. **Americana / Mississippi gothic** — slightly literary, sets up a "songwriter who happens to live in Natchez" frame. Works if the originals lean storytelling-folk.
3. **River soul** *(coined)* — claims a sub-genre of her own. Good long-term branding play; risky if she's only got a half-album of material.

**Decision needed from Amy.** Default to (1) until she chooses.

## 3. Voice + tone

### Canonical statements (in her voice — polished, not transcribed)

1. *"I'm not from Natchez. But the river decided I was, and I stopped arguing about it."*
2. *"My job on stage is to make the room feel like somebody's grandmother's living room — where you can cry a little and nobody comments on it."*
3. *"Big Muddy Records is the label. Tracy runs the business. Chase runs the cameras. I just sing."*
4. *"The Blues Room residency means I get to test new songs on a real audience three nights a week. Most artists would kill for that."*
5. *"I don't write songs about leaving. Everybody already wrote those. I write songs about the ones who stayed."*

### Forbidden framings

- **Never** "up-and-coming," "rising," "emerging," "one to watch." She's a working artist with a residency and a label — not an unsigned hopeful.
- **Never** claim awards, charts, festival slots, or press coverage that doesn't exist. The honesty gate applies.
- **Never** describe her as a "frontwoman" or "songstress" — she's a singer.
- **Never** call her music "genre-defying," "eclectic," or "hard to categorize." It's Southern soul-blues with a residency at the Blues Room. Categorize it.
- **Never** lean on "powerful vocals" / "soulful voice" / "haunting" — generic music-press boilerplate.
- **Never** invoke Janis, Adele, or "an old soul in a young body." Lazy comparisons.
- **Never** stylize the name. **Arrie Aslin.** Not Arri, Ari, Aslan, or all-caps.
- **Never** position her as Amy Allen's side project. Arrie Aslin is the artist; Amy Allen is the legal name. Don't braid them in marketing copy.
- **Never** AI-generated promo imagery of her or her band.

Her records ship on Big Muddy Records. The work is hospitality-shaped: songs you can hear from the bar without having to lean in, sung in a room that smells like cornbread and bourbon.
