# Asana Policy & Procedure — Hillbilly Dreams Inc.

**For:** Amy, Tracy, Elijah, JP, and any future team member who uses Asana.
**Owner:** The Asana Coordinator (an AI agent managed by Chase).
**Version:** 2026-04-10 (v1).
**How long this takes to read:** Five minutes.

---

## What Asana is for (and what it isn't)

Asana is the shared to-do list for Hillbilly Dreams. It exists for three things:

1. **Work that needs to be tracked** across more than one day.
2. **Work that needs to be approved** — you look at something, say yes or no, done.
3. **Work that needs to be remembered** — a commitment, a deadline, a handoff.

Asana is **not** for:

- Five-minute fixes that one person is doing right now. That's a text message.
- Long conversations. That's Slack or iMessage.
- File storage. Files live where files live — Google Drive, Lightroom, the website. Asana links to them.
- Emotional weight. It shouldn't feel like an inbox full of alarms.

If you open Asana and it feels frantic, that's a bug. Tell Chase and it gets fixed.

---

## What the Asana Coordinator does

Chase works with an AI agent called the **Asana Coordinator.** Every task you see in Asana has been cleaned up by the coordinator before it landed in your list. Same format, same voice, same structure. No duplicates. No stale cards. No broken links.

The coordinator also does a **daily sweep at 4:00pm Central.** That's when new tasks land, stale tasks get archived, and you get a short housekeeping comment on the "START HERE" task telling you what changed. You should not get notifications at random hours. If you do, tell Chase.

**The coordinator will never:**

- Mark your task complete for you. You close your own work.
- Delete a task without asking first.
- Change a task's assignee without leaving a comment.
- Send you more than one notification per day about the same thing.
- Ship a task with a broken link, an emoji in the title, or all-caps yelling.

---

## The shape of a task (what you should see every time)

Every task in Asana looks like this:

> **Short summary of what to do and why.**
>
> Two or three plain sentences of context. No jargon. No hype.
>
> **WHAT TO DO**
> 1. First step
> 2. Second step
> 3. Third step
>
> **LINKS**
> - Open the thing — https://full-url-on-its-own-line
>
> **HOW TO APPROVE OR SEND BACK**
> Leave a comment saying "approved" or what needs to change, then mark it complete.

If a task does not look like this, it's not ready to ship. Tell Chase.

## How you approve things

Most of what lands in your list is **review and approve.** Something has been made — a photo gallery, a draft article, a web page, a signed document — and Chase needs you to look at it and say yes or no.

The rule is simple:

1. **Open the link** in the task. Do not look for files elsewhere. Everything you need is linked in the task body.
2. **Look at the thing.** Take your time.
3. **Leave one comment** with either "approved" or "change X."
4. **Mark the task complete.**

That is the entire workflow. You never have to download anything. You never have to hunt for the file. You never have to attach anything back to Asana. The file lives where the file lives — Asana is just the ledger of who approved what.

## How you ask Chase for something

If you need something from Chase — a decision, a file, money, a fix, a call — **create a task on yourself and assign it to Chase with a comment.** Don't make a new project. Don't invent a structure. Just:

1. New task in your own project (`Amy — Inn & Bar Ops`, `Tracy — Business & Finance`, `Elijah — Studio C`).
2. Title it plainly: "Need Chase to decide X" or "Need Chase to send me Y."
3. Reassign to Chase.
4. Set a due date if there is one.

Chase's coordinator will sweep it up in the 4pm batch and route it.

## Communications policy (the tone rules)

This is how tasks in Asana are written and how you can expect to be written to.

**What you will see:**
- Plain, direct language
- Clickable links
- One clear ask per task
- A due date, if there is one
- The same voice across every task

**What you will not see (anymore):**
- Emoji in task titles (no 🚨, 🔴, ⚠️, 🔥)
- All-caps yelling ("URGENT," "ASAP," "CRITICAL," "NOW")
- Exclamation points
- "This is blocking everything" pressure language
- Silent edits — if a task changes, you get a comment saying why
- The same task written five different ways across five different days

If you see any of these, that's a bug. Tell Chase.

## What to do if you're confused

1. **First, leave a comment on the task.** "I don't understand what you want me to do" or "which one first?" The coordinator watches for this and fixes the task + the pattern that produced it.
2. **If you need an answer faster than 4pm,** text Chase directly.
3. **If Asana is full of stuff that doesn't make sense,** tell Chase. He will ask the coordinator to do a sweep.

**You are never expected to just figure it out.** A confusing task is a bug the coordinator has to fix, not your problem to solve.

## What you own vs. what the coordinator owns

**You own:**
- Opening tasks on your list
- Approving or sending back the work
- Marking your own tasks complete
- Telling Chase when something feels off

**The coordinator owns:**
- The shape and tone of every task
- Routing tasks to the right person
- The daily 4pm sweep
- Archiving stale work
- Keeping the "START HERE" task up to date
- Translating Chase's mid-day stream of requests into one clean batch

**Chase owns:**
- Deciding what actually matters
- Approving anything destructive (kills, merges, reassignments)
- You. The coordinator works for Chase, and Chase works for the business. If you need a human, you go to Chase.

## The START HERE task

Each shared seat has a single **"START HERE"** task pinned to the top of the list. That task never gets deleted, renumbered, or replaced. It just gets updated. If you want to know what's going on right now, open the START HERE task — it has the current priorities and links to everything else.

The coordinator posts a short **"Housekeeping"** comment on the START HERE task every time it does a sweep. That comment explains what just changed and why, so you can trust the list is not moving under you.
