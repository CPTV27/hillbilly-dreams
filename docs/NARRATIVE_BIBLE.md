## 1. The Thesis (Why We Exist)

### The Problem

The system isn't going to fix itself. Build a new one next to it and let people choose.

Small towns lose money because dollars leave. 82 cents of every dollar spent in a small town leaves the zip code within 30 days -- through chains, through platforms, through extraction. The hardware store pays Yelp. The restaurant pays DoorDash. The barber pays Square. The money flows out and never comes back.

You're not poor. You're being drained.

For 30 years, small towns have been told the internet will save them. For 30 years, it has mostly extracted from them -- pulling ad dollars to Google, pulling storefronts to Amazon, pulling attention to platforms that have no interest in whether the hardware store on Main Street survives another winter.

The gap isn't technology. It's organization.

Nobody has organized these tools for Main Street. They've organized them for Manhattan. Every small business in town is paying for five or six tools they don't understand, that don't talk to each other, built by companies that have never heard of Natchez. The owner spends ten hours a week being an unpaid systems integrator.

### The Solution

Build local media infrastructure that keeps money, talent, and attention in the community.

The same architecture that runs a billion-dollar media company can run a small-town economy. The difference is they need a thousand people and a building in Midtown. We need four people, a good sound system, and a Victorian mansion on the Mississippi River.

Big Muddy is the proof. We book shows, run a radio station, publish a magazine, operate a record label, and run a hotel -- all from Natchez, Mississippi, all on one codebase, all for $31 a month in infrastructure costs.

Outsider Economics is the philosophy. It explains why local coordination beats extraction, why time is the only currency that can't leave town, and why federation beats scale.

MBT is the product. A licensed operating system that packages everything we built for ourselves and sells it to other towns, districts, and corridors.

### The Pitch Order

1. **Outsider Economics** -- why (the philosophy, the problem, the math)
2. **Big Muddy** -- proof (we did it, here's what happened)
3. **MBT** -- product (now you can buy the system)
4. **Numbers** -- business (here's the math on the business itself)

Never lead with the product. Always lead with the problem.

## 6. Voice Rules

### Chase's Writing Voice (Global)

Start with impact. The first sentence is the point. Not a warm-up. Not context. The point.

Short declarative sentences. No compounds over 20 words. If a sentence needs a semicolon, it needs to be two sentences.

State facts. Show math. Move on. "The infrastructure costs $31 a month." Not "Our infrastructure is remarkably cost-effective."

Dashes for asides -- like this -- not parentheses. Parentheses are for people who aren't sure they should be saying the thing. Say it or don't.

Name the enemy. Extraction, platforms, VC dependency, SaaS sprawl. Name them specifically. But never rant. State the problem. Show the alternative. Move on.

End every section with an action, not inspiration. "List your business here." Not "Together, we can build a better tomorrow."

"You" is the reader. "We" is the company. On Outsider Economics, there is no "we" -- the reader is the operator.

Numbers are always specific. Not "significant growth" -- "$450,000." Not "substantial savings" -- "$500 a month." If you don't have a specific number, don't use a vague one. Say "we don't have the number yet" or don't mention it.

No hedging. "We believe" is weak. "This is" is strong. If it's not true yet, say when it will be. Don't hedge.

**Big Muddy Touring** -- Road-worn, rock & roll, dark twilight. Professional but creative. Amber tones. Wet streets and stage lights. "Come play with us." The voice of a road manager who's seen 500 shows and still loves it.

**Big Muddy Radio** -- Fun outlaw radio. Friendly, wacky, silly, great personalities. NOT dark and brooding. This is the brand that gets to be weird. Inviting chaos. The DJ is your friend who has incredible taste and zero filter.

## 9. The Outsider Economics Connection

The book chapters map directly to the platform capabilities. This isn't a coincidence -- we built the philosophy first and then built the system.

### Chapter-to-Capability Map

**1. The $450,000 Secret / Community is the Currency**
*Book:* 20 people giving 20 hours of coordinated effort generates $450,000 in value. The currency is each other.
*Platform:* The Directory + the Musician Directory. Every listing is a node in the network. Every musician in the database is available to every venue. The coordination IS the product.
*Proof:* 31 artists, 26 venues, 13 cities. One booking database. The coordination premium is real and measurable.

**2. The Extraction Trap**
*Book:* 82 cents of every dollar leaves your zip code within 30 days. Chains, platforms, and SaaS tools are the extraction vehicles.
*Platform:* The local business ecosystem IS the counter-program. Every dollar spent on a DSD listing stays in the corridor. The magazine, the radio, and the directory are locally owned infrastructure. Money in. Value stays.
*Proof:* $31/month infrastructure cost. No VC. No extraction. Every dollar of revenue stays in Natchez.

**3. The Coordination Premium**
*Book:* Five people who talk to each other are worth 23x five people who don't. Metcalfe's Law applied to labor.
*Platform:* The platform connects existing people -- musicians, venues, photographers, writers, radio hosts. It captures the coordination premium by making connections that would otherwise take 50 phone calls.
*Proof:* The 2:1 multiplier. A $1,000 show generates $500+ downstream. That downstream doesn't happen without coordination.

**4. Time as Currency**
*Book:* Your neighbor's Saturday can't be wired to a hedge fund. Time-based exchange is structurally un-extractable.
*Platform:* Touring lets artists monetize their time without middlemen. The van, the booking, the promotion -- we remove every barrier between "I can play music" and "I got paid to play music."
*Proof:* Non-exclusive deals. Revenue shares: 50% door, 70% merch, streaming pass-through. The artist's time converts to money without giving up ownership.

**5. Federation over Scale**
*Book:* Every organization that scales past 100 people starts acting like the thing it was built to replace. Don't scale. Federate.
*Platform:* The multi-market model IS federation. Natchez runs independently. Bearsville runs independently. Market 3 runs independently. Same system. Different operators. No central authority required.
*Proof:* One codebase. Five tenants. Fourteen domains. $31/month. Adding a new market costs near-zero infrastructure.

**6. The Task Board**
*Book:* Not an app. A whiteboard with three columns. The simplest infrastructure that turns a neighborhood into an economy.
*Platform:* Delta Dawn IS a digital Task Board with AI. The morning SMS briefing tells each business owner what to fix today. The admin dashboard shows operators what to do tonight. Three columns: needs doing, in progress, done.
*Proof:* "Your Google listing says you close at 5. Your customers say you close at 9. Want us to fix it?" One text message. One fix. The Task Board in action.
