### 1. Four Visual Style Directions

1.  **"Fading Grandeur & Raw Elegance" (Color):**
    *   **Concept:** This is your Southern Gothic with a vibrant, yet melancholic, pulse. We’re looking for the beauty in decay, the stories etched into peeling paint, the dramatic interplay of light and shadow in antebellum spaces. It's rich, saturated, but never garish. Think high dynamic range not from a filter, but from natural light.
    *   **References:**
        *   **William Eggleston:** For his masterful use of everyday color, capturing the overlooked with an almost unsettling beauty.
        *   **Sally Mann's "Deep South":** For the sense of place, the mythic quality, and the way the landscape becomes a character.
        *   **Architectural Digest (early 2000s):** For interior shots that feel lived-in and luxurious, not staged for a quick sale.

2.  **"Tungsten Dreams & Bourbon Hues" (Color):**
    *   **Concept:** Lean *hard* into your strength: warm tungsten interiors. This is about capturing the cozy, inviting glow of the Inn, the intimate energy of the Blues Room, and the feeling of a well-worn space. Deep oranges, rich browns, and controlled highlights. It should feel like a sip of good bourbon on a cool night.
    *   **References:**
        *   **Todd Hido's "Homes at Night":** For mood, mystery, and the exquisite beauty of artificial light at dusk/night, hinting at stories within.
        *   **Esquire Magazine (classic features):** For editorial photography that puts you *inside* the moment, often with a slightly desaturated, filmic quality.
        *   **Gordon Parks' early color work:** For dignity, warmth, and an unflinching eye on humanity in its environment.

3.  **"Midnight Blues & Delta Grit" (Black & White):**
    *   **Concept:** Your documentary B&W needs to be more than just black and white; it needs to be *blues*. This is about high contrast, deep shadows, and visible grain that evokes the raw energy of live music, the contemplative quiet of a recording studio, or the worn faces of Natchez. It should feel timeless, unvarnished, and true.
    *   **References:**
        *   **Robert Frank's "The Americans":** For its unflinching, poetic gaze on American life, full of grit and nuance.
        *   **Anton Corbijn (early music photography):** For his iconic, stark, and deeply moody portraits and live shots that capture the essence of the artist.
        *   **LIFE Magazine (mid-century photojournalism):** For powerful, storytelling compositions that make you stop and look.

4.  **"River Light & Endless Sky" (Color/B&W Landscape):**
    *   **Concept:** The river bluffs and Natchez landscapes are not just scenery; they are characters in your story. Capture them with a sense of awe and expansive beauty. Focus on dramatic skies, the vastness of the Mississippi, and the way light interacts with the land. Whether in sweeping color or powerful B&W, these shots should ground the viewer in your geography.
    *   **References:**
        *   **Richard Misrach's "Desert Cantos":** For the powerful, almost spiritual connection to large landscapes, often with a subtle, painterly quality.
        *   **Ansel Adams (beyond the obvious):** Study his compositional mastery and the way he rendered the texture and volume of clouds and rock formations, even if your subjects are softer.
        *   **National Geographic (early landscape features):** For classic, compelling compositions that highlight the grandeur and majesty of a place.

### 3. Lightroom Processing Recommendations

Your processing needs to be as intentional as your shooting. Consistency is key across your vast content.

*   **White Balance:** For interiors, embrace the tungsten warmth. Don't neutralize it to sterile white. For exteriors, aim for natural, not overly cool. Learn to use the eyedropper for consistency, but trust your eye for mood.
*   **Exposure:** Expose for the highlights to retain detail, especially in your "Fading Grandeur" shots. Lift shadows only to reveal detail, not to flatten the image entirely.
*   **Contrast:** Develop a rich, deep contrast. Don't be afraid of true blacks. Use the Tone Curve to create subtle S-curves for depth, especially in B&W.
*   **Highlights & Shadows:** Pull highlights down to retain sky and bright source details. Push shadows up *just enough* to avoid crushed blacks and reveal subtle textures.
*   **Clarity & Dehaze:** Use sparingly. Clarity can add punch to textures but too much looks fake. Dehaze is great for atmospheric shots but can quickly make things look digital.
*   **Saturation/Vibrance:** For color, control saturation. Your "Tungsten Dreams" will have deep, natural saturation. For "Fading Grandeur," you might slightly desaturate greens and blues to push the warmth.
*   **Split Toning (Subtle):** Experiment with adding a touch of warm amber to highlights and a very subtle, almost imperceptible cool tone (e.g., deep blue/green) to shadows for specific moods, especially in your "Midnight Blues" B&W.
*   **Grain:** Essential for "Analog Warmth" and "Midnight Blues." Add subtle, film-like grain. Start around 20-30 amount, 20 size, 50 roughness, and adjust. It sells the analog feel.
*   **Sharpening:** Sharpen for output. Less for web, more for print. Always mask to avoid sharpening noise.

### 4. What to STOP Doing Visually

This is where we cut the dead weight.

*   **STOP:** **Over-processed HDR.** It looks dated, artificial, and strips your images of soul. Real drama comes from natural light, not a slider cranked to 11. Your "Fading Grandeur" should hint at decay, not shout it from the rooftops in garish tones.
*   **STOP:** **Generic, brightly lit, sterile photography.** Your brand isn't a dentist's office. You sell mood, authenticity, and history. Avoid flat, shadowless, over-exposed images that could be anywhere.
*   **STOP:** **Ignoring your existing strengths.** If warm tungsten is your forte, make it your signature. Don't fight it. If documentary B&W works, *work it*.
*   **STOP:** **Shooting everything wide and losing detail.** While establishing shots are crucial, zoom in. Get those close-ups of textures, hands, faces, and the small details that tell a bigger story. The Inn is full of stories, not just walls.
*   **STOP:** **Fake bokeh or excessive shallow depth of field for the sake of it.** Let the scene dictate your aperture. Focus on compelling composition and storytelling, not just blurring the background.
*   **STOP:** **Using repetitive compositions.** Find new angles for the river bluffs. Capture the Blues Room from the stage, from the back, from a corner. Push your perspective.

The gap isn't technology. It's organization. And visually, your current gap is focus. Let's make every image tell a story that feels like Hillbilly Dreams.
