# MBT Team Communication Norms

This document outlines how we communicate within the MBT team (Chase, Tracy, Amy, Studio C team, future hires). Our collective goal is to minimize coordination overhead, improve clarity, and ensure everyone has the information and space they need to do their best work. These aren't just rules; they're the foundational principles that enable us to build, innovate, and grow effectively. Read it, internalize it, and refer back to it.

---

### 1. The Channels: What to Use When

Choosing the right channel is crucial for efficiency. Using the wrong channel can lead to delays, missed information, or unnecessary interruptions. Here's our guide:

*   **Text (iMessage)**
    *   **Purpose:** For immediate, time-sensitive issues, day-of operational problems, quick yes/no questions, or urgent alerts that require someone's attention *now*. Think "I need to know this in the next 60 minutes."
    *   **Examples:** "Server is down," "Can you approve this payment in the next 15 mins?", "Running 5 mins late for the call, be there at 9:05," "Critical customer issue, need eyes on this ASAP."
    *   **Why:** This is the shortest path to reach someone quickly and directly. It cuts through the noise of other platforms. However, it's disruptive by nature, so reserve it for true urgency. It is **not** for discussions, detailed problem-solving, or anything that can wait an hour or two. If it's a longer conversation, a text should be followed by a call or a shift to Slack.
*   **Slack**
    *   **Purpose:** Our primary hub for asynchronous operational updates, team-wide visibility on ongoing work, sharing links/files, asking quick questions that don't need an immediate text response, and general team chat. Use specific channels (`#general`, `#marketing`, `#dev`, `#sales`, `#customer-support`) for relevant topics to keep discussions organized.
    *   **Examples:** "FYI, launched feature X to production," "Feedback on this design doc draft please?", "Sharing the Q2 marketing plan draft here for review," "Anyone have experience with [tool Y]?", "Quick question about [process Z]."
    *   **Why:** Slack provides a centralized, searchable, and visible platform for team discussions. It allows team members to respond when they have a moment, minimizing interruptions while keeping everyone informed. It's excellent for collective knowledge sharing and reducing email clutter. If a Slack thread becomes too long or complex, it's a signal to move it to a more structured channel (see "What NOT to do").
*   **Email**
    *   **Purpose:** For communication with external partners, formal documentation trails, anything HR/legal related, and long-form internal communication that requires a formal record or is highly detailed but less time-sensitive.
    *   **Examples:** "Contract draft for Vendor X," "FYI: Q3 earnings report and investor update," "HR policy update regarding PTO," "Formal proposal for Partner Y collaboration," "Detailed post-mortem analysis of the recent outage."
    *   **Why:** Email remains the standard for formal, external, and legally sensitive communications. It provides a clear, auditable record of decisions, agreements, and important information. It's also suitable for detailed, less urgent internal communications where a structured, permanent record is beneficial.
*   **Asana**
    *   **Purpose:** Our single source of truth for project tracking, task management, anything multi-week in scope, detailed requirements, long-term planning, and documenting decisions related to specific tasks or projects. All discussions, files, and decisions pertaining to a particular task or project should live here.
    *   **Examples:** "Task: Develop new landing page for Q3 campaign," "Bug: Login flow broken on iOS 17," "Project: Q4 Product Launch - Phase 1 Planning," "Blocking issue: API integration with [partner] is failing."
    *   **Why:** Asana keeps our work organized, visible, and accountable. It clarifies *what* we're doing, *who* is doing it, and *when* it's due. By centralizing task-related communication and documentation, it significantly reduces the need for status update meetings and ensures everyone knows the current state of a project.
*   **Phone Call / Video Call (Zoom)**
    *   **Purpose:** For strategic discussions, conflict resolution, complex problem-solving that requires real-time interaction, sensitive conversations (e.g., performance feedback), brainstorming sessions, or anything high-stakes where nuance, tone, and immediate back-and-forth are crucial.
    *   **Examples:** "Discussing Q3 strategic roadmap," "Resolving the recent customer complaint and next steps," "Brainstorming new product features for our next sprint," "Performance review discussion with team member," "Crisis management meeting."
    *   **Why:** While asynchronous communication is powerful, some discussions benefit immensely from real-time interaction. Calls allow for deeper engagement, quicker clarification, and the ability to read non-verbal cues. Always follow up a call with a summary in writing (email, Asana comment, or Slack message) to document key decisions and action items.

### 2. Response Time Expectations

Clear expectations around response times prevent frustration and ensure work keeps moving. We value focused work, so these aren't demands for immediate replies, but rather a guide for when a response should generally be expected.

*   **Text (iMessage): 1 hour during waking hours**
    *   **Clarification:** "Waking hours" refer to your typical working day. If you're out of office, on vacation, or it's 3 AM, this expectation does not apply. If something is truly life-or-death and you haven't received a text response, follow up with a call.
    *   **Rationale:** Texts are reserved for urgent matters. A 1-hour window allows for brief focus blocks without constant interruption but ensures critical issues are addressed quickly. It's a balance between urgency and respecting deep work.
*   **Slack: Same business day**
    *   **Clarification:** If you see a Slack message during your workday, aim to acknowledge or respond before the end of that day. This doesn't mean you need to drop everything immediately, but within a few hours is good practice for non-urgent items. If a message is sent late in the day, responding by the next morning is perfectly acceptable.
    *   **Rationale:** Slack is asynchronous by design. We want you to have uninterrupted blocks for focused work. However, timely responses keep momentum, prevent blockers, and show respect for your teammates' need for information.
*   **Email: 1 business day**
    *   **Clarification:** For external partners, this is a firm commitment. For internal emails, it's a good guideline. If an issue is truly urgent, it should not be communicated via email; use Slack or text.
    *   **Rationale:** Emails are often for less time-sensitive, more detailed communication. A full business day provides ample time for thoughtful replies, research, or coordination before responding.
