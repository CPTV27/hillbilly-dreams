### **Voice Rules Reminder: The Big Muddy Radio Sound**

Before we even hit record, let's nail down the vibe. Big Muddy Radio isn't just *any* podcast; it's a specific sonic experience. Our voice is distinct from the Big Muddy Magazine's long-form, deep-dive prose.

**Big Muddy Radio Voice:**
*   **Music-first, Scene-first:** We lead with the sound, the atmosphere, the feeling. Our narratives are woven *around* music, not just punctuated by it. Think of it as a soundtrack with commentary, rather than commentary with a soundtrack.
*   **Evocative & Immersive:** We want listeners to feel like they're right there, whether it's a smoky blues club, a quiet riverbank, or a bustling festival. Use descriptive language that paints pictures with sound.
*   **Conversational, but Focused:** While friendly and engaging, every word serves the story or the music. No rambling.
*   **Authentic & Rooted:** We celebrate the genuine, the raw, the soulful. Our stories come from the heart of the Big Muddy region and its musical traditions.
*   **Concise & Punchy:** Unlike long-form articles, radio thrives on impact. Get to the point, make it sing, and move on.

### **2. Recording: Capturing the Vibe**

This is where we press "record" and capture those precious sound waves.

*   **Mac mini OBS Workflow (at The Inn)**
    *   **Physical Setup:**
        *   **Microphones:** Connect our Rode NT-USB or similar quality microphones to the Mac mini. Ensure they are positioned correctly – about 6-12 inches from the speaker's mouth, slightly off-axis to reduce plosives.
        *   **Headphones:** Provide headphones for all participants (including yourself!) to monitor audio levels and prevent feedback.
        *   **Quiet Environment:** Close windows, turn off HVAC if possible, silence phones. Every little bit helps.
    *   **OBS Configuration (on Mac mini):**
        *   **Launch OBS:** Open the OBS Studio application.
        *   **Scenes:** We should have a pre-configured "Podcast Recording" scene. If not, create one.
        *   **Audio Input Sources:**
            *   In the "Sources" box, click the `+` icon and add "Audio Input Capture."
            *   Name it descriptively (e.g., "Guest Mic 1," "Host Mic").
            *   Select the correct USB microphone from the "Device" dropdown for each input.
            *   **Crucial:** *Ensure you're capturing each microphone as a separate track if possible*. This gives us maximum flexibility in post-production. In OBS, this means going to "Settings" > "Output" > "Recording" tab. Set "Recording Format" to `mkv` (more robust against crashes) or `mp4`. Under "Audio Track," select multiple tracks (e.g., 1, 2, 3) and then go to "Audio Mixer," click the gear icon next to each audio source, select "Advanced Audio Properties," and assign each mic to a unique track number.
        *   **Audio Monitoring:**
            *   In "Advanced Audio Properties," set "Audio Monitoring" for each mic to "Monitor and Output" so you can hear what's being recorded through your headphones.
            *   Adjust individual mic levels in the "Audio Mixer" to aim for peaks around -12dB to -6dB. Avoid clipping (red zone)! It's easier to boost quiet audio than fix distorted loud audio.
        *   **Recording Settings:**
            *   Go to "Settings" > "Output" > "Recording."
            *   **Recording Path:** Set this to a clear, accessible folder (e.g., `~/Documents/BigMuddyRadioRecordings/Episode_Name_Date`).
            *   **Recording Quality:** "Indistinguishable Quality, Large File Size" or "High Quality, Medium File Size."
            *   **Encoder:** Use hardware encoder if available (e.g., Apple VT H264) for better performance, otherwise software (x264).
            *   **Audio Bitrate:** 192 or 256 for each track is usually sufficient.
    *   **The Recording Process:**
        *   **Double Check:** Before hitting record, do a quick soundcheck with all participants. Ask everyone to say a few words.
        *   **Start Recording:** Click the "Start Recording" button.
        *   **Monitor:** Continuously monitor audio levels throughout the session. Listen for background noise, changes in mic position, or any issues. Make small adjustments if necessary.
        *   **Slates:** At the beginning, a quick verbal slate can be helpful: "Big Muddy Radio, Episode [Number/Title], Date."
        *   **Stop Recording:** Click "Stop Recording" when done.
    *   **File Management:**
        *   **Remux (if MKV):** If you recorded in `mkv`, go to "File" > "Remux Recordings" to convert it to `mp4`. This is safer.
        *   **Transfer:** Copy the raw audio files to our shared Google Drive or a designated network drive immediately. Rename them clearly (e.g., `BMR_EpXX_RawAudio_Host.mp4`, `BMR_EpXX_RawAudio_Guest.mp4`).

*   **Remote Recording (Briefly):**
    *   **Riverside.fm:** Log in, create a new studio, invite guests. The platform guides you through local recording and uploading. Download the separate high-quality audio tracks (WAV or MP3).
    *   **Zoom:** Start a meeting, ensure "Record a separate audio track for each participant" is enabled in your Zoom settings (before the meeting). Record to your local computer. Afterward, locate the `audio_only.m4a` files for each participant in the meeting folder.

### **3. Post-production: Polishing the Gem**

This is where we transform raw recordings into a smooth, listenable episode.

*   **Basic Edit: The Art of Subtraction and Balance**
    *   **Software:** Use your preferred DAW (Digital Audio Workstation). **Audacity** (free, open-source), **GarageBand** (free on Mac), or the **Fairlight page in DaVinci Resolve** (free) are excellent for basic podcast editing.
    *   **Import Tracks:** Bring all individual audio tracks into your DAW.
    *   **Synchronization:** If recorded separately, align the tracks. A verbal clap or count-in at the start of the recording helps immensely.
    *   **Remove Silences & Filler Words:**
        *   Listen critically. Cut out long pauses, "ums," "ahs," stutters, or repeated phrases that don't add value.
        *   Be judicious: natural pauses are good; dead air is not.
        *   *Pro Tip:* Use a tool like Descript if you have access – it transcribes and lets you edit audio by editing text, which is incredibly fast for removing silences and filler.
    *   **Fix Levels (Normalization & Compression):**
        *   **Gain Stage:** Adjust the individual track volumes so that all speakers are roughly at the same perceived loudness.
        *   **Normalization:** Apply "Normalize" (or "Loudness Normalization") to each track to bring their peak levels to a consistent target (e.g., -3dB).
        *   **Compression:** Apply a light compressor to even out dynamic range (loud parts aren't *too* loud, quiet parts aren't *too* quiet). A typical setting might be a ratio of 2:1 or 3:1, with a fast attack and medium release.
        *   **Overall Loudness:** Aim for an integrated loudness of -16 LUFS for stereo or -19 LUFS for mono, which is standard for podcasts. Many DAWs have a loudness meter or normalization tool that can hit this target.
    *   **Add Intro/Outro Music & SFX:** Integrate our standard Big Muddy Radio intro/outro music. Add any relevant sound effects (SFX) if they enhance the narrative. Ensure music is mixed *under* dialogue.
    *   **Export:**
        *   **Format:** MP3.
        *   **Bitrate:** 128 kbps (stereo) or 64 kbps (mono) is generally good for voice podcasts. Higher bitrates (192-256 kbps) if there's a lot of music.
        *   **Channel:** Stereo if you have distinct left/right elements, but mono is often sufficient and saves file size for podcasts. If it's mostly dialogue, mono is fine.
        *   **Naming Convention:** `BMR_EpisodeXX_TitleSlug.mp3` (e.g., `BMR_Episode01_BluesInTheDelta.mp3`).

*   **Upload to GCS: Our Digital Archive**
    *   Once your polished MP3 is ready, upload it to our designated Google Cloud Storage bucket.
    *   **Path:** `gs://big-muddy-radio-episodes/`
    *   Ensure the file is publicly accessible if it needs to be served directly from GCS for testing or specific uses, though Buzzsprout will handle the primary hosting.

### **Common Pitfalls: Traps to Avoid**

Even the best programmers hit bugs. Here are some common pitfalls in podcasting:

*   **Sloppy Levels:** The absolute cardinal sin. Nothing turns a listener off faster than wildly fluctuating audio or distorted sound. Always monitor, always edit, always normalize. A consistent, clear audio experience is paramount.
*   **Missing Show Notes:** Without show notes, listeners can't easily find the artists, books, or places you mentioned. It's like releasing software without documentation – frustrating! Always include comprehensive show notes in Sanity.
*   **Late Publish Breaking the Cadence:** Our audience expects consistency. If we say "new episode every Tuesday," we need to deliver. A late publish disrupts listener habits and can cost us engagement. Use `publishedAt` to schedule ahead!
*   **Forgetting Cross-promotion:** A great episode without promotion is like a hidden gem. Ensure all social, web, and TikTok Live coordination is done to get the word out effectively.
*   **Ignoring Analytics:** We put in the work; let's see how it's received. Analytics aren't just numbers; they're insights into our audience and content strategy.

### **Cadence Target: Finding Our Rhythm**

Consistency is key to building an audience. We need to decide on a rhythm and stick to it.

*   **Weekly:** High frequency, keeps the audience engaged, but requires a consistent flow of content and significant production effort. Great for hot topics or ongoing series.
*   **Bi-weekly (Every Other Week):** A good balance. Allows more time for in-depth production and guest coordination, while still keeping the show fresh in listeners' minds. This is often a sweet spot.
*   **Monthly:** Less frequent, allows for very deep dives, complex productions, or seasonal content. Can be harder to maintain consistent audience growth, but good for highly specialized or long-form narrative podcasts.

**Our Goal:** Let's discuss and agree on a sustainable cadence that aligns with our content goals and production capacity. Once decided, we commit to it.
