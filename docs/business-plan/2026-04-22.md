**Connective tissue:**

- **Studio C** — production muscle. DBA of Tuthill Design LLC. Operates the Theater, captures the Hollow, supports the Inn and the Magazine.

**Chase's living arrangement:** no fixed residence. He floats. Woodstock summer = rental + friends. On the road = hotels. Natchez = stay with Amy and Tracy. This is by design and reduces overhead immediately (Bearsville lease exit is committed).

## 1. The strategic frame

### 1.1 Why this works

Three ideas are doing the structural work:

1. **The corridor model.** Natchez is the center of gravity. Bearsville is a working node, not a parallel headquarters. The Hudson Valley footprint reduces over the next year. The Mississippi corridor (and the Snowbird circuit it implies) is the operating ground.

2. **The triangle.** The Hollow creates (community, residencies, content origin). Studio C produces (capture, post, deliverables, tour support). The Theater publishes to the public (ticketed shows, live captures, press). Each leg feeds the others. Story arcs write themselves: rehearsals at the Hollow, load-in at the Theater, a residency that becomes an album that becomes a film that becomes a tour.

3. **The marquee-and-operator split.** Rea is the visible name on the door of the Theater. Studio C is the operating brain. We don't need to own the bricks — control of the calendar and the story matters more than title for the first five years. The same model applies elsewhere: artists in front, infrastructure behind, the company quietly running the control room.

### 1.2 What this is not

- **Not a SaaS pitch.** MBT is a holding/operating company that runs real businesses. The platform is the connective tissue, not a separately-priced product to Main Street SMBs. The walk-in-DSD pricing motion is deprecated.
- **Not a one-region play.** Hudson Valley reduces but does not disappear. Studio C's Bearsville footprint stays put. Summer programming continues.
- **Not a single-founder shop.** MBT is a three-way partnership. Roles are real.

### 4.6 The Theater (Ritz or comparable city-owned venue)

**Status:** **Promoted from "parked" to "active plan to develop."** Business plan in this document is the first draft.

**What it is:** A historic or city-owned theater operated as the urban-stage counterpart to the Hollow. The model: Rea as marquee artist, Studio C as operator, the city as building owner. Content captured live, productions developed in the room, the venue tied narratively to the Hollow.

**Why a five-year project lease beats ownership in year one:**

- Many small cities own historic theaters and lease them cheaply (sometimes nominally — a dollar a year, in some textbook precedents) to nonprofits or operators in exchange for activating downtown and taking maintenance off city books.
- Multi-year resident-company arrangements are common: the city retains ownership, the operator runs the calendar.
- Control of the calendar and the story is more valuable than title for the first five years. Ownership becomes a Phase 2 question once the model proves itself.

**What we offer the city (or a private owner):**
- 5-year programming pilot with renewal options
- A guaranteed minimum number of events per year
- Capital improvements: sound, lights, video capture (Rea's rig, our system design)
- A media footprint — the magazine, Studio C captures, press around revitalization
- A partner local civic leaders can point to

**What we ask for:**
- Favorable lease terms (nominal rent or revenue-share)
- Flexible use of an underused asset
- Possibly: in-kind support (marketing, tourism promotion, small grants)

**Rea's gear as marketing:**
- A visual signature of the room — "the Rea wall," certain amps always present, a particular console
- A story hook — "this theater is wired around Rea's ears." People buy into that the way they buy into legendary studios with iconic boards
- Becomes a character in the documentary work — the cables, the racks, the flight cases

**Story arcs the triangle creates:**
- "From the Hollow to the Theater" mini-series — rehearsals on the land, load-in at the venue, the show, the debrief
- Seasonal residencies — small artists live and work at the Hollow, then perform at the Theater, with Studio C capturing and releasing the cycle
- Live records, concert films, behind-the-scenes content, all of it published through the magazine

**What's next:**
- Identify the building. Three parallel tracks:
  1. The actual Ritz Theater (Natchez) — Chase has prior conversations to draw on
  2. Other city-owned theaters or auditoriums in Natchez
  3. Private-owner historic venues in the corridor
- Define the **minimum viable theater**: capacity, tech spec, location traits, lease terms that make the model worth doing
- Draft the **2-3 page "Project Theater" one-sheet** for owners and city officials

- Confirm Rea's role and rough deal structure with him directly (Chase to lead)
- Identify the right city contact (Mayor's office, Main Street program, tourism board)

## Appendix A — Property research findings (initial)

**Search date:** 2026-04-22 evening
**Goal:** Surface what's on the market in the Natchez / Adams County area for tiny house community development. Not a recommendation — a starting point.

### Adams County, MS — market snapshot

- ~97 properties for sale countywide (LandSearch)
- Median listing price: $238K (Redfin); average list: ~$309K (LandSearch)
- ~$63M of total land value listed; >1,400 acres available (LandWatch)
- Average price per acre: ~$55,500 (LandSearch)

### Notable parcels surfaced

- **10.62-acre tract**, Natchez, on a quiet dead-end road — privacy, wildlife, suited to homesite or small community development
- **11.51-acre surveyed tract** near Homochitto National Forest — accessible from US Hwy 84/98, hunting access
- **12-acre wooded property** — recreation + residential potential

These are starting points. A real property short-list needs Chase + Tracy to walk the land, check soil/access/utilities, and confirm the parcel works for a manufacturing workshop + cluster of small dwellings.

### Mississippi tiny-home regulatory context

- **No statewide tiny-home regulations.** Mississippi is one of a handful of states without state-level rules.
- **County and municipal code is decentralized.** Two towns in the same county can have opposite rules.
- **Most rural counties don't have building codes**, leaving enforcement to the smaller jurisdictions.
- **Tiny-home subdivisions are recognized as a path** to navigate the regulatory patchwork — designating an area for tiny homes lets local government build a framework around it instead of fighting individual permits.
- **Adams County specifically:** zoning and building code situation needs direct confirmation with the county planning office before any property is committed.
