What They're Building Out at the Hollow

Twenty miles east of Natchez, an eighteen-acre experiment is asking what happens when a small group of people decide they'd rather build a life than buy one — and follow the sun while doing it.

By Carrie Dicks
Adams County, Mississippi

The road to the Hollow is the kind that doesn't get paved because nobody who lives down it has ever asked. It cuts off the highway between Natchez and Liberty, runs past two trailer churches and a hand-painted sign for boiled peanuts, then drops into a stand of pine where the light goes yellow and slow.

A hundred yards back from the gravel turnaround, you can hear what the place is before you can see it. Somebody has the shop door open and is sanding something with the long, patient strokes of a person who is building it themselves and isn't in any hurry. From farther back: a screen door, a chicken, water running for what sounds like a garden hose.

• • •

The Hollow occupies eighteen acres of mixed pine and creek bottom in Adams County, just outside the limit where Natchez stops being Natchez. It is not a commune. It is not a development in the sense the word usually means. There is no gate, no clubhouse, no pickleball court.

What it is, if you have to define it in a sentence, is a small group of people who decided that the way most rural land in the South gets used — sit on it, mow it, watch it appreciate, sell it — was a strange use for land in a region that still has, against the grain of every other place in the country, its own water, its own quiet, and a kind of light you can't buy.

So they built houses on it. Five of them so far. None bigger than a thousand square feet. Each with its own porch, its own water hookup, its own rocking chair that nobody bought, the chair just appeared one day on the porch the way certain things do down here. The houses are arranged loosely around a central pole barn that does triple duty: a workshop, a place to make whatever needs making, and on Friday nights a dance hall that holds about thirty people if everybody breathes shallow.

• • •

The premise is straightforward enough to fit on the back of a napkin. Most of the people you'd actually want as neighbors — the writers who paid New York rent for twenty years, the photographers who slept in their darkrooms, the painters who taught adjunct into their fifties, the cooks, the carpenters, the session players, the small-press editors, the people who spent the better part of their working lives making things and renting the place they made them in — eventually look up and find that a lifetime of good work produced a checking account, a pile of receipts, and no piece of ground anywhere with their name on it.

The Hollow is built on the idea that this is not a law of nature. It's a market failure.

"Most of the people I know who actually made things never owned anything. Not a house, not a plot, not a porch. They rented their whole life and then they died."
• • •

The way you become a resident is not by writing a check. Or rather, not only by writing a check. The structure was put together by a small Mississippi LLC that owns the land and the buildings, with the residents as members. To join the founding circle, you trade what you have for what you don't.

One resident traded a paid-off house in another town. Another traded years of hospitality experience and a five-year commitment to plan and run the eventual guest operation, papered as labor against equity over a vest. A third traded a combination of cash and on-property work — running the kitchen on Friday nights, keeping the pump house functional, managing the garden. Founding shares are capped. After the founding circle closes, additional residents come in as renters with access to the shared facilities but no equity in the land.

Profits from the eventual guest operation get split across the membership. Decisions are made at a Sunday breakfast that is technically a board meeting and practically the only board meeting any of them have ever wanted to attend.

The phrase that comes up, in different words, from almost everybody who lives there: we didn't want roommates. Roommates are when you live in the same house with people you don't choose. This is when you live in the same place with people you do.

• • •

The Hollow runs on a two-property cycle. Mississippi is the winter half — roughly October through April, when the pine smells like cold smoke and the dance hall is full on Friday nights. The summer half of the year happens at a sister property in the Catskills, two days' drive north, where the same residents trade the long Mississippi heat for cooler air, a different garden, and the kind of upstate New York stretch where the price tag at the farmstand is still written on cardboard. One membership. One ledger. Two pieces of ground. Follow the sun.

• • •

The thing the residents come back to, when they talk about why this place and not somewhere else, is independence. Water from a well. Power that's mostly solar with a generator for the bad days. A garden that runs along the south side and feeds both the houses and, eventually, the guest table. Tomatoes, okra, the kind of greens you can't buy at the Natchez Kroger because they bruise too easy to ship. Eggs from a coop you can hear from any porch. The closest grocery store at thirty minutes; the closest neighbor at two hundred yards. The kind of internet that does what writers and editors and small-business operators need it to do, and doesn't do anything else.

The hospitality side is coming. The plan is small — eight units, maybe ten — and the kind of place where you stay for three days because somebody you've never met invites you to a porch supper. There will be a garden, run by people who know the names of seven kinds of okra. There will be a pole barn that has shows on Friday. There will probably be a dog.

• • •

Twenty miles east of Natchez is not, in any obvious sense, where anybody would expect this kind of project to take root. The Mississippi River corridor is famous for music and food and the long literary tradition of people writing about both, but its institutional infrastructure runs through New Orleans on one end and Memphis on the other. Natchez itself has a handful of venues, an Inn that books a few nights a month, a couple of festivals.

What it does have is a price. Land here costs a fraction of what it costs in Asheville or Marfa or any of the lifestyle towns you've heard of. The county is permissive about agricultural-zoned outbuildings. Power and water are reachable from the main road without a project. The growing season is long. And the highway out runs in three directions, all of them toward something.

The math, in other words, has been sitting there the entire time. The Hollow is what happens when somebody actually does it.

• • •

I leave on a Tuesday afternoon. The light is the yellow Mississippi pine light that doesn't show up in photographs. Somebody is in the shop, working on something I can't see from the road. Somebody else is in the garden.

The thing about the Hollow that you can't quite put into a magazine article is the thing the residents themselves have the hardest time articulating: it is not, in any conventional sense, a project. It is not trying to scale. It is not trying to disrupt anything. It is, instead, the slow and almost embarrassing return of an old idea — that a small group of people, with a small amount of land, and a clear sense of what they wanted out of a life, could build something together that lasted longer than any of them and was nicer to live in than what most of them had.

Asked what they would tell another person thinking about the same kind of move, one of the founders, standing on a porch at the end of the day, said it like this:

"Bring something you know how to make. We've got the rest of the room."

The Hollow does not currently take public guest reservations; the first booking window is anticipated for fall 2026.
