# What They're Building Out at the Hollow

*Twenty miles east of Natchez, a former blues drummer and a Hudson Valley transplant are doing something stranger than a commune and quieter than a real-estate play. They're calling it home.*

**By Carrie Dicks**

---

The road to the Hollow is the kind that doesn't get paved because nobody who lives down it has ever asked. It cuts off the highway between Natchez and Liberty, runs past two trailer churches and a hand-painted sign for boiled peanuts, then drops into a stand of pine where the light goes yellow and slow.

You hear Wes before you see him. The shop sits a hundred yards back from the gravel turnaround, and on a Tuesday morning the sound coming out of it is somebody hitting a snare drum the way people used to hit snare drums — not for a click track, not for a producer, but because the shop is full of sawdust and the snare needs to be tuned and the easiest way to tune a snare is to play it.

"That'll do," Wes says, when I walk in. He's standing at a band saw, half a cabinet door in his hands, the snare on a folding stand beside him. Behind him is a workbench that looks like it was built before he was born, and it was. "My granddad's. Came down on a flatbed from Florence. Took me two days to get it level."

Wes — last name unprinted at his request, "I haven't earned a last name in a magazine yet" — moved here in the fall. He'd been in Muscle Shoals for eleven years before that, in the same small house, with his mother in a cottage out back, and a dog named Ranger that follows him from room to room and refuses to acknowledge most strangers. The dog is here now, asleep on a strip of carpet under the band saw, in the kind of pose that suggests he gave the question of the move about ninety seconds of thought before deciding.

"My granddad was a drummer too," Wes says. "Played in church and then played in juke joints and then played in church again. He died trying to figure out how to do both at the same time."

---

The Hollow occupies eighteen acres of mixed pine and creek bottom in Adams County, just outside the limit where Natchez stops being Natchez and becomes the rural part of the parish that nobody on Trip Advisor has ever heard of. It is not a commune. It is not a development in the sense that the word usually means. There is no gate, no clubhouse, no wine club, no pickleball court.

What it is, if you have to define it in a sentence, is a small group of people who decided that the way most rural land in the South gets used — sit on it, mow it, watch it appreciate, sell it — was a strange use for land in a region that produced more music per square mile than any other in the country.

So they built houses on it. Five of them so far. None bigger than a thousand square feet. Each one with its own porch, its own water hookup, its own rocking chair that nobody bought, the chair just *appeared* one day on the porch the way certain things do down here.

The houses are arranged loosely around a central pole barn that does triple duty: a workshop, a rehearsal space, and on Friday nights a dance hall that holds about thirty people if everybody breathes shallow. The pole barn was the first structure on the property. It went up in October. It is also, as of this writing, where Wes is standing.

"This is the room I always wanted," he says, and he means the pole barn, but he also means more than that. "I wanted a room where the snare didn't have to apologize for itself."

---

Delia Weaver runs the hospitality side. She came down from the Hudson Valley last year — "Hudson Valley refugee," she calls herself, with the kind of voice that suggests she's said it a hundred times and gotten a laugh every time — and she brought a garden plan, a Rolodex of session musicians, and an old Subaru with a roof rack that has not been removed in nine years.

I find her in the second of the houses, the one with the green porch, sitting on the floor of what will eventually be a wedding-night room, sketching. She has a pencil tucked behind her ear and a glass of iced tea on the floorboards next to her, and she is laughing at something the dog has done, which I did not see, because the dog has already moved on.

"We don't take guests yet," she says, "but we will. The plan is small. Eight units, maybe ten. The kind of place where you stay for three days because somebody you've never met invites you to a porch supper."

Delia spent fifteen years in Nashville before the Hudson Valley. Session vocals, mostly. Background work. The kind of career that pays the rent if you don't ask too many questions about what success was supposed to look like. She doesn't talk about it much. When I ask, she shrugs in a way that is not a non-answer so much as a way of saying *that part is done now, and this part is starting.*

The garden, when it comes in this summer, will run along the south side of the property and feed both the houses and the eventual guest table. Tomatoes, okra, the kind of greens you can't buy at the Natchez Kroger because they bruise too easy to ship. Delia knows the names of seven kinds of okra. I asked.

---

The economic arrangement is the part that most people get wrong when they hear about it secondhand.

It is not, despite the impression you might form from the porches and the iced tea, a charity case or a lifestyle subsidy. It is a cooperative — a real one, with a real ledger — and the way you become a resident is by trading what you have for what you don't. Wes traded his Muscle Shoals house. Delia traded her time in the garden and the hospitality plan, papered as labor against equity over a five-year vest. Two of the other residents are former touring musicians who traded a combination of cash and on-property work — one of them runs the rehearsal calendar for the pole barn; the other is the night cook on Fridays.

The structure was put together by a small Mississippi LLC that owns the land and the buildings, with the residents as members. Profits from the eventual guest operation get split across the membership. Decisions are made at a Sunday breakfast that is technically a board meeting and practically the only board meeting any of them have ever wanted to attend.

"We didn't want roommates," Wes says. "Roommates are when you live in the same house with people you don't choose. This is when you live in the same place with people you do."

---

Twenty miles east of Natchez is not, in any obvious sense, where anybody would expect this kind of project to take root. The Mississippi River corridor is famous for music, but its institutional music infrastructure runs through New Orleans on one end and Memphis on the other. Natchez has a handful of venues, an Inn that books a few nights a month, a couple of festivals. It does not have, and has never had, a recording studio of national reputation.

What it does have, the residents at the Hollow will tell you, is a *price.* Land here costs a fraction of what it costs in Asheville or Nashville or — and Delia laughs at this one — the Catskills. The county is permissive about agricultural-zoned outbuildings. Power and water are reachable from the main road without a project. And the closest blues musicians of national reputation are a half-tank of gas away, on the same highway that put Robert Johnson in the back of every musicology textbook ever written.

"We're not the first people to figure out the math here," Wes says. "We're just the first ones who were dumb enough to actually do it."

---

There is a second piece of land, three states north, that the same group is looking at for the summer of 2027. It's near the small town of East Durham, in Greene County, New York — the part of the Catskills that still has Irish dance halls and the kind of farmstand where the price tags are written on cardboard. The plan there is similar: a small parcel, a pole barn first, a few residences after, a garden, a rehearsal space, eventually a guest table for people coming through.

"Same model," Delia says. "Different vegetables."

The northern site has a partner — a friend of Delia's from her Hudson Valley years who already owns land in the area and has been raising goats and growing herbs on it for a decade. The thinking is that the two sites become a corridor: residents and guests rotate between Mississippi in the cool months and the Catskills in the warm ones, with the touring infrastructure of the Big Muddy organization providing the connective tissue between the two.

If it works, it would be the first time in any of their memories that a working musician of modest means had a shot at owning equity in a place — *two* places — without having to choose between rent and craft for the rest of his career.

"That's the thing nobody talks about," Wes says. "Most musicians I know never owned anything. Not a house, not a plot, not a porch. They rented their whole life and then they died."

He says this without bitterness. He says it the way somebody says the weather forecast.

---

I leave on a Tuesday afternoon. The light is that yellow Mississippi pine light that doesn't show up in photographs. Delia is on the green porch, the dog has reattached himself to Wes, and somebody — I don't know who, I don't see them — is playing a piano somewhere in the pole barn, badly, the way somebody plays who is just sitting down for the first time after a long day.

The road out is the same road in, and on the way back to Natchez I pass two of the same trailer churches and the same boiled-peanuts sign. The sign still has not been repainted. The peanuts, presumably, are fine.

The thing about the Hollow that you can't quite put into a magazine article is the thing that the residents themselves have the hardest time articulating: it is not, in any conventional sense, a project. It is not trying to scale. It is not trying to disrupt anything. It is not trying to be replicable in a venture-capital sense.

It is, instead, the slow and almost embarrassing return of an old idea — that a small group of people, with a small amount of land, and a clear sense of what they wanted out of a life, could build something together that lasted longer than any of them and was nicer to live in than what most of them had.

Wes was asked, before I left, what he would tell another musician thinking about the same kind of move.

"Bring your own snare," he said. "We've got the rest of the room."
