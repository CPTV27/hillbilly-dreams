An Invitation · Adams County, Mississippi

The Hollow

Eighteen acres in Mississippi. A second piece of ground in the Catskills. A small cooperative for people who would rather build a life than buy one.

The Premise

Most of the people you'd actually want as neighbors spend their entire working lives renting. The apartment. The studio. The shop. The desk in the back of someone else's office.

And then one day they look up and discover that thirty years of good work produced a checking account, a pile of receipts, and no piece of ground anywhere with their name on it.

The Hollow is built on the idea that this is not a law of nature. It's a market failure.

What It Is

Eighteen acres of pine and creek bottom.

Twenty miles east of Natchez, just outside the limit where Natchez stops being Natchez. Five small houses, none larger than a thousand square feet, arranged loosely around a central pole barn that does triple duty: a workshop, a place to make whatever needs making, and on Friday nights a dance hall that holds about thirty people if everybody breathes shallow.

It is not a commune. It is not a development in the sense the word usually means. There is no gate, no clubhouse, no pickleball court. There is a garden. There is a chicken coop. There is, almost always, a dog.

How Residency Works

You trade what you have for what you don't.

The structure is a small Mississippi LLC that owns the land and the buildings, with the residents as members. The founding circle is capped — once it closes, additional residents come in as renters with access to the shared facilities but no equity in the land.

To join the founding circle, you bring some combination of cash, a paid-off asset, or a five-year role at the property — running the kitchen on Friday nights, planning the guest operation, keeping the pump house functional, anchoring the garden. Profits from the eventual guest operation get split across the membership.

Decisions are made at a Sunday breakfast that is technically a board meeting and practically the only board meeting any of them have ever wanted to attend.

The Cycle

Two properties. One residency. Follow the sun.

Mississippi is the winter half — roughly October through April, when the pine smells like cold smoke, the garden gives way to greens and root crops, and the dance hall is full on Friday nights.

The summer half of the year happens at a sister property in the Catskills, two days' drive north. Cooler air. A different garden. A different porch. The kind of upstate New York stretch where the price tag at the farmstand is still written on cardboard.

One membership. One ledger. Two pieces of ground. You pack twice a year and you live where the weather is good.

Perspective One

If you'd rather unplug from the grid.

You're tired of paying a utility company you've never met for water that runs through pipes you can't see. You're tired of a power bill that goes up every quarter for reasons nobody can explain. You'd rather have a well, a solar array, a generator for the bad days, and a neighbor who knows what to do when the well pump quits.

The Hollow runs as close to off-grid as a working property can. Water from the ground. Power mostly from the sun. Internet that does what you need and not what you don't. A road in that's just bad enough to keep most people from finding it.

What you might bring in: Cash, sweat, and a willingness to know how the systems on your land actually work.
06 / 12
Perspective Two

If you want to grow your own food.

You've spent enough years buying tomatoes that taste like nothing. You'd rather know what's in your dinner. You'd rather hand somebody a basket of okra and watch them figure out what to do with it. You want a real garden — not a raised bed in a yard, a real one, with rows and seasons and the kind of dirt you fight for.

The Hollow has the south side of the property planted and waiting to be planted. Tomatoes, okra, the kind of greens you can't buy at the Natchez Kroger because they bruise too easy to ship. A coop. A long growing season. A second garden up north for the months when Mississippi is asleep.

What you might bring in: Cash and stewardship of the garden — planning, planting, the running record of what works and what doesn't.

Perspective Three

If your work happens with your hands.

Wood, leather, clay, metal, fabric, food, instruments, machines. You've spent years renting shop space, sharing benches, working around somebody else's hours. You want a workbench that's yours. You want a door you can leave open in the spring. You want to make a mess that nobody is going to ask you to clean up by closing time.

The Hollow's pole barn has a shop side that's already going. There's room for more. The other residents will quietly buy whatever you make — they're that kind of crowd — and the eventual guest operation will give your work a place to land in front of strangers too.

What you might bring in: Cash and a working shop discipline — the room runs on the people who actually use it.
08 / 12
Perspective Four

If your work happens at a desk and you'd rather it happen on a porch.

Writers, editors, designers, small-business operators, the people who do the kind of work that travels in a laptop. You've done the math on your commute. You've done the math on your rent. You've sat in enough coffee shops with bad chairs and worse coffee. You'd rather have a porch, a quiet hour between dawn and the heat of the day, and a community that doesn't ask you what you're working on until you bring it up.

The Hollow has all of that. The internet works for what you need. The garden runs without you if you want it to. There's a creek you can walk to. The rest of the community is close enough that you don't get lonely and far enough that you don't get interrupted.

What you might bring in: Cash and the published voice of the place — newsletter, occasional liner notes, residency journals.
09 / 12
Perspective Five

If you want a community without losing the door that locks.

You've tried the co-living thing. You've tried the family-compound thing. You've tried the "we'll all get a duplex" thing. None of it worked, because the math of shared walls and shared kitchens always asked something of you that you weren't quite willing to give.

The Hollow is the version that works. Your own house. Your own porch. Your own door. The shared parts — the pole barn, the kitchen on Friday nights, the garden, the workshop — are entirely optional. You walk over when you want to. You stay home when you don't. The whole property runs on the assumption that the door is the most important thing on the house.

What you might bring in: Cash, a paid-off asset, or a five-year role anchoring one of the shared pieces.

Why Here

The math has been sitting there the entire time.

Twenty miles east of Natchez is not, in any obvious sense, where anybody would expect this kind of project to take root. The Mississippi River corridor is famous, but its institutional infrastructure runs through New Orleans on one end and Memphis on the other.

What this stretch has is a price. Land here costs a fraction of what it costs in Asheville or Marfa or any of the lifestyle towns you've heard of. The county is permissive about agricultural-zoned outbuildings. Power and water are reachable from the main road without a project. The growing season is long. The highway out runs in three directions, all of them toward something.

The Catskills half of the cycle works on a similar logic. Greene County still has farmstands with cardboard price tags. Land is more expensive than Mississippi but a fraction of what the same vibe costs in the Hudson Valley proper or the Berkshires.

The Hollow is what happens when somebody actually does it.
