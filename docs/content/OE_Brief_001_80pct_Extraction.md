# Outsider Economics Brief — Issue 001
## The 80% Extraction Rate (And How to Stop It)

*Published by Hillbilly Dreams, Inc. | Technology Platform for the New South*

---

Eighty cents of every dollar earned in a Deep South community leaves within forty-eight hours.

Not because people spend recklessly. Not because the local economy is broken in any mysterious way. Because the infrastructure of daily commerce was built to move money out — and it does its job perfectly.

This is the 80% extraction rate. It is not a statistic you will find on a policy brief from a regional development council. It is a mechanical reality. And until you see the mechanism, you cannot interrupt it.

---

### How the Money Leaves

Take a restaurant in Clarksdale, Mississippi. Good food. Loyal regulars. A real business.

Now walk through the weekly expense stack.

The delivery platform takes 30% off every order placed through it — money that routes through San Francisco before it's processed. The point-of-sale system is a monthly subscription to a company headquartered in Toronto. The payroll processor is a national service with a Chicago billing address. The scheduling software, the accounting platform, the customer loyalty app, the email marketing tool — each one a small subscription, each one a wire transfer out of the region, every month, without exception.

That owner isn't buying into the extractive economy because they made a bad decision. They bought the best available options. The best available options were all built somewhere else, for customers everywhere else, with ownership structures designed to return value to shareholders who have never set foot in the Mississippi Delta.

This is the architecture of extraction. It is not malicious. It is just the way the mainstream economy was designed — to aggregate value from the periphery and concentrate it at the center.

The restaurant owner in Clarksdale is not a victim of bad luck. They are a node in a system that was engineered to drain them. Eight to twelve subscriptions. Eight to twelve monthly transfers out. Zero of those dollars compounding locally.

---

### The Pattern Repeats at Every Scale

The same mechanic operates up and down the economic ladder.

The independent contractor who books jobs through a national platform gives up 20% of every transaction. The retailer who accepts credit cards pays interchange fees to a Delaware holding company. The hotel that lists on a national booking site surrenders 15-25% of the nightly rate. The regional media company that runs national ad inventory keeps a fraction of what its audience is worth to the advertiser.

None of these are irrational choices. In isolation, each one makes sense. The platform brings customers. The payment processor handles compliance. The booking site provides reach.

But the cumulative effect is an economy that generates real value — real labor, real culture, real community — and then systematically transfers the financial return to entities that played no role in creating it.

The eighty cents doesn't disappear. It just doesn't stay.

---

### The Only Defense Is Sovereign Infrastructure

The Outsider Economics framework is not a political argument. It is an engineering problem.

If the mechanism of extraction is infrastructure — the platforms, the tools, the payment rails, the data systems that businesses depend on to operate — then the only durable defense is to build and control your own infrastructure.

**Agorism** is the first principle: the parallel economy. The juke joint that takes cash. The contractor who invoices directly. The artist who sells from their own platform instead of a streaming service that pays fractions of a cent per play. These are not romantic holdouts. They are nodes in a sovereign economy — one that functions outside the extraction machine by design.

**Circular Economy** is the second principle: keep the money moving locally. A locally-owned business that uses locally-built tools, hires from the local labor pool, and banks at a community institution compounds its revenue into the regional economy. The question after every transaction is not just whether you made money — it is where that money goes next.

The SaaS company in San Francisco doesn't care about the answer. The business owner in Natchez should.
