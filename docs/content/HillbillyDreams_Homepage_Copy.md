## Chapter 1: The Origin — It Started With a Record

We didn't set out to build a technology company. We started with a voice, a record, and a live room.

The original mission was simple: help Amy Allen — performing as Arrie Aslin — develop her music career, promote her record, and create memorable experiences for a live audience. But independent art needs a runway, and the physical venues required to fund it are usually bled dry by disconnected, expensive software subscriptions.

So we built our own infrastructure.

HDX is the armor we built to protect the music, stabilize cash flow, and buy back our time. It wasn't built to be a software company. It was built so we could stop worrying about the baseline and get back to doing the work we actually set out to do.

## Chapter 2: The Platform — One Engine. Every Industry.

HDX is a business operating system that replaces the disconnected software stack.

Sales pipeline. Billing. Project tracking. Marketing automation. Client delivery. Bank reconciliation. All of it on a single AI-powered platform running on Google infrastructure. One monthly cost. One source of truth for every number in the business.

The closest analog is Oracle NetSuite — a fully integrated back office that runs while the business runs in the foreground. The difference is that NetSuite costs $3,000–$5,000 a month before implementation and was built for companies with finance departments and IT staff. It was not built for a six-person company in Natchez, Mississippi trying to grow to twenty.

HDX was.

We deploy this engine under purpose-built names for specific industries. **S2PX** is our cash-flow baseline — HDX for 3D scanning and AEC firms. One licensing relationship, $134K/year, 82% margin. That single contract funds the entire operating baseline while everything else gets built.

We own the infrastructure. Our clients own their brand, their data, and their results.

## Chapter 3: The Showroom — We Don't Demo Software. We Show You a Running Business.

Most tech companies build in a vacuum. We are operators.

The Big Muddy ecosystem in Natchez, Mississippi is our owned-and-operated QA environment — a micromedia company in a bottle. A boutique hotel with a live music venue. A regional magazine. Big Muddy Radio. The Snowbird Touring Circuit. Big Muddy Records.

Every feature we build gets battle-tested by our staff on the ground before it ever touches a client's business. We don't ask you to trust our roadmap. We ask you to walk through the front door.

**Big Muddy Records** runs the inverted label model: artists keep 100% of their master recordings. We take a monthly retainer and a revenue share only after money is made, never before. Every split, every royalty, every sync placement tracked in real time on MelodyVault.

**The Big Muddy model is licensable.** A hospitality group in Memphis. A tourism corridor in Arkansas. A cultural destination in the Delta. Any regional operator can license the model, the platform, and optionally the brands to stand up their own version. The infrastructure is built. The playbook is proven.

## Chapter 4: The Mission — The 80% Extraction Rate Stops Here.

Right now, eighty cents of every dollar earned in the Deep South leaves within 48 hours — to pay for out-of-state tech platforms, national delivery apps, and distant shareholders. The mainstream economy is an extraction machine. It moves money from the periphery to the center, quietly and persistently.

**Outsider Economics** is our answer. Four principles that function as design constraints on every product decision we make:

- **Agorism** — Build for the parallel economy operating outside the extractive mainstream.
- **Distributed Networks** — The Deep South is not one market. Connect the nodes. Don't flatten them.
- **Anti-Fragility** — Build with and for businesses that get stronger under stress.
- **Circular Economy** — Revenue that stays in the region compounds for the region.

Through **Rise Up**, we are building sovereign infrastructure in the field. Arrie Aslin and the Rise Up Gospel and Blues Band travel the corridor, playing the underserved rooms that built American music. At every stop, the band runs a regional talent search and the economic program runs underneath it. Artists get a platform where they keep 100% of their masters. Local business owners learn to stop the drain.

We figured out how to stop the extraction in our own house. Now we are using the music to bring that infrastructure to the rest of the region.

We build local. The value stays local. We use the technology to protect the art, and we use the art to rebuild the Deep South.
