Plan — Big Muddy Records

Owner: Chase Pierson Status: Pre-revenue. Long-term play. Last updated: 2026-04-29

What it is

Big Muddy Records is the label arm of the Big Muddy ecosystem — a record label rooted in Mississippi blues and the broader corridor sound. We own bigmuddyrecordlabel.com and operate the label as a long-horizon brand asset, not a near-term revenue line.

What's working today

Almost nothing yet. Be honest about it.

  • Domain is live and routes correctly.
  • Roster page exists with Mechanical Bull and Arrie Aslin listed (Mechanical Bull = Chase's catalog, Arrie = band project).
  • "Artist platform" CTA on the records page links to bigmuddyrecordlabel.com.
  • No releases shipped under the Big Muddy Records imprint.
  • No distribution deal in place.
  • No PRO/publisher administration set up.
  • No revenue.

What we have: a name, a domain, a roster page, and three Mechanical Bull masters sitting on a hard drive waiting to be remastered.

The economics

Records is a 12–24 month gestation play. Realistic revenue forecast for the next 12 months: $0.

Year 1 cost (estimate):

  • Mastering (3 records, outside engineer): 1, 500–3,000
  • Distribution setup (DistroKid or Symphonic): 20–300
  • ISRC codes + barcodes: included with distributor
  • PRO registration (BMI/ASCAP): 0–50
  • Cover art / packaging design: in-house (Chase + Tuthill)

Total Year 1 spend: under $3,500. All of it sunk cost against future catalog value.

Year 2+ revenue model (when it starts):

  • Streaming royalties (long tail, small)
  • Sync licensing (the actual upside — film, TV, ads)
  • Physical sales at shows (vinyl/CD merch table)
  • Catalog licensing to other Big Muddy properties (Inn ambient, Radio bumpers, Touring promo)

The label is a catalog asset. We're not chasing hits. We're stockpiling masters we control forever.

The customer

Three customers, in order of priority:

  1. The Big Muddy ecosystem itself — every Big Muddy property uses Big Muddy Records music. Inn lobby, Radio rotation, Touring intros, Magazine documentaries. Internal demand is the floor.
  2. Sync buyers — music supervisors, ad agencies, indie film. Mississippi blues + Americana is undervalued and easy to license because we own everything.
  3. Listeners on streaming platforms — Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube. Slow build via playlist placement and corridor SEO.

Not chasing radio. Not chasing chart position. Catalog and sync.

The risk

Three real risks. Naming them:

  1. Distraction risk. Records eats Chase's attention without producing revenue. If it pulls focus from Touring or MBT, kill it or pause it.
  2. Sync market is crowded. Every blues label pitches sync. Differentiation is "we own the corridor narrative" — unproven thesis.
  3. Catalog value depends on someone caring. If Big Muddy as a brand never breaks out, the label is just three guys' records. The label only matters if the ecosystem matters.

Mitigation: keep spend low. Don't sign artists with advances. No physical pressing until streaming proves a song has legs.

Why it matters to the ecosystem

The label is an audience and brand multiplier, not a P&L line.

  • Touring uses it. Bands on the Big Muddy Touring roster get a label home. That's a real recruitment edge against booking agents who have nothing to offer.
  • Radio uses it. Big Muddy Radio rotation needs original masters we control to avoid licensing fees. Records supplies that.
  • Magazine uses it. Album release = editorial story, video documentary, social campaign. One record fills a month of Magazine content.
  • Inn uses it. Lobby playlist, dining room ambient, special-event playlists.
  • Brand uses it. A label says we're serious about the music. A roster page without a label looks like a booking agent. A roster page with a label looks like an institution.

Every other module gets a 2x lift from having actual records to point at. That's the whole reason Records exists — not to make money in 2026, to make the rest of the ecosystem more credible while we build it.

Decision needed

None this quarter. Plan stands.
