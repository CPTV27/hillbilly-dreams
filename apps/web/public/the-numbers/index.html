The Numbers · Where we are, where we go April 30, 2026

First we show we’re fine. Then we show we’re about to get rich.

Twelve months of revenue, line by line, with the calm baseline first and the new product layered on top. The Inn and the Photography carry the year. Studio C’s new chat-and-service product is the part that changes everything else.

By Tracy Alderson-Allen, Finance
Natchez, Mississippi

Here is the simplest version of the next twelve months. The Inn pays the bills. Photography pays them again. Touring lifts the corridor. The magazine, the records, the radio show — those are catalog assets that pay nothing this year, and that is the plan, not a problem. Studio C runs on a small monthly retainer and steady overflow work. Add it all up and we land between the first-profit anchor and the baseline anchor. We are fine. That is the whole opening chapter.

Then a second story starts on top of the first one. Studio C just locked its first commercial customer for the new chat-and-service product Chase has been building. One customer at five hundred a month is not the headline. The headline is that the product is real, the pricing is set, the marketing is queued, and a pipeline of fifteen to thirty more customers is realistic between now and April. When that line lands on top of the existing forecast, the year stops being “fine” and starts being something else.

This document walks both stories in order. Calm baseline first. Then the upside, with the math in the open.

Part TwoThe new layer

Studio C is the operating company behind a new chat-and-service product called Measurably Better Things. Customers open a chat. They ask it anything. If the chat can answer — which it usually can, because it knows their data — it answers. If it can’t, the request escalates to the Studio C team, who handles it. Same chat. Same bill. The customer doesn’t care whether the answer came from a model or a person. They care that the problem got solved.

Software answers cost cents. Human answers are the premium. The bill shows both meters running, separately, in real time. No surprise charges. Optional caps the customer sets themselves. That is the entire pricing thesis: software is cheap, humans are the premium, and we bill them differently because they are.

The first commercial customer is Scan2Plan, locked tonight at the Business tier. Five hundred dollars a month, which captures the platform and a baseline allocation of human-tokens. Anything beyond the allocation runs on the published per-minute rate, billed against the visible meter. Customers paying more get a discount on the hourly rate — the bigger the monthly commitment, the cheaper the labor itself, because we can plan team capacity against it. Annual prepay knocks twenty percent off any tier, no usage caveats.

Modules — what stacks on top

The base subscription buys the chat door. Customers add capability modules as they need them. A solo operator at $100 might add Bookkeeping for $75 and skip everything else. A scanning company at $500 might add Content Engine for $200 and Customer Support for $100. The modules are real money on top of the base — and the higher-touch modules carry meaningful margins because the labor inside them is real.

Part FourYear two, briefly

The numbers above stop at April 2027 because that is the end of the planning window. But the slope of the MBT line is the part that matters for what year two looks like. A $10k monthly run rate exiting April becomes $120k of straight-line MBT revenue across year two, and that is before any additional customer growth. If Studio C continues adding customers at the mid-scenario rate (roughly one to two per month), the run rate at the end of year two looks closer to $25k–$30k per month, which annualizes to $300k+ from MBT alone in year three.

Stack that on the existing operations — assuming the Inn and Photography lines hold flat, which they should and probably grow — and the year-three picture for the ecosystem comes in around $600k–$700k. That is the “rich” trajectory the title of this document refers to. None of it requires the existing businesses to do anything radically different. The new line is what changes the shape.

And the year-three picture has its own ceiling further out. Once MBT has a hundred customers, the math at the bottom of Part Two kicks in — one product line producing $32k of monthly recurring at a hundred customers, which is a bigger line than the Inn. From the same Studio C team that already runs the chat and the service layer for the existing operations.

“Software is cheap. Humans are the premium. We bill them differently because they are. That is the entire pricing thesis — and it is the reason year two looks the way it does.”
