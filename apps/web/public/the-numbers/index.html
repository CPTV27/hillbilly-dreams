Part TwoThe new layer, already started

Studio C is the operating company behind a new chat-and-service product called Measurably Better Things. Customers open a chat. They ask it anything. If the chat can answer — which it usually can, because it knows their data — it answers. If it can’t, the request escalates to the Studio C team, who handles it. Same chat. Same bill. The customer doesn’t care whether the answer came from a model or a person. They care that the problem got solved.

Software answers cost cents. Human answers are the premium. The bill shows both meters running, separately, in real time. No surprise charges. Optional caps the customer sets themselves. That is the entire pricing thesis: software is cheap, humans are the premium, and we bill them differently because they are.

Part FourYear two, briefly

The numbers above stop at April 2027 because that is the end of the planning window. But the slope of the MBT line is the part that matters for what year two looks like. Exit run-rate at the mid scenario is roughly $60k/mo combined — meaning the year-two opening MRR for MBT alone is around $30k/mo, which annualizes to $360k of straight-line MBT revenue across year two before any additional customer growth.

If Studio C continues adding customers at the friends-and-family rate — which by then becomes referral-driven — the run rate at the end of year two looks closer to $50k–$70k per month from MBT alone, which annualizes to $600k+ in year three from the chat-and-service line.

Stack that on the existing operations — assuming the Inn and Photography lines hold flat, which they should and probably grow — and the year-three picture for the ecosystem comes in around $900k–$1.2M. That is the “rich” trajectory the title of this document refers to. None of it requires the existing businesses to do anything radically different. The new line is what changes the shape.
