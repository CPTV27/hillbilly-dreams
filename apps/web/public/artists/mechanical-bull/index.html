The Story

The preacher's son, the gruff growl, and the catalog they built between Woodstock and the world

Chase Pierson grew up the son of a preacher who moved his family every few years, whenever the good lord told him to go. Chase kept the wandering up as an adult — Boston as an ambulance crew chief, Texas as a mechanic and tow-truck driver, Kansas as an appliance repairman — before settling in Woodstock, New York, where he gave married life a try and walked out six years later with a daughter and a notebook full of country songs.

He took those songs to the producer Adam Widoff (Lenny Kravitz, Mercury Rev, Kate Pierson, David Torn) and started recording them in the studios above the Catskills. By the time the first album was done, there was a full band: Avalon Peacock — daughter of the legendary composer Annette Peacock — on vocals and drums. David Malachowski on guitar. Chris Zaloom on pedal steel and dobro. George Quinn on bass and mandolin. J-Bird Bowman behind the kit. The keyboardist John Medeski sat in for an organ part on one of the tracks.

The first record came out in February 2007 on Woodstock MusicWorks. It was called All Hat No Cattle — also catalogued as Songs to Get Divorced To, which became the more familiar title once the music started getting picked up for film. The second record, A Million Yesterdays, dropped November 1, 2007, with a press push run by The Planetary Group out of Boston.

Within a year the catalog was getting played on a Trust AM 1278 country show in the UK, on Radio New Caledonia in the South Pacific, and in living rooms across the Hudson Valley. Within two years, four songs were licensed to a movie called Lonely Street. A year after that, an original Pierson/Widoff co-write became the end-credit song for As Good As Dead, and Chase's "Amtrack" and "Narrow Escape" landed on CMT's Can You Duet — performed by Avalon and her duet partner Ryan, Chase writing both.

Then Chase got pulled into the next thing — a media-production company called Sound + Vision Syndicate, then a series of recording and distribution platforms, then the long arc that eventually became Big Muddy Touring + the Measurably Better Things group operating out of Natchez, Mississippi in 2026. The Mechanical Bull catalog never went away. It still runs on Spotify and Apple Music. Royalties still arrive. The recordings still hold up.

This page is the archive — the receipts, the placements, the lineup, the press. The catalog is being formally re-released through Big Muddy Records in 2026 alongside Chase's newer work and the rest of the roster.

The Band

Six pieces above the Catskills, plus one keyboard legend

Mechanical Bull was a six-piece country folk band recorded and produced in the Woodstock-Saugerties studio circuit. Chase wrote almost all the songs and shared lead vocals with Avalon Peacock — whose voice the press kept calling "angelic, yet dark" against Chase's "gruff growl." Adam Widoff produced, played most of the instruments not played by other band members, and co-wrote on several tracks.

Chase Pierson
Vocals · Guitar · Songwriter
Wrote most of the catalog. BMI #1220623. Born preacher's son; spent the years before Woodstock as an ambulance crew chief in Boston, mechanic in Texas, appliance repairman in Kansas.
Avalon Peacock
Vocals · Drums
Daughter of legendary composer Annette Peacock. Has performed with George Clinton and Coheed and Cambria. The dark-edged counterpart to Chase's gruff growl.
Adam Widoff
Producer · Multi-instrumentalist · Co-writer
Has recorded and performed with Lenny Kravitz, Toshi Reagon, David Torn, Kate Pierson, Mercury Rev. When not playing music, writes angry manifestos warning about the dangers of technology. Lupot Music (BMI).
David Malachowski
Guitar
Legendary beyond Woodstock. Has played with Shania Twain, Savoy Brown, Commander Cody and his Lost Planet Airmen, Janie Fricke, Garth Hudson and the Crowmatix. Also a music reviewer for the Daily Freeman.
Chris Zaloom
Pedal Steel · Dobro · Telecaster
Local guitar legend out of Stoney Clove. Slinky steel work and bendy dobro lines defined the band's twang.
George Quinn
Bass · Guitar · Mandolin
Has played and recorded with John Herald, Vassar Clements, Bill Monroe, Jerry Douglas and many more. Founding member of the Sing Sing Bluegrass Reform band — ask his parole officer.
J-Bird Bowman
Drums · Vocals
Also occasionally charming and handsome. Sometimes credited as Jason Bowman.
John Medeski
B3 Organ · Guest, "Lukewarm Coffee"
Of Medeski Martin & Wood. Sat in for one track on A Million Yesterdays; the recording remains the standout sonic moment of that album.

International Airplay

Where the songs got heard

The Mechanical Bull catalog had a verifiable international footprint by 2010, beyond the US distribution rails (Spotify, Apple iTunes, Amazon MP3, Napster, Last.fm, Xbox Music, etc.) that CD Baby ran through:

  • United Kingdom — John Morris, Trust AM 1278 country show. Quoted Chase as "a genius" in the standard press copy.
  • New Caledonia — Julie Riso show on RFO Radio Nouvelle-Calédonie. Played "Desert Air" in September 2009, late-night country block (1:50 AM NY time, prime overnight country slot in the Pacific).
  • Switzerland — Conny & Reto Winkler invited Mechanical Bull to play in Kleingurmels in January 2010. (Chase noted at the time he was in Europe twice that year.)
Live

The Hudson Valley + NYC honky-tonk circuit, 2007 — 2010

Mechanical Bull lived on the small-room country/Americana circuit. Their NY runs included Hank's Saloon (Brooklyn — closed 2018), Rodeo Bar (Manhattan, 27th & 3rd — closed 2014), the Cutting Room, Knitting Factory. Upstate they played Marion's in Woodstock, Pig Bar in Saugerties, Rosendale Mountain Stage (sharing bills with Stoney Clove Lane), and Bearsville Theater. Two of those venues are now gone — the small-honky-tonk circuit got hollowed out in the 2010s. The catalog outlasted the rooms.
