A $165 Device Is Bringing AI to Towns the Internet Forgot

While OpenAI and Google race to build cloud-dependent AI for metro markets, a four-person startup in Natchez, Mississippi is shipping hardware that works without reliable broadband — and it costs less per month than a Netflix subscription to run.
By Sarah Chen and Marcus Williams April 5, 2026, 5:00 AM CDT · Updated April 5, 2026, 8:12 AM CDT

The device looks like nothing. A matte-black box roughly the size of a paperback novel, with a single green LED and two ports. It sits on a shelf behind the register at a barbecue joint in Yazoo City, Mississippi, next to a jar of toothpicks and a framed photo of B.B. King. It has no screen. No keyboard. No fan noise. The owner, who asked not to be named because she hasn’t told her landlord about the antenna on the roof, calls it “the little brain.”

The device is the Sovereign Pi, made by Hillbilly Dreams Inc., a company based 150 miles south in Natchez. It’s built on a Raspberry Pi 5 with 8GB of RAM, runs a local AI model, and costs $165 to manufacture. It drafts responses to Google reviews, generates social media posts, tracks business analytics, and produces marketing content — all without a constant internet connection.

In an industry spending billions on data centers and GPU clusters, the Sovereign Pi represents a different bet entirely: that the most underserved market for artificial intelligence isn’t enterprises that want to spend more on compute, but small businesses in places where the cloud doesn’t reliably reach.

“Everyone in Silicon Valley is building for people who already have gigabit fiber,” said Chase Pierson, the CEO and CTO of Hillbilly Dreams. “We’re building for people who lose internet when it rains.”

Pierson, 40, speaks with the unhurried cadence of someone who has lived in the rural South long enough to stop apologizing for it. A former media infrastructure architect who designed broadcast-to-distribution pipelines for major networks, he moved to Natchez and decided the same architecture that runs a Viacom could run a small-town economy. He incorporated Hillbilly Dreams with two equity partners — Tracy Alderson-Allen, who manages finance and hospitality operations, and Amy Allen, who runs their inn and bar — and began building.

The Broadband Problem Nobody Fixed

Mississippi is 79.3% rural by land area. In the Delta — the flat, fertile region between Memphis and Vicksburg where cotton once made millionaires and poverty never left — broadband access is a patchwork of dead zones, throttled connections, and prices that would make a Brooklyn resident wince.

The numbers tell a story of systematic abandonment. Delta counties have lost between 36% and 49% of their population since 1960. Coahoma County, home to Clarksdale, peaked at 49,361 residents in 1960; the 2020 census counted 22,124. Issaquena County, the least populated in the state, has fewer people than a Manhattan city block. These are not places where Comcast is building out fiber.

The federal government has acknowledged the gap. The NTIA’s Digital Equity Act directs states to develop plans for closing the digital divide. Mississippi has a State Digital Equity Plan. The USDA’s Rural Business Development Grants fund technology deployment in underserved areas. The Delta Regional Authority covers 255 counties across eight states, all of Mississippi among them.

But the money has mostly gone to laying cable and building towers — supply-side infrastructure. Pierson argues the demand side is the actual bottleneck.

“You can wire every county in the Delta with fiber tomorrow,” he said. “The barbershop owner still doesn’t know what to do with it. What she needs isn’t more bandwidth. She needs a tool that answers her Google reviews while she’s cutting hair.”

How the Hardware Works

The Sovereign Pi runs MBT, the company’s AI platform, locally on the device. When internet is available, it syncs — pushing drafted review responses to Google, scheduling social media posts, uploading analytics. When internet drops, the device keeps working. Drafts queue. Analytics accumulate. The green LED stays on.

“The device costs us $9.33 a month to run. That’s 67 times cheaper than running the same workload through OpenAI.” — Chase Pierson, CEO, Hillbilly Dreams Inc.

The AI cost structure is what makes the economics work. Pierson’s team built on Google Cloud Platform, where Gemini API access and $200 in monthly free credits cover the compute for the entire device fleet. The total AI cost per device: $9.33 a month. For context, running comparable workloads through OpenAI’s API would cost roughly $625 a month, according to Pierson’s internal benchmarks — a 67x difference.

The device ships two ways. Businesses subscribing to the company’s Deep South Directory — a regional business listing and marketing platform — get the hardware free. The subscription covers AI, directory listing, review management, and social media tools. Alternatively, the device sells standalone at $299, a 45% margin over the $165 cost of goods. At either price point, the company reaches breakeven within two months.

The Add-On Math

What makes Wall Street analysts who have seen the pitch deck take notice is the accessory revenue. The battery pack ($59), solar module ($49), and faraday cage ($39) are optional, but Pierson says early deployments show roughly 40% of businesses adding at least one accessory — mostly the battery, for a region where power outages are routine.

The signage network is where per-unit economics get interesting. For $199 a month, the company manages up to five digital displays at a business location — menu boards, event listings, promotional screens — all fed by the same AI that handles the business’s marketing. Additional screens cost $99 each. It turns a single $165 device into a recurring-revenue terminal.

The Federal Money

The timing may be deliberate. The Digital Equity Act, signed into law as part of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, allocated $2.75 billion to close the digital divide. States are now distributing those funds. Mississippi’s plan explicitly calls for tools that help small businesses participate in the digital economy.

The Sovereign Pi, with its offline-first design and sub-$300 price point, fits neatly into the federal definition of digital infrastructure. The Delta Regional Authority, which covers all 82 Mississippi counties and 173 more across seven adjacent states, has historically funded technology deployments that reach underserved populations.

“We didn’t design this for grant applications,” Pierson said. “We designed it for a barbershop that can’t keep its Wi-Fi on. If that also qualifies for federal digital equity funding, that’s alignment, not coincidence.”

USDA Rural Business Development Grants, which fund technology access in communities under 50,000 population, represent another channel. Nearly every town in the target region qualifies.

Four People, 120,000 Businesses

The company has four people. Pierson handles engineering and sales. Alderson-Allen runs finance. Allen manages hospitality operations at the company’s inn and bar, which double as a testbed. A fourth team member handles shows and programming for the company’s entertainment arm, which Pierson describes as a customer-acquisition engine: live music draws crowds, crowds generate business for directory members, directory members pay subscriptions.

The go-to-market is deliberately analog. Pierson drives a Sprinter van from town to town, walking into businesses and demonstrating the device. No cold calls. No email campaigns. Face to face, in towns where that still means something.

“You can’t sell AI to a catfish restaurant in Greenville over Zoom. You have to eat the catfish first.” — Chase Pierson

The target market is 100,000 to 120,000 small businesses across Mississippi, Louisiana, Alabama, Arkansas, and Tennessee. Even modest penetration — 1% to 3% in the first 18 months — would mean 1,000 to 3,600 deployed devices, each generating recurring subscription and signage revenue.

The Bigger Question

Whether a $165 box can meaningfully close the digital divide in one of America’s most economically distressed regions remains an open question. The device handles a narrow set of tasks — reviews, social media, basic analytics, signage — not the full spectrum of business computing. And offline AI, by definition, can’t access real-time data.

But the constraint may be the feature. In towns where the alternative is doing nothing — where Google reviews go unanswered for months, where the Facebook page hasn’t been updated since 2019, where the closest “digital marketing agency” is 90 miles away and charges $2,000 a month — a device that handles the basics without depending on infrastructure that doesn’t exist may be exactly right.

“We’re not competing with Salesforce,” Pierson said. “We’re competing with nothing. Our competition is a spiral notebook and a prayer.”

The green LED blinks. The drafts queue. The catfish keeps frying.
