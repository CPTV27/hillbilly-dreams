A Four-Person Team Built an AI Platform That Could Replace $800 a Month in Small-Business Tools

Hillbilly Dreams Inc. runs 14 websites from one codebase and thinks it can sell small-town businesses a single subscription to replace Yelp, Mailchimp, Hootsuite, and more. The economics are surprisingly compelling.

By Katherine M. Delaney
April 5, 2026 9:03 am ET

Natchez, Miss.—Chase Pierson was sitting on the front porch of a 19th-century inn he co-owns here when he started doing math that would bother him for months. The barbershop across the street was paying $99 a month for a Hootsuite subscription it barely used. A restaurant down the block spent $300 a year on Yelp and still couldn’t respond to its own reviews without logging in to three different platforms. A gift shop had a Mailchimp account with 43 subscribers that cost $13 a month.

“Every small business in town is paying for five or six tools they don’t understand, that don’t talk to each other, built by companies that have never heard of Natchez,” said Pierson, 41, who is the chief executive of Hillbilly Dreams Inc. “We thought: what if one system already knew the town?”

The answer is Measurably Better Things, a platform the company has spent three years building that bundles business directory listings, AI-powered review management, social-media scheduling, content creation, analytics, and a half-dozen other modules into subscriptions that range from free to $250 a month. The product ships under a consumer brand called Deep South Directory and targets businesses in towns with populations under 50,000.

Pierson isn’t a typical startup founder. He has no venture-capital backing. His company has four people and is split into equal equity stakes among three partners. The headquarters is the inn itself. The company’s other brands include a touring outfit that books bands, a regional magazine, a record label, and an internet radio station—all of which run from the same codebase as the business directory.

That last detail is the technical trick at the center of the pitch. Fourteen separate domains—including bigmuddytouring.com, bigmuddymagazine.com, bigmuddyradio.com, and deepsouthdirectory.com—all resolve to a single Next.js application deployed on Vercel. Each domain renders different content using multi-tenant routing, but they share the same database, the same AI models, and the same content pipeline.

The Nine Modules

The platform is organized into nine modules: Directory, Magazine, Radio, Records, Touring and Events, Commerce, Broadcasting, an AI Content Pipeline, and Analytics. For a paying business, the pitch is straightforward: claim your listing, and the platform handles everything else—writing social posts, drafting responses to Google reviews, scheduling email newsletters, even producing short radio spots.

What makes the system unusual is that each module feeds the others. When the touring arm books a band to play at the inn, the magazine publishes a preview, the radio station adds the artist to rotation, the directory updates event listings for nearby restaurants and bars, and the analytics module tracks whether those listings drove foot traffic. Pierson calls this the “data flywheel”: every brand feeds the same regional data supply, directory listings make the AI smarter, and the AI makes listings more valuable.

“We don’t have to guess what’s happening in Natchez,” Pierson said. “We are what’s happening in Natchez.”

The Mechanical Bull

One module has already produced measurable results. The company’s music-release system—internally nicknamed the Mechanical Bull—bundles recording, production, radio promotion, social-media campaigns, and distribution into a single pipeline. In the traditional music industry, marketing a single release can cost between $50,000 and $100,000 when accounting for radio promotion, publicity, digital advertising, and video production.

Pierson claims the Mechanical Bull reduces that to under $3,000 per release by eliminating the middlemen. The artist records at the company’s own studio, the AI generates promotional copy and social assets, the radio station handles airplay, the magazine publishes the story, and the touring arm books the shows. Each step is a module in the same system.

Music-industry consultants say the $50,000-to-$100,000 figure is accurate for a mid-tier independent release with national ambitions, but note that Pierson’s model works best for regional artists who don’t need national radio placement. “If your market is the Mississippi Delta, this makes total sense,” said Laura Chen, a music-business professor at Belmont University in Nashville. “If you’re trying to get on Spotify’s editorial playlists from Natchez, that’s a different problem.”

WiFi as a Marketing Channel

One of the more unusual features is a captive-portal system for WiFi networks. When a customer connects to WiFi at a participating business, they see a branded landing page with that business’s directory listing, current specials, and a prompt to leave a review. The idea came from RevoFi, a mesh-networking project Pierson was involved with in 2022 that explored decentralized internet access.

“Every coffee shop already has WiFi. Nobody is using that moment when the customer opens their phone,” Pierson said. “That’s a free marketing channel that exists in every business right now.”

The Two-Region Test

The company plans to prove the model works by running it in two very different markets. The first is Natchez and the surrounding Mississippi region—small towns, tourism-dependent, historically underserved by technology companies. The second is Woodstock, N.Y., and the surrounding Hudson Valley, where the company is preparing to launch a sister brand called Bearsville Creative this summer.

“If it works in Natchez and it works in Woodstock, then we know the model isn’t about geography,” Pierson said. “It’s about town size.”

The economics depend on density. Each region needs a critical mass of paying businesses before the AI models have enough local data to be genuinely useful. Pierson estimates that threshold at roughly 100 businesses per region—a number he plans to reach through door-to-door sales, starting at five businesses a day.

What Could Go Wrong

Skeptics see several problems. The platform is attempting to do what a dozen specialized companies each do individually. Yelp has spent 20 years refining business listings. Mailchimp has a decade of email-deliverability infrastructure. Hootsuite has partnerships with every major social network. Bundling all of those functions into a single product built by four people is ambitious to the point of implausibility, said David Reinhardt, a SaaS analyst at Gartner.

“The economics of bundling always look good on paper,” Reinhardt said. “The question is whether a four-person team can maintain nine modules at a quality level that businesses will actually pay for. That’s an execution problem, not a market problem.”

Pierson acknowledges the risk but argues that AI has fundamentally changed the math. “Three years ago, you needed a team of 20 to build what we have. Today, the AI writes the social posts. The AI drafts the review responses. The AI produces the radio copy. Our job isn’t to do all of that manually. Our job is to build the system that does it, and then stay close enough to the businesses to know when the system gets it wrong.”

The company’s photography-first design philosophy is another deliberate constraint. Every image on the platform is a real photograph, not an AI-generated illustration. Pierson insists this is a competitive advantage in a market that has been flooded with generic AI imagery. “People can tell the difference,” he said. “Real photos of real places. That’s it. That’s the whole design system.”

A Familiar Pattern

Pierson’s background offers some context for the ambition. Before Hillbilly Dreams, he designed a media production-and-distribution pipeline called the DeFacto Codec Market—essentially an open-source architecture for broadcast, production, analytics, and distribution. He argues the same infrastructure that runs a company like Paramount can run a small-town media economy, provided the AI handles the work that would otherwise require a staff of dozens.

At 93% projected gross margins, the unit economics are favorable if the company can reach its density targets. Software subscriptions at the $25-to-$250 range have almost no marginal cost per customer once the platform is built. The main ongoing expenses are AI inference costs—the company uses Google’s Gemini models for most generation tasks and Anthropic’s Claude for complex reasoning—and the human time required to maintain local relationships.

“The gap in these towns isn’t technology,” Pierson said. “It’s organization. Nobody has put the pieces together in a way that makes sense for a business with eight employees and no IT department. That’s what we sell.”

Whether a team of four can out-organize an entire SaaS industry remains to be seen. But the porch in Natchez has good WiFi, and the math, at least on paper, works.
