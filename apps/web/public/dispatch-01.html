Dispatch · 02 From the corridor

A note from Natchez

The river is still moving.
So are the rooms beside it.

The Mississippi corridor between Memphis and New Orleans contains one of the densest catalogs of American music ever produced inside a single geography. The blues was invented here. Soul was assembled here. Rock and roll passed through on its way to the rest of the world. The rooms where this happened are mostly still standing. The musicians who can still play it are mostly still alive. The audience that wants to hear it has never gone anywhere.

What has been missing is the connective tissue. The Nashville model — own the venue, own the label, own the route, own the press, capture the margin where it occurs — was perfected in one city but never applied to a corridor. Big Muddy is what happens when a small group of operators decides to build it.

Six suites in Natchez with a music venue inside. A Sprinter van and a route. A non-exclusive label that lets artists keep their masters. A 24/7 radio stream from the room itself. A magazine that covers every show. Coming next: a creative village on land outside town for the people who do this work for a living and have, until now, had nowhere to put down a porch.

And a sister project in development in Upstate New York. South for the winter to the Big Muddy, north for the summer to the Catskills. One coalition. Two pieces of ground. A year-round migratory route for working professionals — the kind of route the industry has never bothered to build.

This dispatch is the first time the whole thing has been in one document. The pages that follow are the route, the rooms, the people, the principles, and what's on the calendar. The work is already underway. The invitation, if you want one, is at the back.

— Big Muddy Natchez, Mississippi · Spring 2026

Dispatch · 05 The Blues Room

The Blues Room.

The room holds about thirty standing. The PA is the kind that gets out of the way.

The house band, on most nights, is Robert "Chicken" Burke and Adam Widoff. Chicken's catalog runs through Duke & The King, Toshi Reagon's BIGLovely, and a stack of George Clinton sessions. Widoff played guitar for Lenny Kravitz and made his own records under Mechanical Bull. They have been playing together for thirty years.

Friday Night Sessions are weekly. The room is hosted by Arrie Aslin. Every set is recorded straight off the board. Some of them become records.

We book the venues, drive the gear, advance the show, run the audience, and split the fair. Six things, in order:

Twenty-six venues in active rotation. Shows confirmed end-to-end without an agent in the middle.

Dispatch · 08 Big Muddy Acres

Big Muddy Acres.

A creative village on land outside Natchez. The first phase breaks ground this fall.

On the land · early site walk

At the center: the Mechanical Bull Pole Barn for the Arts. Workshop. Dance hall. Recording space. Around it: tiny houses for the people who make the work. Musicians, producers, photographers, writers, builders, growers. A garden along the south side. A water hookup at every porch. A coop you can hear from any of them.

Run as a live demonstration of the principles in Outsider Economics. Coordination, community, federation — applied to a piece of ground. Founding-circle membership is small and closes after a finite number of houses are built; everyone else who wants in comes through the guest program after Phase 1 opens.

And not only here. Big Muddy Acres is the southern half of a year-round migratory route for the working artist: south for the winter to the Big Muddy, north for the summer to a sister project currently in development in Upstate New York. One membership. Two pieces of ground. Follow the sun. Bypass the standard industry extraction.

Dispatch · 09 The Operating Manual

Outsider Economics.

Six principles. One book. Every decision the ecosystem makes runs through them.

01

Community Is the Currency

Trust transacts faster than money. Build the relationships first; everything else priced afterward.

02

The Extraction Trap

Watch what leaves a place and where it goes. The leak, not the income, is the variable that matters.

03

The Coordination Premium

Working together is the cheapest leverage. Most economic gain inside a small place is unrealized coordination.

04

Time as Currency

Hours are what you actually have. Spend them as if the rate were public.

05

Federation over Scale

Many small things, loosely coordinated, beat one big thing trying to be all of them.

06

The Task Board

Make the work visible. Anything that can't fit on the board doesn't get done.

Dispatch · 10 The Coalition

Who builds this.

This is not a startup. It is a coalition of working professionals who decided to point their careers at one corridor at the same time.

Musicians and producers. Innkeepers and photographers. Writers, builders, growers, radio programmers, a sound engineer, a cook. People with thirty-year track records who run their own books and bring their own gear.

Tracy Alderson-Allen runs the Inn and the books. Amy Allen runs hospitality and the bar. Chase Pierson runs the systems and the shows. The rest of the room is the people listed above, mostly without names on the page, doing the work.

The portraits above are a sampling. The full coalition is roughly two dozen working professionals across the corridor and the Hudson Valley. Names appear in features on a per-show, per-record basis — never on a roster page.
