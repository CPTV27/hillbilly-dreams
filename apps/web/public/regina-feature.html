The Biscuit Queen

Regina Charboneau has been cooking for fifty years. The biscuits are still the thing people drive across the state for.

Regina Charboneau doesn't need an introduction in Natchez. She is Natchez food. But if you're coming from somewhere else — from the river, from the highway, from one of those travel shows that keep finding her — here's the short version.

She cooked in the Alaska bush before she cooked anywhere else. Then Paris. Then San Francisco, where she opened Regina's at the Regis in 1985 and built a blues club called Biscuits & Blues in 1995 that won multiple W.C. Handy awards for Best Blues Club in America. Classic Southern cuisine and live music nightly — the formula that every restaurant in the corridor is still trying to replicate.

In 2001, she came home. Natchez. Bought Twin Oaks, a historic home that became a bed and breakfast. Restored King's Tavern — the oldest building in the Natchez territory, built before Mississippi was a state. Became culinary director of the American Queen riverboat. Wrote four cookbooks. Got named the Biscuit Queen by the New York Times, the Travel Channel, and the Hallmark Channel.

"She came home to Natchez in 2001 to raise her sons, and she never left. The corridor starts at her table."

The Cooking School

In 2019, she opened Regina's Kitchen — a cooking school in downtown Natchez. The classes are casual, fun, and the kind of thing tourists plan their trips around. Biscuits, obviously. But also the full range of Mississippi River cooking — the cuisine that travels from New Orleans to Memphis, picking up flavors at every stop.

She'd been doing versions of this for fifteen years, hosting cooking classes at Twin Oaks for passengers on the American Queen riverboat. The cooking school made it permanent. A place in town where the food tradition gets passed on, one class at a time.

The Books

Four cookbooks. The most notable: Mississippi Current Cookbook: A Culinary Journey Down America's Greatest River (Lyons Press, 2014). It's exactly what it sounds like — a cookbook that follows the river from source to mouth, collecting recipes and stories at every bend. The kind of book that belongs on the same shelf as the Big Muddy Magazine.

Her newest, Six Courses, is about Southern entertaining — recipes, stories, and the wisdom that comes from fifty years of feeding people for a living. She's working on a fifth.

Biscuits & Blues

Her brother Peter Trosclair now runs Biscuits and Blues in downtown Natchez. The name traveled from San Francisco back to Mississippi. Hot biscuits, cool blues, and the jukebox loaded with Elmore James and Howlin' Wolf. It opens at six and keeps the pot hot until the lunch crowd thins.

The biscuit sandwich with country ham and pepper jelly has become its own pilgrimage. Through-travelers on Highway 61 plan their stops around it. That's not marketing — that's fifty years of getting the butter temperature right.

Regina Charboneau is the kind of person the Deep South Directory exists for. She doesn't need more followers — she needs the tourists who are already coming to Natchez to find her before they find the chains. That's the job. Put the real thing in front of the people who are looking for it.
