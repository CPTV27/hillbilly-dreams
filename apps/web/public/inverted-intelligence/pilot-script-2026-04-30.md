### 2. THE NAME, THE PREMISE

This is Inverted Intelligence. I'm Chase Pierson.

Here's what we mean by inverted intelligence.

For about two hundred years, the high-margin work was the thinking work. Analysis. Writing. Judgment. Design. The stuff humans did with their heads. Manual labor was cheap because we'd built machines to do most of it. Software made it cheaper still. The premium was on the mind.

That's flipped. That's what flipped — past tense. It happened in the last eighteen months and most of the country hasn't noticed yet because nobody changed the price tags.

The thinking work is the thing AI does for cents now. The premium moved to the things AI cannot do. Showing up. Putting your name on something. Holding a room. Making a call that somebody — usually you — has to live with afterward.

That's the inversion. That's the whole show. We're going to talk about it for a while.

---

### 3. THE THESIS, FULLY

Let me say it the long way.

Five years ago, if my contractor friend at the bar wanted a competitive analysis on a hospital bid, he'd hire a consultant. The consultant would charge him two hundred dollars an hour, do about six hours of work, and hand him a deck. Twelve hundred dollars. Reasonable. That was the market.

Today the contractor opens ChatGPT — twenty bucks a month, less than his cell phone bill — and the chatbot writes him a competitive analysis in ninety seconds. Better English than most consultants. Footnotes. Tables. The whole bit.

So what's that consultant worth now?

[pause]

Nothing — if all the consultant was selling was the deck. The deck is free now. Anybody can make the deck. My nine-year-old can make the deck.

But the consultant who actually shows up at the hospital, walks the loading dock with the facilities guy, asks the chief of operations who really signs the check, and then sits across from my contractor friend on a Friday afternoon and says "here's how I'd bid this, and here's the number I'd hold on" — that consultant is worth more than they used to be. Not less. More. Because the comparison has flipped. The deck is now the floor. The human in the room is the ceiling.

That's the inversion. Software is cheap. Humans are the premium. We bill them differently because they are.

Almost nobody has updated their pricing. Consultants still bill the deck. Agencies still bill the deck plus a markup on the deck. Software companies still sell you a seat license and call it a day. They priced it the old way and the world quietly turned over underneath them.

We started billing it the new way about a year ago and it works. That's the part I want to spend the rest of the episode on, because it's not theory. It's running.

---

### 4. WHY THIS MATTERS NOW

Look at the price of the major chatbots. ChatGPT is twenty dollars a month. Claude is twenty dollars a month. Gemini Advanced is twenty dollars a month. They all cost the same as a Netflix subscription. They all do work that, five years ago, you'd have paid a junior associate forty thousand dollars a year to do badly and be late on.

Twenty bucks a month. The cognitive labor floor.

Now ask the question nobody in any boardroom is asking out loud: if the floor is twenty bucks, what does the ceiling look like?

The ceiling is the human. Specifically, the human who can do the thing the chatbot can't. Walk into a room. Take responsibility for an outcome. Make a judgment somebody has to live with. Be in a place. Look you in the eye.

That work used to compete with the chatbot's work, and it lost on price. Now it doesn't compete with the chatbot's work. It's a different market entirely. And that market — the room market, the showing-up market, the somebody-has-to-decide-this market — has no price ceiling at all because nobody's set one yet.

We set one. Down here. In a small town in Mississippi. With a chatbot we run for our own customers, and a team behind it that picks up the phone when the chatbot can't.

### 5. THE PROOF — BIG MUDDY AND STUDIO C

Here's how it actually works on our end.

We run an inn in Natchez. Big Muddy Inn. Tracy and Amy run it. We have guests. The guests have questions. What time is breakfast. Can you book me a tour. The room's too cold. There's a better way to spend Saturday than the one your website suggested — what is it.

Most of those questions, our chatbot can answer. The chatbot has access to the booking system, the room data, the menu, the local calendar. Cents per answer. Twenty-four seven. Never sick. Never bored.

A handful of those questions, the chatbot can't answer. The guest needs to talk to a person. The reservation got messed up. The plumber needs to come back. They want to plan a wedding. They want to tell us a story. The chat hits a wall and a human picks up.

Same chat. Two meters. The customer sees both numbers running in real time. Software answers cost cents. Human answers cost more — multiples more — because they should. Pay for the answer, not the path.

Nobody gets a surprise bill. Nobody waits on hold. Nobody talks to four different departments. There's a chat. There's a meter. The bill at the end of the month matches the meter. Everybody knows what they're paying for.

We tier it. Free if you just want to mess around. Twenty dollars a month if you want a real router across thirty-some specialist agents — that's the price ChatGPT charges for one model, and we give you the whole switchboard. A hundred bucks a month if you want a human in the loop when the chat hits the wall — half an hour of a real person, included, every month, no negotiation. Two hundred and up if you need a small team behind you.

The hundred-dollar tier is the one. Most people don't need a full-time agency and most people definitely don't need to babysit a chatbot. They need a chatbot that works AND a human they can text when it doesn't. That's a thing nobody else sells at any consumer price. And it's the thing every small operator I've ever met actually needs.

The team behind all of it is called Studio C. They're the people who answer when the chat can't. They're the operating company. The chat is the door. Studio C is the room behind the door.

That's the product. That's how we're betting on the inversion. We didn't write a white paper about it. We built it. We're running it. The Big Muddy Inn runs on it. Studio C is staffing up against it. Every customer we sign past the inn is another data point that says yeah, this is the right shape.

### 6. THE SHOW GOING FORWARD

This show, going forward — Inverted Intelligence — is about the people living that math.

Some weeks it's a monologue like this. I'll see something on the road or behind the bar that fits the thesis and I'll talk about it for ten minutes.

Some weeks it's a conversation. We're going to bring in operators who are noticing the same thing from their own corner. Producers, contractors, real estate brokers, the people running small institutions, the people running themselves as small institutions.

Episode 1, next week, is Robert Burke. Everybody calls him Chicken. He's a producer. He was about to sign a record deal with us — Big Muddy Records — for fifty thousand dollars cash. Standard deal. The kind of deal labels have done since labels existed.

We didn't do that deal. We made him a different one. We told him: instead of fifty thousand dollars in cash that's gone in a year, we'll value the record at fifty thousand and you take a tiny house. On a piece of land we're putting together east of town. You own a piece of property. We own the record. You're the first resident.

He took it.

That's Episode 1. Why he took it, what it means for how the music business works in 2026, and why I think this is the first deal of about a hundred we're going to do — and the cleanest possible example of what inverted intelligence looks like when the math actually changes hands.

Some weeks after that, it'll be a field report. Tracy at the inn. Amy behind the bar. Whoever's in the studio that week. Whoever's on the road. The point is the show is the work. The show is people doing the work. Not commentary on the work. Not predictions about the work. The work itself, narrated by the people doing it.
