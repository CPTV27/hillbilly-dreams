### COLD OPEN

[INTRO MUSIC: short, warm, acoustic-leaning, ~10 sec — fades under]

[pause]

In nineteen ninety-something — I was twenty-two years old, working in the basement of an ambulance station in Boston as a crew chief. I had a Tascam four-track recorder I'd bought with my second paycheck and I was making little country songs at three in the morning between calls. I knew, even then, that the songs weren't going to make me any real money. The math didn't work. Studios cost money. Pressing CDs cost money. Distribution cost money. The labels made all of it back and the artist made about nothing. Everybody knew this. It was the price of admission.

[pause]

What I didn't know yet — what nobody knew yet — was that within fifteen years all of those costs would fall down a hole. The studio became a laptop. The CD pressing became a Spotify upload. The distribution became a permanent URL on cdbaby dot com slash cd slash mechanicalbull. I have one. It still works. People still buy songs through it for a dollar and the royalty check shows up in the mail.

[pause]

But here's the thing the math nobody pointed out at the time. The same fifteen years that destroyed the cost of releasing a record also destroyed the cost of *looking like* a real band — and that turned out to matter way more than anyone expected. When the cost of production goes to zero, what becomes valuable is the part the production can't fake. Showing up at a small honky-tonk in Brooklyn on a Sunday night and playing well. Holding a room. Being the person whose name is on the poster. The thing that doesn't scale.

That's the show. That's what this whole thing is going to be about. We're going to take twelve minutes a week and look at what happens when the price of doing the thing falls to nothing — and what stays valuable on the other side.

### THE NAME

This is Inverted Intelligence. I'm Chase Pierson.

The name comes from a thing I noticed about three years ago and have been chasing ever since. For about two hundred years, the high-margin work in any economy was the work humans did with their heads. Analysis, writing, judgment, design. We built machines to do the manual work cheap. We built software to make the cognitive work *cheaper*. And the premium kept moving up the cognitive chain — until the chatbots arrived and the chain ran out of links. The premium had nowhere left to go.

Or rather — it had one place left to go. Down. Out of the head, back into the body. Back to the room. Back to the person.

That's the inversion. That's the whole show. We're going to take a lot of episodes to look at it from a lot of angles. But that's the one sentence you need.

### MY ARC, BRIEFLY, BECAUSE IT MATTERS

A little about me, since I'm going to be your narrator. I grew up the son of a preacher who moved his family every few years, whenever the good lord told him to go. So I learned early that you don't get rooted by waiting — you get rooted by showing up wherever you happen to be. I kept the moving going as an adult. Boston as an ambulance crew chief. Texas as a mechanic and a tow truck driver. Kansas as an appliance repairman. I finally landed in Woodstock, New York, gave married life a try, walked out six years later with a daughter and a notebook full of country songs.

I made a band called Mechanical Bull. We put out two records on a tiny label called Woodstock MusicWorks. We got reviewed by the Daily News in McKeesport Pennsylvania, four out of four stars. We got airplay on Trust AM twelve seventy-eight in the UK. Radio New Caledonia played us in the South Pacific at one in the morning Sunday — the perfect hour for country music, the DJ said. Four of our songs got licensed for a movie called Lonely Street. One song, Eye for an Eye, was the end-credit song for a movie called As Good As Dead. Two songs landed Avalon Peacock in the finals of a CMT show called Can You Duet. I wrote them. She sang them.

None of this made us rich. None of it was supposed to. The point was: we did real work, in real rooms, in real towns, and the receipts add up to something even though no individual receipt is much.

That whole time, I was also working in television. I did almost thirty years of live video and live TV. I was an executive producer on a show called Hollywood Today Live. I built a video studio in Bearsville called Studio C. I have spent my entire adult life watching the cost of *production capability* fall through the floor — from the million-dollar broadcast suite to the twenty-dollar-a-month chatbot — and the entire time I have been quietly noticing that the people who survived the falls were the people who were good at being in the room.

### 2008

I want to tell you about a memo I wrote in two thousand and eight, because it's relevant.

I was working with a manager named Maria Infantine in lower Manhattan and a couple of producers named Mike and Abe out at Crowned Kings Productions. We had this list of business ideas we were prototyping. A Ringo Starr music series we were trying to get American Express to sponsor. Reality TV concepts for CMT and VH One — small-town stories, the kind of thing that didn't exist yet but was about to. A concert series we wanted to build around the Felice Brothers, who were touring Europe at the time and unreachable. A viral marketing campaign for a song I'd written called Marriage is Gay, which we were going to push through the two thousand and eight election cycle. We wanted to build a website, hold parties in major cities, get the local LGBT organizations to throw them. We were going to make t-shirts. We were going to make buttons. We had a JibJab-style video idea.

None of these things happened. The economy collapsed in the fall of two thousand and eight and most of those plans evaporated. But what's interesting in retrospect is what we were *actually doing*, which is: prototyping the business models for an internet that didn't quite exist yet. We were trying to monetize human attention by gathering it into rooms and putting brand sponsors on the rooms. We were trying to use small shows to build big audiences. We were trying to attach political songs to political moments and let the songs do the marketing.

We were early. We were also right. Most of those models showed up five or ten years later, executed by other people, who got paid handsomely.

I tell you this because the people who get paid for the inversion that's happening right now will probably also be people who were early and right and didn't get paid for it the first time. I am one of them. My friends in the music business are some of them. And the show is going to be about meeting them, on the air, and figuring out together what worked the second time and what didn't.

### THE THESIS, NOW THAT YOU KNOW WHO IS SPEAKING

So here is the thesis, plainly.

Software answers cost cents. Human answers cost more. Multiples more, if you price them honestly. Almost nobody is pricing them honestly. Consultants still bill for the deck the chatbot now writes for free. Agencies still bill for the deck plus a markup on the deck. Software companies still sell you a seat license and call it a day. Everyone priced things the old way and the world quietly turned over underneath them.

We started billing for it the new way about a year ago and it works. Software answers cost cents. Human answers cost twenty-five dollars. Same chat. Two meters. The customer sees both numbers running in real time and pays for what they actually used.

The center of gravity for the new pricing is — get this — a hundred dollars a month. Not twenty. Not a thousand. A hundred. A hundred buys you a working chatbot AND thirty minutes of a real person every month. Nobody else sells that, at any consumer price. And every small operator I have ever met actually needs it.

That's the product. We run it for the Big Muddy Inn in Natchez Mississippi, which we own. We run it for our touring company. We run it for a couple of outside customers who started signing this month. The team behind it is called Studio C — same name as the video studio I built in Bearsville, just transplanted to the Mississippi corridor. The chat is the door. Studio C is the room behind the door.

That's how the inversion looks when you actually run it.

### THE SHOW, GOING FORWARD

This show is about the people running it.

Some weeks I'll just talk for ten minutes about a thing I noticed at the bar at the Inn or on the road or in a recording session. Some weeks we'll have a guest — somebody operating in the same shape, who's noticed the same thing.

Episode One, next Wednesday, is Robert Burke. Everybody calls him Chicken. He's a producer. He was about to sign a record deal with us — Big Muddy Records — for fifty thousand dollars cash. Standard deal. The kind of deal labels have done since labels existed.

We didn't do that deal. We made him a different one. We told him: instead of fifty thousand dollars in cash that's gone in a year, we'll value the record at fifty thousand and you take a tiny house. On a piece of land we're putting together east of town. You own a piece of property. We own the record. You're the first resident.

He took it.

That's Episode One. Why he took it. What it means for how the music business works in twenty twenty-six. And why I think it's the first deal of about a hundred we're going to do — and the cleanest possible example of what inverted intelligence looks like when the math actually changes hands.
