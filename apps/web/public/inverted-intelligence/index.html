What Inverted Intelligence Means

April 30, 2026 ~12 min 1,800 words v2 — locked draft

The pilot. Thirty years of live TV. The Mechanical Bull era. The 2008 Sound + Vision Syndicate prototypes that nobody asked for. The Hudson Valley honky-tonk circuit. And the moment in Natchez when the inversion finally had a name worth printing.

For about two hundred years, the high-margin work in any economy was the work humans did with their heads — analysis, writing, judgment, design. We built machines to do the manual work cheap, then software to make the cognitive work cheaper. The premium kept moving up the cognitive chain. Until the chatbots arrived and the chain ran out of links.

This is the show that takes twelve minutes a week to look at what happens when the price of doing the thing falls to nothing — and what stays valuable on the other side.

About the show

Inverted Intelligence is a weekly long-form essay recorded at Big Muddy in Natchez, Mississippi. Twelve to fifteen minutes per episode. Written and read by Chase Pierson — sometimes solo, sometimes with rotating guests: operators, founders, researchers, and people seeing the inversion in their own work.

The premise: when AI gets cheap and good at the cognitive work, the premium inverts. Showing up, holding accountability, making the judgment call, picking up the phone — those become the high-margin layer. The show is about what that looks like in practice.

It also doubles as the marketing engine for Measurably Better Things, the platform that prices software and human work on the same screen, with two meters.
