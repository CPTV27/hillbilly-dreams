Inverted Intelligence.

The narrative through-line for everything Measurably Better Things does — product, pricing, marketing, programming.

Concept locked 2026-04-30 · Chase Pierson

The thesis in one sentence

As AI gets better at the work that used to make humans valuable, the value of human work inverts: the things only humans can do — show up, take accountability, hold a room, make a moral call, be in a place — become the premium layer. We priced this correctly first.

The longer version

For two hundred years, "intelligence" meant the cognitive work humans did better than machines: analysis, writing, judgment, design. That was the high-margin work. Manual labor was cheap because machines — and later, software — commoditized it. The premium was on the mind.

Now the inversion: AI handles cognitive work for cents per call. The premium moves to the things AI can't do — being physically present, being accountable to a face, holding institutional knowledge inside a relationship, making a judgment call that someone has to live with. Human-in-the-loop becomes the luxury good. Pure software becomes the floor.

Every business model that priced "human time" at fifty to two hundred dollars an hour against "software" at twenty dollars a month seat licenses had it backward. The right pricing in the inverted regime: software cheap, humans expensive, and the same interface for both.

"Software is cheap. Humans are the premium. We bill them differently because they are."

How MBT lives this thesis

The token model is the inverted intelligence model made operational. Customers pay software-token rates for the AI work — a fraction of a cent per call. They pay human-token rates — multiples higher — for the human work. Same chat, two meters. The customer sees the price ratio in real time and learns what they're paying for.

This is not theory. It's already built. The chat MVP is live. The Studio C operating company runs the human side. The tier structure — Free, $20 Standard, $100 Service, $200 Operator, $500 Business, Enterprise — maps to how much human exposure each customer needs.

We didn't follow the trend. We saw it and priced for it.

Where the thesis shows up

In product marketing

Every customer-facing surface gets a one-line hook tied to the thesis.

Three taglines we run consistently
  • Pricing page: Software is cheap. Humans are the premium. We bill them differently because they are.
  • Onboarding chat opener: I can answer most things. The hard stuff goes to a human. Either way, you pay for the answer, not the path.
  • Tier-upgrade prompt: You've used the chat for X. The $100 tier connects you to a human when the chat hits a wall. That's where the real value lives.

In the radio show

Big Muddy Radio runs a weekly program titled Inverted Intelligence. The show is the marketing — not the other way around. Listeners self-select into the thesis, then convert.

Format is not a single shape. It rotates across four:

Four formats, rotating
  • 30-min weekly essay — Chase plus a guest, recorded conversation, lightly edited
  • Long-form interview — operators, founders, researchers seeing the inversion in their own work
  • Field reports — Tracy and Amy and others reporting from the inn, the bar, the road on what humans actually do that AI doesn't
  • Listener questions — chat customers ask, the show answers

In the pilot rollout

The thesis gets tested in two markets first.

Pilot plan
  • Natchez (Big Muddy ecosystem) — Tracy, Amy, Inn guests, Big Muddy Touring artists, Magazine readers. Soft launch the chat plus human-token pricing here first.
  • Woodstock (Bearsville network) — same offering, Hudson Valley creative class. Different customer profile, same product.
  • Tier coverage — push customers across all tiers ($0 Free, $20 Standard, $100 Service, $200+ Operator). Learn which tier each cohort settles into.
  • Iterate on the thesis — track which language (in the chat, in the radio show, in the marketing) lands. Refine the message.
  • Go wide once cohort metrics confirm — $100 conversion rate from Standard exceeds threshold, Service customers stay over six months, the radio show pulls reliable downloads. Then scale.

Why the timing matters

The trend is visible to anyone paying attention but few have priced for it. Frontier model labs are still selling per-seat or per-token-with-no-human-attached. Consulting firms still bill billable hours without an AI layer. Agencies are bolting AI onto their existing twenty-thousand-dollar retainers without restructuring the price.

We're early. Not because we're smart — because we built it from the customer side first. Big Muddy Inn needed to actually run on this stack, then we noticed the model was generalizable. The marketing window is now: be the people who name the trend, not the people who explain it three years later.
