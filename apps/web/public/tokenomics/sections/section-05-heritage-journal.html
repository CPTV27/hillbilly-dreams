On the True Cost of an Answer

A short essay on why the price of thinking, like the price of paper, depends on whose hand is holding the pen.

There is a certain category of confusion that arises whenever a new technology is asked to settle into the older grammar of commerce. The latest such confusion concerns the language model, which charges fractions of a cent for what feels, to the person typing the question, like the work of a small university. The arithmetic is real. A page of generated text, produced by software, costs less than a postage stamp once did. The mind reels, briefly, then carries on.

What gets lost in the cheapness of the machine is the thing we used to call expertise. A photographer who has spent twenty years learning where to stand at sunrise still costs what twenty years cost. A lawyer who knows which sentence in your contract will sink you still bills for the knowing. A doctor still studies, a chef still tastes, a builder still measures twice. None of this has gotten less expensive. If anything, it has gotten more so, because the rest of the world got faster around it.

*   *   *

The arrangement we propose, then, is a quiet one. Software tokens are billed at software prices, which is to say almost nothing. Human tokens are billed at human prices, which is to say what the work is worth. Both move through the same chat window, which is the only window most people will ever look through, and the bill arrives later, itemized for those who care to read it.

“You are paying for the answer. The path the answer took is our concern, not yours.”

This sounds simple because it is simple. The complication has always belonged to the seller, never the reader. A magazine does not ask you to price the typesetter, the photographer, the editor, the paper, and the truck that carried the issue to the newsstand. It asks you for the cover price. What happens behind the masthead is a matter of craft, and craft is what you trust the masthead for.

*   *   *

We have built the chat in this spirit. Ask a question and the system decides, in the small space of a heartbeat, whether the answer is a software answer or a human one. If it is software, the price is the price of running the software, and we mark it up the way any honest shop marks up its inventory. If it is human, a person you would otherwise have to find, vet, and schedule does the work, and the price reflects that person’s afternoon.

The reader pays for the answer. The author keeps a careful ledger. Both parties leave the transaction whole, which is, in the end, the only test of a fair price.
