What MBT is

Measurably Better Things — MBT — is the platform. It's a chat-first AI product that customers sign up for. They upload their business data, and they get a chat that knows their business and routes between models, agents, and humans. Software answers what software can answer cheaply. When the request needs a human, the chat escalates to a person who finishes it. Two meters: software tokens at a thin margin over the model cost, human tokens at a much higher rate. Same chat, two prices, total transparency.

I built it. I run it. I keep building it. The technology, the agents, the billing infrastructure, the chat surface — all of that is on me. MBT is the product Big Muddy and our other customers are paying for.

What Studio C is

Studio C is the production arm. When the chat escalates, somebody has to actually do the work — edit the video, write the email, build the deck, deliver the thing. That's Studio C. You also handle the administrative work that surrounds production: intake from the chat, scheduling, vendor coordination, QA, delivery back to the customer. The pipeline that turns a request into a deliverable.

Two important things to be clear about. One: Studio C is not responsible for running MBT. The chat working, the agents working, the platform staying up — that's me. You don't carry that weight. Two: Studio C's responsibility is production work and the admin of the production pipeline. That's the scope. Inside that scope, you own it; outside that scope, you don't.

"MBT is the platform that enables Studio C's production sales. Studio C is the production arm that makes those sales real."

How they connect

A customer signs up for MBT. They get a chat. They ask the chat for something. If the request is one the AI can finish — drafting an email, summarizing a document, looking something up — the chat finishes it and meters software tokens. If the request requires production — a video edit, a brand deliverable, a real piece of work — the chat hands it off into the Studio C pipeline. Studio C delivers. The chat reports the result back to the customer and meters human tokens for the production time.

Without MBT, Studio C is just another production shop competing on price. With MBT, Studio C sells through a chat-first interface with software margins on the AI side and human margins on the production side. Same screen, two meters. That's the leverage.

Current customers

Real customers, real money, today
  • Big Muddy Touring — internal anchor customer. The Inn, the magazine, the radio station, the touring arm. We run our own ops on MBT every day. This is the proof case.
  • Utopia — first external client. Production engagement live.
  • Two new clients — just brought on. $500/month engagements.
  • Scan2Plan — proposed engagement, not yet closed. Production for marketing + administration of the platform I built for them.

The model we're selling is a $500/month base engagement — call it ten hours of human-token work at fifty an hour, plus access to the chat, plus the platform infrastructure. Anything beyond the ten hours bills hourly through the human-token meter. That's the standard offer for the customers we're talking to right now. There are smaller and larger tiers above and below that, but the $500 retainer is what's working.

The billing model

The way the meter works: when a customer interacts with the chat, every interaction has a cost. Software-generated answers cost software tokens — a fraction of a cent per call, in line with what the underlying model costs us, with a thin margin. Human-generated work costs human tokens — priced by the minute, at a rate that reflects what skilled production time is actually worth. Five dollars a minute for ops-grade work; ten or more for specialist work. The customer sees both meters at the bottom of the chat. No hidden bills. No surprise invoices. Pause anytime.

The reason this works is that it's honest about what costs what. Software is cheap. Humans are expensive. Most software companies bury that. We surface it. Customers actually like it because it explains what they're paying for.

For Studio C: the human-token meter is your revenue line. Every minute you log against a customer request is a minute that bills. Time tracking has to be honest from day one or the model breaks.

Entity status

Studio C has not been incorporated yet. To date, the work has been billed through Tuthill Design (Tracy + me). New customers we bring on under the Studio C banner will route through a new entity we form — most likely a New York corporation. The new entity gives us clean billing, a clean cap table, and the ability to bring on partners properly. Until that's filed, treat Studio C as a service line operating under Tuthill Design.

The incorporation is on the near-term list. Not tonight's decision, but it's coming.

Three founders: me, Elijah, Miles. Beyond that, the structure is an open question. One scenario we've talked about: five shares at twenty percent each. Three shares to the three of us. Two shares reserved — held back for future market partners as we expand. That gives us room to bring on, say, a Natchez operator down the road who plays the role you two are playing in the Northeast. It's a working scenario, not a decision. Tonight is a good place to start the conversation about what feels right.
