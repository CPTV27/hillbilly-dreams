Big Muddy / Studio C · 2026

The owner of a small inn types a question into a chat box at 11:42 on a Tuesday night. Two guests are arriving Friday with a complication. She asks, plain English, what to do. Three seconds later the answer comes back. In the corner of the screen, a small number ticks up by less than a penny.

That penny is the whole story.

The chat is reading her booking history, her room calendar, her past notes on the same kind of guest. A model answers because the question fits inside what a model does well. Software tokens. Cheap. The meter barely moves.

The next morning she asks a harder one. A vendor is double-billing her. She wants it fixed, not explained. The chat tries, hits a wall, hands the thread to a person on the Studio C team. They make two calls, send one email, close the loop by lunch. The meter ticks again, this time noticeably. Human tokens. Premium rate.

Software tokens (today) $0.04 Human tokens (today) $38.00

Both numbers sit on the same screen. She can see exactly what she paid for the easy answer and exactly what she paid for the hard one. No invoice arrives later with a surprise on it. The bill shows up while the work is happening.

Same chat, two meters. The customer pays for the answer, not the path.

This is the trade we think is fair. A model wrote the first reply. A person resolved the second. She didn't have to know which was which to ask the question. She didn't have to negotiate two contracts to get help. Software is cheap because software is cheap. Humans cost more because humans showing up and taking accountability is, increasingly, the rare thing.

More complicated questions burn more tokens, on either side of the line. The price scales with what the work actually took. That's it. That's the model.

The tiers, in one paragraph

Customers buy a base subscription that includes a monthly allotment of both software tokens and human tokens. If they use less than the allotment, they keep what they didn't use. If they use more, the meter just keeps running at published rates — no surprise invoice, no overage trap, an option to cap usage at any number they pick.

Why this matters

For about two hundred years the high-margin work was the cognitive work. Analysis, writing, judgment, design. Software made it cheaper, year over year. Then the chatbots arrived and the cognitive labor floor fell to twenty dollars a month.

The premium had to go somewhere. It went down the chain — out of the head, back to the room. To showing up. To holding accountability. To the person who actually answers the phone.

Almost nobody has updated their pricing for this yet. Consultants still bill for the deck the chatbot now writes for free. Agencies still bill for the deck plus a markup. Software companies still sell you a seat license and call it a day.

We bill it the new way. The math sells itself.
