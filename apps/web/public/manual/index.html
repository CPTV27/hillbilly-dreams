This is the manual for how Measurably Better Things — MBT — and the surrounding ecosystem work. 2. The thesis — Inverted Intelligence

For two hundred years, "intelligence" meant the cognitive work humans did better than machines: analysis, writing, judgment, design. That was the high-margin work. Manual labor was cheap because machines, and later software, commoditized it. The premium was on the mind.

Now the inversion. AI handles cognitive work for cents per call. The premium moves to the things AI can't do — being physically present, being accountable to a face, holding institutional knowledge inside a relationship, making a judgment call that someone has to live with. Human-in-the-loop becomes the luxury good. Pure software becomes the floor.

Most software companies haven't priced for this yet. They still charge twenty dollars a month for software and fifty to two hundred dollars an hour for humans. We priced for the inversion first: software cheap, humans expensive, same interface for both. That's the whole bet.

Read the full concept doc at /inverted-intelligence. The radio show by the same name is one expression of the thesis; the pricing model is another; the way Studio C is structured is a third. Everything ties back here.

"Software is cheap. Humans are the premium. We bill them differently because they are."

The technology platform. The chat, the agents, the billing meters, the multi-tenant infrastructure, the AI router, the brand kits, the production ops module. This is the product customers buy. Built and operated by Chase. Legal entity Measurably Better Things LLC, Mississippi (formation in progress as of 2026-04-24).

The flywheel: HDI holds the strategy. MBT builds the platform. Implementations prove the platform works and generate revenue. Revenue funds further platform development. Platform improvements lift every implementation.

4. The product — chat, tokens, modules

MBT is a chat-first AI platform with a hybrid SaaS-plus-service business model. A customer signs up, uploads their business data, and gets a chat that knows their business. The chat answers what software can answer. When it can't, it escalates to a human on our team — typically Studio C — who finishes the work.

Two meters at the bottom of every conversation. Software tokens at a thin margin over the underlying model cost — a fraction of a cent per call. Human tokens at the rate of skilled production time — five dollars a minute for ops-grade work, ten or more for specialist work. Customers see both meters. Customers pay for both. No surprise bills.

Six tiers, designed to span Free → Enterprise. Add modules à la carte for specific capabilities (lead gen, video production, podcast editing, social management). Add interface options for where the chat lives (MBT web, terminal CLI, Apple Notes, ClickUp, Notion, Slack, custom-built UI billed in tokens). The full module + interface menu is at /mbt/modules.

Tokenomics in five minutes: /explain. Detailed product definition: docs/positioning/mbt-product-definition-2026-04-30.md.

5. Studio C — the production vendor

Studio C is the production arm of MBT. When the chat escalates, Studio C is who arrives to do the work. Video edits, audio production, design deliverables, content writing, brand kits — anything that needs human hands on a deliverable. Studio C also handles the admin of the production pipeline (intake, scheduling, vendor coordination, QA, delivery).

Two important boundaries: Studio C is not responsible for running MBT. The chat working, the agents working, the platform staying up — that's Chase's job, not Studio C's. Studio C is responsible for production work and pipeline admin. Inside that scope, they own it. Outside, they don't.

Three founders: Chase, Elijah Tuttle (Chief of Staff), Miles Dubois (Production Manager). Currently operating as a service line under Tuthill Design LLC. Planned future incorporation as a New York corporation. Working ownership scenario: five shares × twenty percent each, three to founders, two reserved for future market partners — not committed.

10. How decisions and escalation work

Three approval tiers. They apply equally to production work, customer commitments, and platform changes.

TierWho can decideSLAExamples
AChase only48hPilot launches. Commercial deliverables. External-paid client work. Pricing changes. Brand-defining decisions.
BElijah or Miles (Studio C); Tracy or Amy (Inn)24hRecurring work matching an approved template. Routine ops. Vendor coordination.
CAutomated, no humanInstantSocial clip generation under approved show rules. Deliverable rendering from approved masters. Status updates.

Tier A jobs cannot be shipped at Tier B even if a Tier B operator thinks the work is fine. The state machine enforces this: a job marked Tier A must route through "chase_review" before delivery, not directly from "internal_review."

If you're not sure which tier something is, default up. Treat it as Tier A and ask Chase. The cost of an extra check is small. The cost of shipping a Tier A decision under Tier B authority is real.
