# Shared Services

![Hub and spoke diagram of shared services](https://storage.googleapis.com/bmt-media-bigmuddy/illustrations/outsider-economics/12-shared-services-hub.webp)

Pool resources without merging. Ten businesses sharing one photographer is cheaper than ten businesses each using stock photos.

## The Concept

Shared services are the practical backbone of a federation. They're the things every business needs but no single small business can afford at quality:

- Photography and video production
- Social media management
- Bookkeeping
- Website maintenance
- Review monitoring
- Event promotion
- Bulk purchasing

## How It Works

**The pool model:**
- 10 businesses each pay $100/month into a shared services fund
- Total: $1,000/month
- That $1,000 buys a professional photographer for 8 hours, a social media manager for 20 hours, and a bookkeeper for 10 hours
- Each business gets better service than they could afford alone

**The rotation model:**
- A photographer visits 2-3 businesses per week on a rotating schedule
- Fresh photos every month for every member
- One person's slow Tuesday is another person's content shoot

**The credit model:**
- Members earn credits by contributing services
- A chef who caters a federation event earns credits toward photography
- No cash changes hands — but real value moves

## What to Share (and What Not To)

**Share:**
- Marketing and content production
- Back-office operations (bookkeeping, compliance)
- Technology infrastructure (website, directory, analytics)
- Bulk purchasing (supplies, insurance)
- Training and skill-building

**Don't share:**
- Customer relationships (each business owns theirs)
- Pricing decisions (independence matters)
- Creative identity (your brand is your brand)
- Hiring decisions (your team is your team)

## Starting Small

You don't need 10 members and a budget to start. Three businesses and one photographer is a shared service. Two restaurants splitting a delivery driver is a shared service. The scale comes later.

**Step 1:** Find 3 operators who need the same thing
**Step 2:** Find 1 person who can provide it
**Step 3:** Split the cost evenly
**Step 4:** Run it for 30 days and measure the value
