# Chapter 15: The $20 That Changed the Plumbing

## Overview

This chapter presents the macro economic argument. A single $20/month directory subscription is a sensor. Five hundred sensors across the corridor create an economic nervous system. Five thousand create a regional intelligence network. The math works because of velocity — a dollar that recirculates inside a county three times produces three times the impact of a dollar that leaves on day one. This chapter models the current economic leakage rate, the projected impact of platform adoption at scale, and the compounding effect over five years. It also addresses the ARPA funding window that makes rapid deployment financially possible.

## The Leakage Problem

Small-town economies in the Mississippi corridor leak capital. A dollar earned by a local business exits the county through a predictable sequence:

| Transaction | Where the money goes | Time to exit |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Credit card processing | Payment processor (San Francisco) | Immediate |
| SaaS subscriptions | Software companies (nationwide) | Monthly |
| Marketing agency fees | Agency (Memphis, Nashville, Jackson) | Monthly |
| Supply chain payments | Distributors (regional/national) | 30 days |
| Commercial rent (absentee landlord) | Landlord (out of state) | Monthly |
| Insurance premiums | National carrier | Quarterly |
| Franchise fees | Franchisor (out of state) | Monthly |
| Payroll taxes | Federal/state government | Biweekly |

The commonly cited estimate — and the one used in regional economic development literature — is that 80 cents of every dollar earned by a small business in a rural Mississippi county leaves the county within 48 hours. This figure is derived from Federal Reserve studies of rural capital flows and corroborated by USDA Economic Research Service reports on rural economic leakage.

The remaining 20 cents circulates locally: employee wages spent at other local businesses, local vendor payments, property taxes funding county services. But even that 20 cents experiences secondary leakage — the employee spends their wages at a chain store whose profits leave the county, or at a gas station owned by a national brand.

### The Velocity Multiplier

Economic velocity measures how many times a dollar changes hands within a defined area before it leaves. The Local Multiplier Effect, documented by the New Economics Foundation and the Institute for Local Self-Reliance, shows:

| Dollar type | Velocity (transactions before exit) | Multiplier |
|-------------|-------------------------------------|-----------|
| Dollar spent at chain/national business | 1.0–1.4 | 1.0–1.4x |
| Dollar spent at locally owned business | 1.8–3.5 | 1.8–3.5x |
| Dollar spent at locally owned business using local vendors | 3.0–4.2 | 3.0–4.2x |

When a hotel guest pays $165 for a room at a locally owned hotel that uses a local cleaning service, a local bakery for breakfast, and a local musician for entertainment, that $165 generates $297–$693 in local economic activity before exiting the county. The same $165 spent at a national chain hotel generates $165–$231 — because the profits, franchise fees, and supply chain payments leave immediately.

The platform does not create new dollars. It increases the velocity of existing dollars by keeping more transactions local.

## The $20 Subscription as Economic Sensor

Each $20/month directory subscription represents a business that has connected to the platform. Every connected business generates data:

| Data type | What it reveals | Aggregate value |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Revenue trends | Which sectors are growing or shrinking | Sector-level economic forecasting |
| Review sentiment | Consumer satisfaction by business type | Quality-of-life indicator |
| Competitive pricing | Rate and price movements across markets | Inflation and demand signals |
| Hiring indicators | New job postings, contractor searches | Employment trend data |
| Content production | Marketing activity levels | Business confidence indicator |
| Referral patterns | Which businesses send customers to each other | Economic network mapping |
| Upgrade/downgrade rates | Which tiers businesses choose | Willingness-to-invest indicator |

At 50 businesses per town, the data is useful for individual businesses. At 500 businesses across the corridor, the data becomes an economic intelligence system. At 5,000, it becomes a real-time economic dashboard for the entire region.

No government agency, no chamber of commerce, and no economic development organization currently has access to this granularity of real-time economic data for the Mississippi corridor. The Census Bureau publishes data on a 5-year lag. The Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes monthly data at the state level. County-level economic data is sparse, delayed, and aggregated beyond usefulness.

The directory produces this data as a byproduct of normal operations. The $20 subscriber gets a business dashboard. The platform gets a sensor.

## Modeling the Impact

### Scenario 1: 500 Businesses on Platform (Year 2–3)

Assumption: 500 businesses across the seven-city Snowbird Circuit, averaging $150,000 in annual revenue per business.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total corridor business revenue (500 businesses) | $75,000,000 |
| Current estimated local retention rate | 20% ($15,000,000 stays local) |
| Platform-driven retention improvements | See below |

Platform impact on local retention:

| Mechanism | Leakage reduced | How |
|-----------|----------------|-----|
| Marketing spend stays local | 2–3% of revenue | Platform replaces out-of-town agency; $20/mo vs. $2,000/mo agency retainer |
| Content production stays local | 0.5–1% of revenue | Business-contributed content replaces agency-produced content |
| Technology spend stays local | 0.5–1% of revenue | Platform subscription ($20–499/mo) vs. 4–6 separate SaaS tools ($200–800/mo, all out of state) |
| Referral network increases local spending | 1–2% of revenue | Directory peer-to-peer referrals keep customers in-network |
| AI-driven pricing optimization | 1–2% of revenue | Better pricing decisions increase revenue without increasing costs |
| **Total retention improvement** | **5–9%** | |

Revised retention model at 500 businesses:

| Metric | Before platform | After platform | Delta |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-------|
| Local retention rate | 20% | 25–29% | +5–9 percentage points |
| Dollars retained locally (annual) | $15,000,000 | $18,750,000–21,750,000 | +$3,750,000–6,750,000 |
| Velocity multiplier on retained dollars | 2.0x | 2.5x (improved network density) | +0.5x |
| Total local economic impact | $30,000,000 | $46,875,000–54,375,000 | +$16,875,000–24,375,000 |

The delta — $16.9M to $24.4M in additional local economic activity — is generated not by bringing new dollars into the corridor but by slowing the exit of existing dollars and increasing their velocity while they remain.

### Scenario 2: 5,000 Businesses on Platform (Year 5–7)

Assumption: 5,000 businesses across an expanded corridor (seven core cities plus ten additional towns from Chapter 10), averaging $120,000 in annual revenue per business (smaller average due to micro-businesses joining at scale).

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|

| Estimated local retention rate with platform | 28–32% |
| Dollars retained locally (annual) | $168,000,000–192,000,000 |
| Velocity multiplier | 2.8x (mature network density) |

Compared to baseline (20% retention, 2.0x velocity):

| Metric | Baseline | With platform at scale | Delta |
|--------|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Total local economic impact | $240,000,000 | $470,400,000–537,600,000 | +$230,400,000–297,600,000 |

At 5,000 businesses, the platform generates an estimated $230M–$298M in additional local economic activity per year. This is the compound effect of velocity improvement across a dense network.

## The ARPA Window

The American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) allocated $826 million to the State of Mississippi. As of early 2026, a substantial portion remains unspent. The deadline for obligation of ARPA State and Local Fiscal Recovery Funds is December 31, 2026. Funds must be fully expended by December 31, 2028.

Relevant ARPA eligible use categories for the platform:

| Category | Application | Platform alignment |
|----------|------------|-------------------|
| Revenue replacement | Local government operational technology | Municipal deployment |
| Economic development | Small business technical assistance | Directory subscriptions, AI business tools |
| Broadband/digital access | Digital inclusion for rural businesses | Platform as digital infrastructure |
| Workforce development | Skills training and employment programs | Talent pipeline, content production training |
| Tourism recovery | Destination marketing and tourism infrastructure | Directory, Magazine, content engine |

### Municipal Block Purchase Model

A municipality using ARPA funds purchases directory memberships for Main Street businesses:

| Purchase size | Cost | Businesses served | Per-business annual cost |
|--------------|------|-------------------|------------------------|
| 50 memberships ($20 tier) | $12,000/year | 50 | $240 |

For context, the average small Mississippi city spends $50,000–200,000 annually on agency-driven destination marketing. The platform replaces that spend while simultaneously delivering AI business tools to every participating business.

## What Changes When the Plumbing Changes

The $20 subscription does not feel like infrastructure. It feels like a business tool. But at scale, the network of $20 subscriptions becomes the economic plumbing of the corridor.

| Before platform | After platform (at scale) |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| Economic data arrives 12–60 months late | Economic data is real-time |
| Marketing dollars leave the county | Marketing happens on-platform, locally operated |
| Businesses compete in isolation | Businesses collaborate through referral networks |
| Talent leaves for Nashville/Atlanta | Talent develops locally through Rise Up pipeline |
| Content produced by out-of-town agencies | Content produced by local businesses, assembled by AI |
| Tourism promotion is top-down (CVB → visitor) | Tourism promotion is peer-to-peer (business → business → visitor) |
| Government economic reports are backward-looking | Government receives forward-looking AI analysis |
| Technology is a cost center (SaaS subscriptions leaving) | Technology is a retention tool (platform keeps dollars local) |

The plumbing metaphor is precise. The current system leaks. Dollars flow through the county like water through old pipes — most of it exits before it can be used. The platform does not add water. It fixes the pipes.

- Reach 1,500 businesses across corridor
- Activate referral network analytics

- Reach 5,000 businesses
- Platform data becomes a primary input for regional economic planning
- Content production volume supports standalone media operation
- Directory network effects create natural monopoly in corridor small-town business tools
