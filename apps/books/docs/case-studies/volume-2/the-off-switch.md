# Chapter 10: The Off Switch

## Overview

Every critical system that sustains a household -- income, food, shelter, communication, money -- is controlled by an external entity that can terminate access without the individual's consent. These termination capabilities are "off switches." Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) represent the most comprehensive off switch under development: programmable money with embedded spending restrictions. A coordination network based on direct skill exchange operates outside this control infrastructure. This chapter maps the dependency chain, analyzes CBDC programmability, and defines the dual economy model for reducing off-switch exposure from a typical 95/5 external/internal ratio toward a target of 60/40 or 50/50.

---

## 10.1 The Dependency Chain

The following table maps the five primary dependencies of a typical U.S. household, the entity controlling each, and the termination mechanism.

| Dependency | Controlling Entity | Termination Mechanism | Single Point of Failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Income** | Employer (single company) | Layoff, restructuring, automation | Yes -- 100% of household income from one source |
| **Food** | Corporate supply chain (Kroger, Sysco, Walmart -- all publicly traded) | Supply chain disruption, store closure, price manipulation | Yes -- most households cannot self-supply food for >7 days |
| **Shelter** | Mortgage servicer or landlord (often REIT or holding company) | Missed payment triggers eviction/foreclosure clock | Yes -- housing functions as a subscription with external renewal control |
| **Communication** | Telecom corporations (phone, internet, email providers) | Service termination, data access by government request, account closure | Yes -- average U.S. phone bill: $68/month |
| **Money** | Federal Reserve (issuance), commercial banks (custody), digital payment systems (transaction layer) | Account freeze, transaction block, funds hold (PayPal: up to 180 days) | Yes -- all dollars are IOUs from institutions backed by $34 trillion in federal debt |

**Real-world termination examples:**
- Canada (2022): Personal bank accounts frozen for participation in trucker protests. No criminal charges. No court orders. Banks complied with government directive.
- PayPal: Reserves the right to hold funds for 180 days during "investigation."
- Suez Canal (2021): Single ship blockage disrupted global supply chains, causing medication and food shortages.

Each link in this chain is controlled by an entity that is not the individual, and that will terminate access when it serves that entity's interest -- not through malice, but through indifference to the individual as anything other than a data point.

## 10.2 Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

A CBDC is government-issued digital currency with programmable spending rules. Physical cash cannot be programmed: a paper bill works everywhere, for any purchase, with no conditions and no identity verification. A CBDC can embed rules into the currency itself.

### Programmable Restrictions Under Discussion or in Pilot

| Restriction Type | Status | Jurisdiction |
|---|---|---|
| Geographic spending limits | Active in pilot programs | China (digital yuan) |
| Holding limits (maximum balance caps) | Publicly discussed by officials | European Central Bank (digital euro) |
| Programmable spending conditions | Active in pilot | Nigeria (eNaira) |
| Expiration dates (forced spending within time window) | Discussed by policy researchers | Multiple jurisdictions |
| Purchase category restrictions | Discussed by policy researchers | Multiple jurisdictions |
| Automatic tax deductions per transaction | Discussed by policy researchers | Multiple jurisdictions |

### Potential Programmable Conditions

The following conditions have been publicly discussed by central bank officials or monetary policy researchers:

1. Currency expires if unspent within 90 days (forced economic stimulus)
2. Currency does not function outside a 50-mile radius of the holder's registered address
3. Purchases above $500 require additional identity verification
4. Specific product categories blocked (algorithmic determination)
5. Automatic environmental surcharge calculated per transaction

These are not speculative scenarios. They are documented policy discussions from the institutions designing these systems.

**Implication:** CBDCs represent the most comprehensive off switch in development. Unlike account freezes (which target access to money), CBDCs can program the money itself to not function under specified conditions.

## 10.3 What Cannot Be Switched Off

A coordination network based on direct skill exchange operates outside the financial system's control infrastructure.

| System | Off Switch | Network Equivalent | Off Switch |
|---|---|---|---|
| Employer income | Layoff | Network skill exchange | None -- 19 other members still need services |
| Bank account | Account freeze | Points bank (shared spreadsheet) | None -- no government agency monitors local spreadsheets |
| Corporate food supply | Supply chain disruption | Network food production (gardens, chickens, local growers) | None -- local food cannot be remotely disabled |
| Programmable money (CBDC) | Spending restrictions embedded in currency | Time-based exchange | None -- no currency involved; exchange denominated in human hours |

**Core principle:** Skills cannot be frozen. Labor capacity cannot be remotely disabled. Time-based exchange does not route through any controllable payment rail. The "currency" is human time and human skill -- neither of which has a kill switch.

## 10.4 Transaction Rerouting

Each transaction moved from the external economy (corporate supply chain) to the internal economy (coordination network) produces two effects:

1. **Value retention:** Economic value stays in the community instead of being extracted.
2. **Dependency reduction:** One fewer transaction routed through a system with an off switch.

This is not a boycott strategy. Boycotts are temporary and driven by emotional response. Transaction rerouting is a permanent structural change in how needs are met.

**Rerouting examples:**

| Need | External Route | Internal Route |
|---|---|---|
| Eggs | Grocery store (Kroger/Walmart) | Network member with chickens |
| Vehicle repair | Dealership or chain shop | Network mechanic |
| Bookkeeping | Hired CPA | Network member trade (bookkeeping for carpentry) |
| Childcare | Daycare center ($800-$1,500/month) | Network childcare rotation |
| Home repair | Hired contractor | Network trades members |

Each rerouted transaction is a permanent reduction in external dependency.

## 10.5 The Dual Economy Model

Total withdrawal from the external economy is not feasible. Taxes, mortgages, medical bills, and other institutional obligations require dollars and formal financial interfaces.

The operational model is a dual economy: external economy for obligations that require institutional interface, internal economy for everything the network can provide.

### Economy Ratio Targets

| Ratio (External/Internal) | Description | Off-Switch Exposure |
|---|---|---|
| 95/5 | Current typical U.S. household | Near-total dependency |
| 80/20 | Early-stage network participation | Meaningful reduction in exposure |
| 60/40 | Achievable target for active network members | Substantial resilience |
| 50/50 | Ambitious target | Half of economic life outside off-switch reach |

### Internal Economy Capacity by Category

| Category | Services Providable Within a Network |
|---|---|
| Home repair | Plumbing, electrical, carpentry, painting, HVAC maintenance |
| Professional services | Bookkeeping, tax preparation, legal basics, web development, marketing |
| Food | Garden produce, eggs, preserved foods, group cooking |
| Childcare | Rotation-based childcare, tutoring, after-school care |
| Transportation | Shared vehicles, group purchasing of fuel, ride coordination |
| Event services | Planning, catering, setup/teardown, AV support |
| Construction | Deck building, fencing, outbuilding construction, renovation labor |

Every skill added to the network converts one more category from external dependency to internal provision.

### External Economy (Minimized)

Reserved for obligations that cannot be met internally:
- Federal, state, and local taxes
- Mortgage or rent (until network develops property capacity)
- Medical bills and insurance premiums
- Utilities (electricity, water, gas)
- Materials that cannot be sourced locally

This volume began with a number: $450,000 -- the estimated annual value locked in 20 people's uncoordinated labor (Chapter 1).

It concludes with a different number: zero. Zero single points of failure. Zero off switches. Zero corporate intermediaries between network members and the people who can meet their needs.

**System architecture comparison:**

| Property | Conventional Economy | Coordination Network |

## Implementation Steps

1. Map the household dependency chain: list every system depended upon, identify the controlling entity, and note the termination mechanism.
2. Rank dependencies by vulnerability (single point of failure = highest priority).
3. Select one dependency to address first with a local alternative (food production, skill trade, shared purchasing).
4. Execute the first internal transaction: trade a skill with a network member instead of hiring an external provider.
5. Track external vs. internal spending for one month. Calculate the current external/internal ratio.
6. Set a target ratio for 90 days (e.g., move from 95/5 to 85/15).
7. Identify the next 3 transactions that can be rerouted from external to internal.
8. Launch the coordination network if not already operational (see Chapter 8).
9. At 90 days, recalculate the external/internal ratio and set the next target.
