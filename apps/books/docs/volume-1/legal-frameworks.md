# Chapter 9: Legal and Governance Frameworks

## Overview

Task board communities operate within existing legal systems. This chapter covers the four primary legal structures available, their advantages and limitations, state-specific requirements, the tax implications of barter and labor exchange, and governance frameworks that protect community autonomy while ensuring legal compliance.

The correct legal structure depends on community size, asset ownership, revenue generation, and federation plans. Most communities start informal and formalize as they grow. This chapter provides the decision framework for when and how to formalize.

Volume 2 provides state-specific guidance for Mississippi, Louisiana, Tennessee, and Alabama — the Deep South corridor states. Volume 3 addresses multi-state federation legal structures and interstate labor exchange compliance.

## The Four Primary Legal Structures

### Structure 1: Unincorporated Association

**What it is:** A group of people organized for a common purpose without formal incorporation. No state filing required.

**Best for:** Communities of 5-20 members with no shared assets and no external revenue.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Formation | Written agreement among members (no state filing) |
| Cost | $0 |
| Liability protection | None — members personally liable |
| Tax status | Members report individual income/barter value |
| Governance | Defined by internal agreement |
| Asset ownership | Held by individual members or informally shared |
| Maximum recommended size | 20 members |

**Advantages:**
- Zero cost and zero bureaucracy to start
- Complete governance flexibility
- No annual filings or compliance requirements
- Appropriate for the first 90 days of any community

**Limitations:**
- No liability protection (if a task results in injury or property damage, individual members are personally liable)
- Cannot hold assets in the community's name
- Cannot open a bank account
- Not suitable once the community generates revenue or holds valuable assets

### Structure 2: Limited Liability Company (LLC)

**What it is:** A formal business entity that provides liability protection while allowing flexible governance through an operating agreement.

**Best for:** Communities of 10-50 members with shared assets, infrastructure projects, or external revenue.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Formation | State filing (articles of organization) |
| Cost | $50-500 depending on state (plus annual fees in some states) |
| Liability protection | Yes — members' personal assets protected |
| Tax status | Pass-through (members report on individual returns) or elect S-corp/C-corp |
| Governance | Defined by operating agreement (highly customizable) |
| Asset ownership | LLC owns assets on behalf of members |
| Maximum recommended size | 50 members |

**Advantages:**
- Liability protection for members
- Flexible governance through operating agreement
- Can hold assets, open bank accounts, sign leases
- Can generate and distribute revenue
- Operating agreement can encode task board governance, democratic decision-making, and exit procedures

**Limitations:**
- Annual state fees in most states ($0-800/year depending on state)
- Requires annual filings in most states
- Operating agreement should be drafted or reviewed by an attorney
- Member changes require operating agreement amendments

**Operating Agreement Essentials for Task Board Communities:**

The operating agreement should include:
1. Purpose statement aligned with community values
2. Membership criteria and onboarding process
3. Decision-making process (consensus, majority vote, or delegation)
4. Contribution requirements (minimum hours, capital contributions)
5. Asset ownership and usage rules
6. Revenue distribution formula
7. Conflict resolution protocol (four-tier system from Chapter 4)
8. Exit process and payout terms
9. Dissolution process and asset distribution

### Structure 3: Cooperative Corporation

**What it is:** A corporation owned and governed democratically by its members, organized under state cooperative statutes.

**Best for:** Communities of 20-200+ members with significant shared assets, substantial revenue, or plans for formal federation.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Formation | State filing under cooperative corporation statute |
| Cost | $100-1,000 depending on state |
| Liability protection | Yes — corporate liability shield |
| Tax status | Subchapter T (cooperative taxation) or standard corporate |
| Governance | One member, one vote (required by most state cooperative statutes) |
| Asset ownership | Corporation owns assets; members own shares/patronage |
| Maximum recommended size | No limit |

**Advantages:**
- Democratic governance built into legal structure (one member, one vote)
- Can access cooperative-specific financing (Farm Credit, cooperative banks, USDA programs)
- Patronage dividends allow tax-efficient member distributions
- Well-understood legal form with extensive case law
- Can qualify for cooperative tax treatment under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code

**Limitations:**
- More complex formation than LLC
- Board of directors required in most states
- Annual meeting and reporting requirements
- Some states have limited or outdated cooperative statutes

**Types of cooperatives relevant to task board communities:**

| Type | Member Relationship | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Worker cooperative | Members are workers | Mondragon, Cooperative Home Care Associates |
| Consumer cooperative | Members are customers | Park Slope Food Coop, REI |
| Producer cooperative | Members are suppliers | Ocean Spray, Land O'Lakes |
| Multi-stakeholder | Multiple member classes | Weaver Street Market (workers + consumers) |

### Structure 4: DAO LLC (Decentralized Autonomous Organization)

**What it is:** An LLC whose governance is encoded in smart contracts or digital protocols, recognized in certain states.

**Best for:** Digitally native communities, communities with significant digital asset holdings, or communities planning cross-state federation with automated governance.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| Formation | State filing in DAO-recognizing states (Wyoming, Tennessee, others) |
| Cost | $100-500 plus smart contract development |
| Liability protection | Yes (in recognizing states) |
| Tax status | Treated as LLC for federal tax purposes |
| Governance | Encoded in digital protocols; varies by implementation |
| Asset ownership | DAO holds assets; token-based membership |
| States recognizing | Wyoming (2021), Tennessee (2022), others pending |

**Advantages:**
- Automated governance reduces coordination overhead
- Transparent, auditable decision-making
- Well-suited for cross-geography federation
- Can integrate with digital hour tracking systems

**Limitations:**
- Limited state recognition
- Smart contract development costs
- Regulatory uncertainty at federal level
- Requires digital literacy among all members
- Legal precedent is still developing

## Choosing the Right Structure

| Community Stage | Recommended Structure | Trigger to Upgrade |
|---|---|---|
| Days 1-90 | Unincorporated association | Shared assets exceed $1,000 or external revenue begins |
| Months 4-12 | LLC with operating agreement | Revenue exceeds $50,000/year or membership exceeds 30 |
| Year 1-3+ | Cooperative corporation | Community wants formal democratic governance, cooperative financing access, or federation formalization |
| Any stage (digital-native) | DAO LLC | Community operates primarily digitally with cross-state membership |

## Tax Implications of Barter and Labor Exchange

The IRS treats barter exchange as taxable income. Task board communities must understand these requirements to maintain compliance.

### IRS Barter Exchange Rules

Under IRS Publication 525, the fair market value of goods and services received through barter is taxable income. This applies to task board communities:

- If a member receives services valued at $500 through the task board, that $500 is reportable income
- The tax obligation falls on the recipient, not the provider
- Barter exchanges exceeding $600 per year require Form 1099-B from a "barter exchange" or self-reporting by members

### Practical Tax Compliance

**For small communities (under $600/member/year in exchanges):**
- Members self-report barter income on personal tax returns
- No community-level reporting required
- Keep records of hours exchanged and values for documentation

**For larger communities ($600+/member/year):**
- Register as a barter exchange with the IRS (Form SS-4)
- Issue Form 1099-B to members receiving $600+ in exchange value
- Maintain accurate records of all exchanges with fair market valuations

**For communities generating external revenue:**
- File appropriate business tax returns (LLC or cooperative)
- Separate internal labor exchange from external revenue accounting
- Consult a CPA familiar with cooperative taxation

### State Tax Considerations

State tax treatment of barter varies:
- Most states follow federal treatment (barter income is taxable)
- Some states offer cooperative tax exemptions or preferential treatment
- Sales tax may apply to goods exchanged through barter in some states
- State-specific guidance is essential — consult a local tax professional

## Governance Frameworks

### Minimum Governance Requirements (All Communities)

Regardless of legal structure, every task board community needs:

1. **Written purpose statement** — what the community exists to do
2. **Membership criteria** — who can join and how
3. **Contribution expectations** — minimum hours, expected participation
4. **Decision-making process** — how decisions are made at each level
5. **Conflict resolution protocol** — the four-tier system from Chapter 4
6. **Exit process** — how members leave and what happens to their contributions
7. **Asset management** — how shared assets are maintained and governed

### Decision-Making Methods

| Method | Best For | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| Consensus | Values decisions, community agreements | Slow, can be blocked by one person |
| Supermajority (75%) | Policy changes, external partnerships | Requires formal voting infrastructure |
| Simple majority (51%) | Operational decisions, task prioritization | Can marginalize minority views |
| Delegation | Day-to-day operations, crisis response | Requires clear scope and accountability |
| Sociocracy (consent) | Ongoing governance, role assignment | Requires training, unfamiliar to most |

**Recommended hybrid approach:**
- Consensus for community values and purpose
- Supermajority (75%) for structural changes (legal structure, membership criteria, asset acquisition)
- Simple majority for operational decisions (task multipliers, meeting schedules)
- Delegation for day-to-day coordination (rotating coordinator handles board management)

### Governance Documentation Checklist

Every community should maintain these documents:

1. **Community Agreement** (1-2 pages) — purpose, values, expectations, conflict resolution
2. **Operating Procedures** (2-5 pages) — meeting cadence, task board management, hour tracking, multiplier adjustment
3. **Membership Roster** — names, skills, contact, join date, contribution records
4. **Financial Records** — cash contributions, expenditures, revenue (if applicable)
5. **Meeting Minutes** — decisions made, tasks assigned, issues raised

## Implementation

### Step 1: Operate Informally for 90 Days
Start with a written community agreement (unincorporated association). No state filings needed. Focus on proving the coordination model works.

### Step 2: Assess Formalization Triggers
At the 90-day review, evaluate:
- Does the community hold shared assets worth more than $1,000?
- Is the community generating external revenue?
- Do members need liability protection?
- Is the community planning infrastructure projects?

If any answer is yes, proceed to formalization.

### Step 3: Choose and File
Based on the decision framework above, select the appropriate legal structure. Budget $200-500 for legal review of formation documents.

### Step 4: Draft Governance Documents
Create the community agreement, operating procedures, and financial records system. Use the templates from this chapter as starting points.

### Step 5: Establish Tax Compliance
Determine whether the community needs to register as a barter exchange. Set up record-keeping for member exchanges. Consult a CPA for the first year.
