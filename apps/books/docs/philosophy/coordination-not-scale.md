# Coordination, Not Scale

![Connected small shops outperforming one giant factory](https://storage.googleapis.com/bmt-media-bigmuddy/illustrations/outsider-economics/05-federation-vs-scale.webp)

The dominant economic playbook says: grow. Get bigger. Scale. Outsider Economics says: coordinate. Get better at working together.

## Why Scale Doesn't Work for Small Towns

Scale requires:
- Venture capital (which wants 10x returns and doesn't invest in towns of 15,000)
- Standardization (which kills the character that makes your town worth visiting)
- Extraction (which pulls value toward headquarters)
- Speed (which burns out the people who actually live there)

Small towns that try to scale end up competing on someone else's terms, with someone else's money, for someone else's benefit.

## What Coordination Looks Like

Coordination is different. It means:

- **10 businesses sharing one bookkeeper** instead of 10 businesses each struggling with QuickBooks alone
- **A shared content calendar** where one photographer serves six restaurants instead of six restaurants using stock photos
- **A federated booking system** where the hotel, the restaurant, and the music venue cross-promote without a middleman taking 25%
- **A task board** where work finds the right person instead of the right person finding work

The unit of value isn't the transaction. It's the **coordination act** — the moment two independent operators align without surrendering independence.

## The Coordination Premium

When coordination works, you get more output from the same resources. This is the coordination premium:

- Shared equipment = lower cost per use
- Shared marketing = lower cost per customer
- Shared knowledge = faster problem-solving
- Shared reputation = higher trust with visitors

A coordinated town of 500 businesses can outperform an uncoordinated town of 5,000 — not by being bigger, but by being more connected.
