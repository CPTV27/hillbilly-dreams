# Chapter 13: Franchising Joy

## Overview

Rise Up is a format, not a band. Like Blue Man Group, Cirque du Soleil, or Second City, the brand is the experience — not any individual performer. This chapter examines the franchise model: how multiple Rise Up units operate in different regions with different rotating casts, all sharing brand infrastructure, platform technology, and recording/distribution systems. The unit economics, startup costs, and expansion sequence are documented below.

## The Format as Franchise

A franchise requires three things: a repeatable format, shared infrastructure, and unit economics that work independently of the parent. Rise Up meets all three criteria.

### The Repeatable Format

Every Rise Up performance follows the same structure regardless of location, cast, or venue:

| Segment | Duration | Content |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Opening set | 20 minutes | House band, three songs, establishes energy and caliber |
| Featured performers | 40 minutes | 3–4 rotating cast members perform solo or duo sets |
| Collaborative finale | 20 minutes | Full ensemble, audience participation, signature closer |
| Post-show engagement | 30 minutes | Meet the artists, merch, recordings available for purchase |

Total runtime: approximately 80 minutes of performance plus 30 minutes of post-show. The format is tight enough to replicate but flexible enough to accommodate regional musical traditions. A Memphis unit leans blues and soul. A New Orleans unit features gospel, jazz, and brass. A European unit can incorporate local musical traditions alongside American roots music.

The format does not depend on any single performer. Cast members rotate. House band members can be replaced. The musical director shapes the experience. The brand carries the audience expectation.

### Shared Infrastructure

All Rise Up units share:

| Infrastructure | Description | Cost allocation |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| Brand and IP | Name, logo, format rights, marketing templates | Franchise fee |
| Platform technology | Ticketing, merch, streaming, content management | Per-unit license |
| Recording/distribution | Live capture → master → streaming platforms pipeline | Records division handles |
| Content engine | Magazine, Radio, social media production framework | Central production |
| Booking network | Venue relationships, tour routing, promoter contacts | Shared database |
| Artist development pipeline | Discovery → development → solo career path | Cross-unit pipeline |

A new unit does not build any of these systems from scratch. The infrastructure exists. The unit plugs in.

## Unit Model

### Unit Components

Each Rise Up unit requires:

| Component | Description | Minimum requirement |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Musical director | Shapes the sound, runs auditions, manages cast | 1 per unit |
| House band | Core rhythm section + lead instruments | 4–5 musicians |
| Rotating cast | Local and regional performers who join for shows | 8–15 on roster |
| Tour manager | Logistics, venue advance, crew management | 1 per unit |
| Content producer | Video, photo, social media, editorial | 1 per unit |
| Home venue | Base of operations for rehearsals and anchor shows | 1 per unit |
| Regional circuit | 3–7 cities within touring range of home venue | Defined route |

Total team per unit: 7–8 full-time or contracted positions, plus a rotating cast roster of 8–15.

## Franchise Economics

### Per-Unit Startup Costs

| Category | Cost range | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Franchise fee | $25,000–50,000 | One-time, covers brand license + platform setup |
| Home venue setup | $15,000–40,000 | Sound system, lighting, staging (if not venue-provided) |
| Equipment | $20,000–35,000 | Instruments, recording gear, merch initial inventory |
| Working capital (3 months) | $45,000–75,000 | Crew compensation, venue deposits, marketing |
| Insurance | $5,000–10,000 | Annual liability + equipment |

This range assumes the franchisee does not own the home venue. If the franchisee operates an existing venue (a theater, a hotel, a church), the home venue setup cost drops significantly.

## The Franchise Network Effect

Multiple units create compounding value that single units cannot:

### Cross-Unit Artist Movement

An artist discovered in the Natchez unit can perform with the Memphis unit, then the New Orleans unit. The artist's audience grows across the corridor without the artist paying for separate tours. The franchise network IS the touring circuit.

### Shared Catalog

Recordings made at any unit are available to all units for radio programming, playlist curation, and streaming distribution. The catalog grows with every show across every unit. A three-unit network performing 42 shows each produces 126 live recordings per year — a substantial and growing catalog.

### Combined Audience

| Units | Combined metro population | Potential annual audience |
|-------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| 1 (Natchez) | 14,520 | 5,000–15,000 |
| 2 (+ Memphis) | 1,314,520 (MSA-adjusted) | 25,000–75,000 |
| 3 (+ New Orleans) | 2,584,520 (MSA-adjusted) | 60,000–180,000 |
| 5 (+ Atlanta, Nashville) | 9,584,520 (MSA-adjusted) | 150,000–450,000 |

Each additional unit expands the addressable audience for all units. A fan who sees Rise Up in Memphis and travels to New Orleans has a reason to attend the New Orleans unit. The brand creates travel motivation — the same dynamic that drives fans of touring Broadway shows.

### Content Volume

| Units | Annual shows | Annual recordings | Annual content assets |
|-------|-------------|------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | 42 | 42 | 350+ |
| 3 | 126 | 126 | 1,050+ |
| 5 | 210 | 210 | 1,750+ |
| 7 | 294 | 294 | 2,450+ |

At seven units, the franchise produces nearly 300 live recordings and 2,450 content assets per year. This volume supports a standalone media operation — a radio network, a streaming catalog, a documentary series, and a magazine with a continuous supply of original material.

## The Cultural Argument

Rise Up is not a variety show. It is a cultural export.

American gospel and blues originated in the Mississippi corridor. The music that shaped rock and roll, soul, R&B, and hip-hop was created within the geographic footprint of the Snowbird Circuit. This music has a global audience.

Documented international demand for American roots music:

| Market indicator | Value | Source |
|-----------------|-------|--------|
| Gospel music market (global) | $12.5 billion (2023) | Grand View Research |
| Blues festival attendance (Europe, annual) | 2M+ across major festivals | European Blues Union |
| Spotify blues genre streams (monthly, global) | 1.2B+ | Spotify for Artists estimates |
| American roots music festival revenue (US) | $800M+ annually | Pollstar / festival industry reports |

A Rise Up London unit performing American gospel and blues in a 500-seat venue is not a novelty act. It is a premium cultural export with documented demand and limited supply. No comparable franchise format currently operates in this space.

## The Franchise Agreement Structure

| Term | Value |
|------|-------|
| Initial franchise fee | $25,000–50,000 |
| Ongoing royalty | 5% of gross revenue |
| Technology license | $500–1,000/month |
| Territory exclusivity | Defined by home city + 75-mile radius |
| Term length | 5 years, renewable |
| Training | 2-week intensive with anchor unit in Natchez |
| Brand standards | Format compliance, quality standards, content requirements |
| Recording rights | All recordings enter shared catalog; revenue splits per artist agreement |

The franchise agreement does not transfer ownership of the format. It licenses the right to operate a Rise Up unit within a defined territory. The franchisor (Hillbilly Dreams Inc.) retains the brand, the platform, and the master catalog. The franchisee retains local venue relationships, local artist relationships, and unit-level profit.

## Implementation

### Phase 1: Prove the Anchor Unit (Months 1–18)
- Launch Unit 1 in Natchez with full Snowbird Circuit
- Run six tours minimum
- Document per-show and per-tour economics
- Establish repeatable format, training materials, and brand standards
- Build catalog of 40+ live recordings

### Phase 2: Franchise Documentation (Months 12–18)
- Create Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD)
- Develop operations manual
- Build training curriculum (2-week intensive)
- Establish brand standards and compliance framework
- Retain franchise attorney for state registrations

### Phase 3: Unit 2 Launch (Months 18–30)
- Identify Memphis franchisee (likely a venue operator or promoter with existing infrastructure)
- Execute franchise agreement
- Train Memphis musical director and core team
- Launch Memphis unit with Mid-South Circuit
- Cross-promote with Natchez unit

### Phase 4: Network Expansion (Year 3+)
- Launch Unit 3 (New Orleans)
- Establish cross-unit touring exchanges
- Begin European market research
- Scale content production for multi-unit catalog
