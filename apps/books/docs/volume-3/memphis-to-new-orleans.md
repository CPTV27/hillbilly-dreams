# Chapter 11: Memphis to New Orleans on a Prevost

## Overview

The Snowbird Circuit is a seven-city touring route running from Memphis, Tennessee to New Orleans, Louisiana along the Mississippi corridor. Rise Up performs at each stop. The bus is a Prevost — not a van, not a trailer — because the bus itself functions as a mobile recording studio, a radio broadcast unit, and a content production vehicle. This chapter documents the route logistics, the per-stop economics, and the compounding effect of a tour that feeds seven revenue streams at every city.

## The Route

The Snowbird Circuit follows the river. The full seven-city run covers approximately 680 miles of driving over 10–14 days, depending on rest days and double-headers.

Population data: U.S. Census Bureau 2020 estimates. Total addressable population across the seven stops exceeds 1.3 million. The route touches two state capitals (Baton Rouge, and Jackson within a reasonable detour from Vicksburg), three major tourism destinations (Memphis, Natchez, New Orleans), and two delta blues heritage centers (Clarksdale, Greenville).

### Drive Time Constraints

No single leg exceeds two hours and fifteen minutes. This is deliberate. The bus doubles as a workspace. Short drives mean the team arrives with energy, not exhaustion. The longest leg — Vicksburg to Natchez or Greenville to Vicksburg — still leaves most of the day available for load-in, soundcheck, content production, and local engagement.

## The Bus as Mobile Infrastructure

A standard touring van carries instruments and people. A Prevost carries infrastructure.

### Configuration

| Zone | Function | Equipment |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Front lounge | Radio broadcast / podcast recording | Shure SM7B mics, Focusrite Scarlett 18i20, acoustic treatment panels |
| Mid-cabin | Editorial workspace | MacBook stations, portable photo editing setup, mobile hotspot array |
| Rear lounge | Artist green room / recording booth | Portable vocal booth, in-ear monitor system, reference monitors |
| Bay storage | Backline, merch, staging gear | PA system, lighting rig, merchandise inventory |

The front lounge converts to a broadcast studio in under ten minutes. Two hosts, two guests, four microphones, one interface. The same setup records a radio segment, a podcast episode, or an artist interview. Content produced on the bus during a two-hour drive between Natchez and Baton Rouge publishes to Big Muddy Radio before the bus arrives at the venue.

### Content Output Per Leg

Each drive between cities produces:

| Content type | Quantity | Distribution channel |
|-------------|----------|---------------------|
| Radio interview | 1 | Big Muddy Radio |
| Podcast segment | 1 | All podcast platforms |
| Behind-the-scenes video | 2–4 clips | Social media |
| Artist profile draft | 1 | Big Muddy Magazine |
| City guide notes | 1 set | Magazine / Directory |

Over a seven-city run, the bus produces a minimum of seven radio episodes, seven artist profiles, and 14–28 social video clips — all during transit time that would otherwise be idle.

## Venue Strategy

The Snowbird Circuit does not rely on a single venue type. Venue selection follows the economics of each market.

| City | Venue type | Capacity range | Ticket price range |
|------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Memphis | Theater / concert hall | 500–1,200 | $25–45 |
| Clarksdale | Blues club / civic auditorium | 150–400 | $15–25 |
| Greenville | Convention center / church venue | 200–500 | $15–25 |
| Vicksburg | Theater / outdoor amphitheater | 300–800 | $20–30 |
| Natchez | Home venue (Inn) + Convention Center | 100–600 | $20–35 |
| Baton Rouge | Theater / university venue | 500–1,500 | $25–40 |
| New Orleans | Music hall / church venue | 300–1,000 | $25–50 |

Natchez is the home base. The Inn parlor hosts intimate showcases (50–100 capacity) while the Natchez Convention Center handles larger productions (600+ capacity). Every other stop uses existing venue infrastructure. No new venues need to be built.

### Venue Deal Structures

Three standard arrangements:

| Structure | Description | Best for |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Door deal | Tour keeps 80–85% of ticket revenue after venue costs | Established markets with strong pre-sales |
| Guarantee + overage | Venue pays flat fee ($3,500–8,000) plus percentage of revenue above breakeven | New markets where audience size is uncertain |
| Co-promotion | Venue and tour split costs and revenue 50/50 | Venues with their own marketing infrastructure |

The preferred structure is the guarantee plus overage. It ensures a minimum net per show while preserving upside in strong markets.

## The Directory Growth Effect

Each tour stop is a directory growth event. The tour team arrives in a city and the following happens in parallel with show preparation:

1. The content producer photographs Main Street businesses
2. The tour manager introduces the directory to venue operators, restaurants, and hotels
3. Post-show, the audience — local business owners, musicians, community members — receives directory information
4. The city guide published in the Magazine drives traffic to directory listings

Projected directory growth per tour stop:

| Metric | Conservative | Optimistic |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| New free listings per stop | 5 | 20 |
| $20 conversions within 30 days | 1 | 5 |
| New listings per full tour | 35 | 140 |

Over four tours per year, the Snowbird Circuit adds 140–560 new directory listings annually — without a single sales call. The tour IS the sales channel.

## The Snowbird Audience

The circuit is named for the demographic it serves. Snowbirds — retirees and seasonal travelers who move south during winter months — represent a high-value audience for corridor tourism:

| Characteristic | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Average age | 55–75 |
| Average household income | $75,000–120,000 |
| Travel spending per trip | $2,500–5,000 |
| Preferred entertainment | Live music, dining, cultural tourism |
| Geographic origin | Midwest, Northeast, Canada |

These travelers follow routes. The Natchez Trace Parkway, the Great River Road, and Interstate 55 all run through the corridor. A touring circuit that moves along the same route provides a reason to follow the music from city to city — booking hotel rooms, eating at restaurants, and shopping at businesses listed on the directory.

The tour does not just sell tickets. It sells the corridor.

## Implementation

### Phase 1: Proof of Concept (Months 1–6)
- Book three-city pilot run (Natchez, Vicksburg, Clarksdale)
- Use rented van, not Prevost (test the route before committing to bus lease)
- Record all shows; publish to Radio and streaming platforms
- Document per-stop economics to validate projections

### Phase 2: Full Circuit (Months 7–12)
- Expand to seven-city route
- Lease Prevost for first full run
- Install mobile recording and broadcast equipment
- Establish venue relationships in all seven cities
- Run two full circuits

### Phase 3: Recurring Operation (Year 2+)
- Six tours per year on established circuit
- Add satellite routes (Jackson spur, Delta loop, Gulf Coast extension)
- Establish standing venue deals with guaranteed minimums
- Integrate tour calendar with directory and Magazine editorial calendar
