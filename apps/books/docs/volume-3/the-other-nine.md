# Chapter 10: The Other Nine

## Overview

The corridor towns profiled in Chapters 5-9 — Clarksdale, Vicksburg, Greenville, Tupelo, and Oxford — represent the first wave of deployment. This chapter profiles the next nine towns in the corridor expansion: Meridian, Hattiesburg, Laurel, McComb, Brookhaven, Ocean Springs, Bay St. Louis, Shreveport, and El Dorado. Each follows the same adoption pattern: directory first, $20 upgrades, CVB adoption, civic pilot, touring circuit, radio features, magazine city guide. The differences are in scale, entry point, and local conditions.

## The Standard Adoption Pattern

Every town in the corridor follows a six-stage deployment sequence. The stages overlap — they are not strictly sequential — but the order is consistent:

| Stage | Action | Timeline |
|-------|--------|----------|
| 1. Directory launch | First 10-15 free listings through in-person demos | Weeks 1-2 |
| 2. $20 conversions | Photo demos and referral reveal drive first paid subscribers | Weeks 3-8 |
| 3. CVB/chamber adoption | Content partnership replaces or supplements agency spend | Months 2-4 |
| 4. Civic pilot | CivicX proposal to city government, grant-funded | Months 3-6 |
| 5. Touring circuit | Rise Up adds the town as a performance stop | Months 4-8 |
| 6. Media integration | Radio features local artists; Magazine publishes city guide | Months 1-6 (ongoing) |

The timeline compresses as the corridor matures. The first deployment (Clarksdale) takes 6-8 months to reach stage 4. The ninth deployment (El Dorado) may reach stage 4 in 3-4 months because the proof base is deeper, the content network is larger, and the CVB/chamber conversations reference eight previous deployments instead of one.

## Meridian

**Population: 33,627 | County: Lauderdale | State: Mississippi**

Meridian is the second-largest city in eastern Mississippi and the birthplace of Jimmie Rodgers, the "Father of Country Music." The Jimmie Rodgers Museum draws country music fans, but the broader downtown economy does not capture them effectively.

Meridian's key asset is its geographic position: it sits at the intersection of I-20 and I-59, making it a natural stopping point between Jackson, Birmingham, and Tuscaloosa. Approximately 25,000 vehicles pass through daily on these interstates. The directory's primary value is capturing search traffic from travelers — "where to eat in Meridian" and "things to do in Meridian MS" — and routing them to downtown businesses.

The MSU-Meridian campus (Mississippi State satellite) provides a student population of approximately 1,500, adding foot traffic and a younger demographic. The campus also offers a potential institutional deployment for the platform.

**Directory adoption projections:** 45 free listings in year one, 10 at $20, 1 at $99. The CVB partnership targets $18,000-24,000/year. CivicX is proposed to the city government with an estimated $30,000/year savings on administrative processes.

**Rise Up fit:** Meridian's musical heritage (Jimmie Rodgers, plus a significant gospel tradition) supports a 300-500 capacity show at the MSU Riley Center or Temple Theatre. Country and gospel talent search components draw from eastern Mississippi churches and music programs.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $26,000-32,000 (directory + CVB + CivicX pilot).

## Hattiesburg

**Population: 48,602 | County: Forrest/Lamar | State: Mississippi**

Hattiesburg is the second-largest town in the corridor by population (after Shreveport) and home to the University of Southern Mississippi (enrollment: ~14,000). The city has a more diversified economy than most corridor towns: healthcare (Forrest General Hospital), military (Camp Shelby, one of the largest National Guard training sites), and education.

Hattiesburg's directory adoption faces the same sophistication challenge as Tupelo and Oxford — business owners have existing digital tools. The entry point is the university and the CVB, not direct-to-business demos.

Camp Shelby's significance: approximately 100,000 service members train at Camp Shelby annually. These service members spend money in Hattiesburg — restaurants, hotels, entertainment — but are currently guided by word of mouth and Google Maps. The directory's Hattiesburg page, optimized for "things to do near Camp Shelby," captures a market segment that no local business association currently serves.

The Hub City (Hattiesburg's nickname, from its railroad junction history) has an active downtown with the Hattiesburg Arts Council, Saenger Theater, and a growing restaurant scene. The Arts Council is a natural partner for Rise Up programming.

**Directory adoption projections:** 55 free listings in year one, 13 at $20, 2 at $99. CVB partnership: $24,000-36,000/year. Institutional potential: University of Southern Mississippi campus services or Forrest County Schools (enrollment: ~4,200).

**Rise Up fit:** The Saenger Theater (capacity: 1,000+) is the premium venue. Hattiesburg's proximity to New Orleans (110 miles) means the talent pipeline draws from both Mississippi and Louisiana artists.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $35,000-48,000 (directory + CVB + potential institutional).

## Laurel

**Population: 18,540 | County: Jones | State: Mississippi**

Laurel is the HGTV town. The television show *Home Town*, featuring Ben and Erin Napier, has aired since 2016 and has transformed Laurel from a declining lumber town into a renovation tourism destination. The show has been documented to increase Laurel's tourism by an estimated 300-400% since its premiere.

This presents a specific directory opportunity: Laurel already has a tourism audience. The audience arrives, watches filming locations, walks downtown, and asks "what else is there?" The directory answers that question.

The Lauren Rogers Museum of Art — the first art museum in Mississippi, opened 1923 — adds a cultural dimension beyond the HGTV brand. The museum's permanent collection includes European and American paintings, Georgian silver, and Native American baskets.

Laurel's risk: dependence on a single television show. If *Home Town* ends or declines in popularity, Laurel's tourism drops with it. The directory helps Laurel build a digital presence independent of the show — a comprehensive town listing that ranks in search engines on its own merits, not solely on HGTV association.

**Directory adoption projections:** 35 free listings in year one, 8 at $20, 1 at $99. CVB partnership: $12,000-18,000/year. The *Home Town* tourism creates higher-than-average foot traffic for the business base.

**Rise Up fit:** Downtown Laurel has event spaces suitable for 200-capacity shows. The HGTV audience skews female, 35-55 — a demographic that responds well to gospel and Americana programming.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $16,000-22,000 (directory + CVB).

## McComb

**Population: 12,465 | County: Pike | State: Mississippi**

McComb is the birthplace of Bo Diddley and a significant site in the civil rights movement (the first SNCC voter registration drive in Mississippi, 1961). The town sits on I-55, the direct route between Jackson and New Orleans, and is a natural fueling stop.

McComb's economy is constrained: median household income of $28,734, poverty rate above 30%. The town resembles Greenville in its economic profile — high cultural value, low economic capture. The directory and CivicX deployment follows the Greenville model: word-of-mouth adoption, grant-funded civic pilot, emphasis on the local multiplier effect.

McComb's railroad heritage (the town was built by the Illinois Central Railroad in 1872) provides additional tourism positioning. The railroad depot has been restored as a cultural center.

**Directory adoption projections:** 25 free listings in year one, 5 at $20. CVB/chamber partnership: $6,000-12,000/year. CivicX pilot: $9,600/year with estimated $25,000 savings.

**Rise Up fit:** Small venue (150-200 capacity). The Bo Diddley heritage makes McComb a symbolically important tour stop — the birthplace of a rock and roll pioneer hosting a new talent search.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $10,000-16,000 (directory + chamber + CivicX).

## Brookhaven

**Population: 12,520 | County: Lincoln | State: Mississippi**

Brookhaven is the Lincoln County seat, located on I-55 between Jackson and McComb. The town's economy is centered on small manufacturing, healthcare (King's Daughters Medical Center), and agriculture. It does not have a dominant tourism draw, which makes it a useful test case: can the directory create economic value in a town that is not a destination?

The answer depends on the I-55 traffic. Brookhaven sits between Jackson (60 miles north) and McComb (30 miles south) on one of Mississippi's busiest interstates. The directory captures "where to stop on I-55" search traffic and routes it to Brookhaven's restaurants and shops.

Brookhaven's downtown has undergone incremental renovation in recent years. The Brookhaven Little Theatre (established 1959) and a growing restaurant scene provide modest cultural infrastructure.

**Directory adoption projections:** 25 free listings in year one, 5 at $20. Chamber partnership: $6,000-10,000/year. CivicX: likely year 2 deployment, following McComb's pilot results.

**Rise Up fit:** Small venue (150-200 capacity). Brookhaven functions as a connecting stop between Natchez and Jackson on the touring route rather than a headlining destination.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $8,000-14,000 (directory + chamber).

## Ocean Springs

**Population: 18,302 | County: Jackson | State: Mississippi**

Ocean Springs is the wealthiest town in the corridor by median household income ($55,618) and the most artistically concentrated. The Walter Anderson Museum of Art — dedicated to the visionary artist Walter Inglis Anderson (1903-1965) — anchors a gallery district that includes 15+ working galleries and studios along Government Street.

Ocean Springs has what most corridor towns lack: an existing creative economy with paying customers. The galleries sell work. The restaurants draw diners from Biloxi, Gulfport, and Mobile. The Peter Anderson Arts & Crafts Festival (held annually in November, named for Walter Anderson's brother Peter) draws an estimated 100,000-150,000 visitors over two days.

The directory in Ocean Springs serves a different function than in poorer towns. Here, the value is network intelligence and cross-promotion — connecting the galleries to the restaurants to the hotels through a referral network that currently operates informally. The formal tracking of referrals, endorsements, and collaborations adds measurable data to relationships that already exist.

**Directory adoption projections:** 40 free listings in year one, 10 at $20, 2 at $99. CVB partnership: $18,000-24,000/year. The Gulf Coast tourism infrastructure is more developed here; the CVB expects professional-grade tools.

**Rise Up fit:** Outdoor stage or gallery courtyard (200-300 capacity). The Ocean Springs audience is arts-oriented; the show format emphasizes the artistic quality of the performance over the talent search spectacle.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $22,000-30,000 (directory + CVB).

## Bay St. Louis

**Population: 13,216 | County: Hancock | State: Mississippi**

Bay St. Louis is a Gulf Coast arts town that was devastated by Hurricane Katrina in 2005 and rebuilt as a creative community. The Old Town district features galleries, restaurants, and boutiques. The town hosts Second Saturday Art Walks and multiple arts festivals.

Bay St. Louis is smaller than Ocean Springs but shares its arts-district DNA. The directory deployment mirrors Ocean Springs with adjustments for scale:

| Factor | Ocean Springs | Bay St. Louis |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Population | 18,302 | 13,216 |
| Gallery count | 15+ | 10-12 |
| Median income | $55,618 | $42,981 |
| Festival draw | 100,000-150,000 | 30,000-50,000 (estimated) |
| Directory approach | Network intelligence | Network intelligence (smaller scale) |

Bay St. Louis's beach access and its proximity to New Orleans (60 miles east) make it a weekend destination for New Orleans residents. The directory captures the "day trip from New Orleans" search market — a segment that currently defaults to generic travel blogs.

**Directory adoption projections:** 30 free listings in year one, 7 at $20, 1 at $99. CVB/chamber partnership: $12,000-18,000/year.

**Rise Up fit:** Beach stage or Old Town outdoor venue (200-300 capacity). A Bay St. Louis show positioned as a beach concert with a talent search draws the New Orleans weekend crowd.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $14,000-20,000 (directory + chamber).

## Shreveport

**Population: 187,593 | County: Caddo Parish | State: Louisiana**

Shreveport is an order of magnitude larger than every other town in this chapter. It is included in the corridor expansion because of two factors: the Municipal Auditorium (where Elvis, Johnny Cash, and Hank Williams performed on the Louisiana Hayride) and the city's role as the gateway to the western corridor.

Shreveport does not follow the standard small-town deployment. It is too large for a single boots-on-the-ground campaign. The entry points are institutional:

| Entry point | Target | Rationale |
|-------------|--------|-----------|
| Municipal Auditorium | Venue partnership for Rise Up | Historical significance, available for booking |
| Caddo Parish Schools | District of ~30,000 students | CivicX institutional deployment |
| Shreveport-Bossier CVB | CVB with substantial budget | Content volume and quality upgrade |
| Downtown Development Authority | Business improvement district | Directory for downtown businesses |

Shreveport's casino industry (six casino properties between Shreveport and Bossier City) parallels Vicksburg — the casino economy operates independently, and the directory targets non-casino businesses.

**Directory adoption projections:** Targeted deployment in downtown only — not city-wide. 60 free listings in year one, 15 at $20, 3 at $99. CVB partnership: $36,000-60,000/year (larger budget, larger expectations). Caddo Parish Schools: institutional license at $72,000-120,000/year (30,000 students, E-Rate eligible).

**Rise Up fit:** The Municipal Auditorium (capacity: 3,000) is the signature venue. A Rise Up show at the Municipal Auditorium — the stage where the Louisiana Hayride launched Elvis — is the corridor's highest-profile performance. This show is a year 2 or 3 event, after Rise Up has established its reputation through smaller venues.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $50,000-80,000 (directory + CVB). Institutional revenue from Caddo Parish Schools, if secured, adds $72,000-120,000 in year 2.

## El Dorado

**Population: 17,826 | County: Union | State: Arkansas**

El Dorado is the corridor's northern anchor in Arkansas, located 120 miles north of Natchez. The Murphy Arts District (MAD), opened in 2017, is a $100 million investment that includes an amphitheater, music hall, a repurposed movie theater, and green spaces. MAD was funded by the Murphy Oil Corporation (now Delek US) and represents the largest private arts investment in the region.

El Dorado is the inverse of Greenville: it has infrastructure without population. MAD can host 8,000+ people in its outdoor amphitheater, but El Dorado has fewer than 18,000 residents. The facility needs an audience pipeline, and the corridor provides one.

The directory in El Dorado connects MAD programming to the corridor's touring network. Rise Up performs at MAD. Big Muddy Radio broadcasts from MAD. The Magazine covers MAD events as corridor content. El Dorado becomes the northern anchor of the touring circuit — the last stop before Little Rock and the first stop when heading south.

**Directory adoption projections:** 30 free listings in year one, 6 at $20, 1 at $99. Partnership with MAD: $12,000-24,000/year. The MAD relationship is more important than the CVB relationship in El Dorado — the arts district IS the town's tourism infrastructure.

**Rise Up fit:** MAD Music Hall (capacity: ~500) for intimate shows. MAD Amphitheater (capacity: 8,000+) for a corridor-wide festival event in year 3 or later.

**Estimated year 1 platform revenue:** $16,000-28,000 (directory + MAD partnership).

## Implementation

The nine towns in this chapter do not deploy simultaneously. The deployment sequence follows a geographic and strategic logic:

| Phase | Towns | Timeline | Rationale |
|-------|-------|----------|-----------|
| Phase 1 | McComb, Brookhaven | Months 1-3 | I-55 corridor between Natchez and Jackson; proximity to home base |
| Phase 2 | Meridian, Hattiesburg | Months 3-6 | Eastern Mississippi; larger towns with institutional potential |
| Phase 3 | Laurel | Month 4-6 | Between Hattiesburg and Meridian; HGTV audience overlap |
| Phase 4 | Ocean Springs, Bay St. Louis | Months 6-9 | Gulf Coast cluster; deployed together for efficiency |
| Phase 5 | El Dorado | Months 8-10 | Northern anchor; MAD partnership anchors the deployment |
| Phase 6 | Shreveport | Months 10-14 | Largest market; requires institutional entry and longer runway |
