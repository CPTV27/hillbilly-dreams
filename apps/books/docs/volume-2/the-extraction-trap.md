# Chapter 2: The Extraction Trap

## Overview

In a typical U.S. small town (population 5,000-15,000, median household income ~$38,000), eighty-two cents of every dollar earned by a resident leaves the local economy within thirty days. This chapter documents where that money goes, why the outflow is accelerating, and presents the arithmetic for reversing it. The extraction trap is not a poverty problem. It is a structural plumbing problem: the pipe runs one direction, and it runs out.

---

## The 82% Extraction Rate

Analysis of money flows across eleven small towns along the Mississippi corridor (populations 5,000-15,000) yields a consistent finding: 82% of earned income exits the local economy within thirty days through non-negotiable spending channels.

The breakdown by category:

| Category | % of Income | Destination |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Housing (rent/mortgage) | 30-35% | REITs, out-of-state investors, Charlotte/New York bank HQs |
| Groceries and retail | 15-20% | Bentonville AR (Walmart), Goodlettsville TN (Dollar General), Sysco/US Foods shareholders |
| Transportation | 10-15% | Houston (oil majors), Delaware/South Dakota (auto lenders), Hartford/Omaha (insurance) |
| Utilities and telecom | 8-10% | Regulated monopoly shareholders, major metro telecom HQs |
| Debt service | 10-15% | Citibank, JPMorgan, Capital One, federal loan servicers |
| **Total extraction range** | **73-95%** | |
| **Midpoint** | **82%** | |

The data is consistent across the eleven-town sample. The variation (73-95%) correlates primarily with the presence or absence of locally owned businesses and community banks.

## The Dollar's Journey: Two Paths

The local multiplier effect — a standard concept in regional economics — measures how many times a dollar changes hands within a community before exiting. The contrast between extractive and local spending paths illustrates why the multiplier matters.

### Path A: Extractive Channel

A worker earns $1.00 at a locally situated but externally owned manufacturing plant. The worker spends it at a gas station. The station owner retains approximately $0.02 profit on fuel. The remaining $0.98 splits among the oil company, distributor, credit card processor, and taxes. Total local transactions: one. Total local economic impact: negligible.

### Path B: Local Channel

A worker earns $1.00 and spends it at a neighbor's farm stand. The farm stand operator retains $0.85 (costs are primarily personal labor and local inputs). The operator spends that $0.85 at a local hardware store. The hardware store owner spends the margin at a local diner. The diner owner pays a local worker.

Same dollar. Four transactions. Four people touched. Four relationships reinforced.

**A dollar that circulates three times locally performs the economic work of three dollars earned. A dollar that exits on the first transaction performs the economic work of zero dollars.**

The core diagnosis: most small towns are not under-earning. They are under-circulating.

## Accelerating Factors

Three structural trends are increasing the extraction rate.

### 1. E-Commerce as Siphon

E-commerce represented 11% of U.S. retail sales in 2019. By 2025, the figure exceeds 22% and continues to climb. Each percentage point represents billions of dollars rerouted from local retail to centralized fulfillment operations. Every online order that replaces a local purchase is a dollar permanently removed from local circulation.

### 2. Banking Consolidation

The United States had 15,000 banks in 1990. Approximately 4,100 remain as of 2025. Community banks — institutions that made lending decisions based on local knowledge — have been absorbed into regional and national chains that optimize for shareholder return. When a local bank is acquired, lending decisions migrate to offices hundreds of miles away. The replacement algorithm has no model for the borrower's community context.

### 3. The Franchise Extraction Model

A locally owned restaurant retains approximately $0.65 of every dollar in the local economy. A franchise retains approximately $0.35 — the difference exits through franchise fees, mandatory supplier agreements, and corporate marketing fund contributions.

| Business Type | Local Dollar Retention |
|--------------|----------------------|
| Locally owned restaurant | $0.65 |
| Franchise restaurant | $0.35 |
| Dollar General | < $0.20 (estimated) |

The franchise model is engineered from the ground up to move money out. Dollar General's site selection algorithm specifically targets communities with low income and limited shopping options — a fact documented in the company's investor presentations.

## The Extraction Loop

The extraction trap is self-reinforcing:

1. Check-cashing services charge 3% because banks left the community.
2. Banks left because the community could not generate sufficient deposits to justify a branch.
3. Deposits were insufficient because earned income exited through extractive spending channels.
4. The only available spending channels are chains whose profit structures route money to distant shareholders.

The loop operates without any single actor intending harm. Each participant — the check-cashing operator, the Dollar General, the departing bank — acts rationally within its own incentive structure. The systemic result is continuous wealth extraction from the community.

## The Reversal Arithmetic

The math for partial reversal is straightforward.

**Given:**
- Community population: 5,000
- Median household income: $38,000
- Total community income: $190,000,000/year
- Current extraction rate: 82%
- Annual extraction: $155,800,000

**If the extraction rate decreases by 10 percentage points (from 82% to 72%):**
- Additional local retention: $19,000,000/year
- At a conservative local multiplier of 1.5x: $28,500,000 in local economic activity

A 10-percentage-point shift in a community of 5,000 people generates $28.5 million in additional local economic activity annually. This requires no grant, no external investment, and no policy change. It requires redirecting spending that the community already generates.

## Drain-Closing Strategies

Four documented approaches reduce the extraction rate.

### 1. Local Procurement Policies

Municipal and county governments redirect purchasing to local suppliers, accepting a 5-10% price premium where necessary. The premium returns as tax revenue, local employment, and circulating dollars. Documented payback period: 18 months.

### 2. Community-Owned Utilities

Approximately 2,000 U.S. communities operate their own electric utilities. Their rates average 11% lower than investor-owned utilities, and revenue stays in the community rather than flowing to institutional shareholders. Several have used surpluses to fund municipal broadband — closing an additional extraction channel.

### 3. Quantified Buy-Local Programs

Programs that publish specific dollar-retention figures change consumer behavior measurably. The key data point: $100 spent at a local business returns $68 to the community versus $43 at a national chain. When residents see the receipts, purchasing patterns shift.

### 4. Direct Labor Exchange (Task Board Model)

When skills are exchanged directly — plumbing for bookkeeping, carpentry for tax preparation — no dollar enters or exits the transaction. The value is created and retained entirely within the network. The extraction rate on direct labor exchange is zero. This is not a marginal improvement on the 82% problem. It is a structural bypass of the extraction pipeline entirely.

## Implementation

1. **Conduct a personal extraction audit.** Review one month of bank and credit card statements. Categorize each transaction by the headquarters location of the receiving business. Calculate the percentage of spending that exits the local economy.

2. **Identify three substitutable purchases.** Select three recurring expenditures currently flowing to non-local businesses that could be sourced locally. Common candidates: eggs, coffee, bread, haircuts, basic repairs.

3. **Execute substitutions for 30 days.** Track spending shifts and note any changes in cost, quality, or convenience.

4. **Run the community extraction analysis.** Select a six-block radius. Estimate total income earned by residents within that radius. Trace spending destinations. Calculate the extraction rate. Compare to the 82% benchmark.

5. **Publish the findings.** Present extraction data at a public forum, community meeting, or local publication. Specific dollar figures change behavior more effectively than general exhortations to shop locally.
