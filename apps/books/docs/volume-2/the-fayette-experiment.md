# Chapter 16: The Fayette Experiment

## Overview

Fayette, Mississippi (population 1,580) is the county seat of Jefferson County. The town has no grocery store, no bank branch (the last closed in 2021), and a median household income of $19,000. The nearest full grocery store is 35 miles south in Natchez. This chapter documents a coordination protocol in which three CDL holders and a group of participating families eliminated over $193,000/year in food access costs using one used refrigerated trailer, a Google Sheet, and a group text thread. No grants were applied for. No nonprofit was formed. No external capital was required.

---

## The Problem: Food Access Cost in Jefferson County

### Transportation Cost of Groceries

| Data Point | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Distance to nearest full grocery store (Natchez) | 35 miles one way |
| Drive time (one way) | 42 minutes |
| Vehicle fuel efficiency (baseline) | 25 mpg |
| Gas price (baseline) | $3.40/gallon |
| Round-trip fuel cost | $9.52 |
| Average trips per household per month | 3.6 |
| Monthly transportation cost per household | ~$140 |
| Annual transportation cost per household | $1,680 |
| Estimated households in Jefferson County | ~600 |
| **Total annual county transportation cost for food access** | **~$1,000,000** |

Jefferson County residents spend approximately $1 million per year on gasoline solely to access groceries. This money leaves the county entirely.

### County-Level Extraction Rate

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total annual income in Jefferson County | ~$29 million |
| Estimated percentage leaving county within 30 days | 82% |
| Annual extraction | ~$23.8 million |

82 cents of every dollar earned in Jefferson County flows to out-of-county entities: groceries purchased in Natchez, gas stations owned by external companies, car payments to out-of-state lenders, phone bills, insurance premiums.

## The Protocol

### Assets Already Present

| Asset | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| CDL holder 1 | Drives a refrigerated truck for a food distributor. Passes Jefferson County weekly on regular route. |
| CDL holder 2 | Drives for a lumber company. Has commercial vehicle access. |
| CDL holder 3 | Operates a small hauling business. Owns a flatbed and a box truck (idle on weekends). |
| Wholesale access | Restaurant Depot in Jackson (90 miles northeast). Requires business license to shop — CDL holder 3 holds one for the hauling company. |
| Refrigerated trailer | Purchased used on Facebook Marketplace for $8,000. |

### Operating Model

1. 15 families commit to $75/week in group orders.
2. Orders submitted via Google Sheet by Monday night.
3. CDL crew drives to Restaurant Depot in Jackson on Wednesday.
4. Thursday afternoon: distribution at community center parking lot.

**Infrastructure:** One used refrigerated trailer ($8,000), one Google Sheet (free), one group text thread (free), three people with existing CDLs and trucks.

**Time to operational:** 3 weeks from kitchen-table planning to first delivery.

| Case of canned vegetables | $19.00 | $11.00 | 42% |
| **Average markup, staple groceries** | — | — | **35-50% (avg 38%)** |

### Per-Family Savings

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Weekly family spend | $75 |

| Weekly savings per family | $45.70 |

### Delivery Fee Structure

Each family pays a $15 delivery fee per week on top of the grocery order.

| Scale | Families | Annual Delivery Revenue | Per CDL Holder (split 3 ways) |
|-------|----------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Initial | 15 | $11,700 | $3,900 |
| Month 4 | 32 | $24,960 | $8,320 |
| Target | 40 | $31,200 | $10,400 |

Route cost is fixed regardless of family count. One truck, one trip. Revenue scales linearly with participation.

### Local Multiplier Effect

Retained dollars circulate locally. In low-income communities, the local multiplier on a retained dollar is between 1.5 and 2.0 — meaning each dollar spent at a local business generates $0.50-$1.00 in secondary economic activity.

$193,000 retained x 1.7 local multiplier = **$328,000 in total economic impact.**

## Emergent Effects

By month 6, the Thursday distribution became a micro-market:
- Participants began arriving early and staying late
- Home-produced goods appeared under Mississippi's cottage food law (allows direct sales of non-potentially-hazardous foods without a license)
- Documented examples: one participant earned $2,400 in 3 months selling pies; a backyard gardener moves $60-$80/week in greens with near-100% profit margins (input cost: seeds and water)

The distribution point created physical proximity. Proximity produced additional coordination without central planning.

## Implementation

### Step 1: Identify Logistics Assets
Locate community members with CDLs, commercial vehicles, or wholesale purchasing access. These individuals exist in most communities and are often unaware of the coordination opportunity their skills represent.

### Step 2: Calculate Extraction Cost
Determine the community's monthly gasoline expenditure solely for accessing basic goods. Multiply households by average monthly trips by round-trip fuel cost.

### Step 3: Build the Order Sheet
Create a spreadsheet with three columns:
- Column A: What families need
- Column B: Weekly dollar commitment per family
- Column C: Who can haul it

### Step 4: Set the Schedule
Select a consistent day for distribution. Weekly, same time, same place. Consistency drives participation.

### Step 5: Execute the First Run
Complete one wholesale purchasing trip and one distribution. Track savings against retail prices. Share the results with participants.
