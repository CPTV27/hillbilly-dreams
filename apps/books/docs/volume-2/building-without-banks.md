# Chapter 6: Building Without Banks

## Overview

Banks perform three functions: value storage, value transfer, and capital allocation. The U.S. banking industry generates $260 billion in annual revenue from consumers through fees and interest spreads for these services. A coordination network replicates all three functions without the intermediary and without the associated costs. This chapter details the mechanics of internal exchange systems, lending circles, community investment through labor, and hybrid cash-plus-time models.

---

## 6.1 The Three Functions of Banks

Banks provide exactly three services to consumers:

| Function | Bank Version | Network Version |
|---|---|---|
| **Value Storage** | Holds cash in accounts; charges ~$12/month maintenance fee | Stores value as member capacity (skills + available hours); no fee |
| **Value Transfer** | Moves money between parties; charges wire fees, 3% credit card surcharges, processing delays | Direct skill exchange between members; no fees, no delays |
| **Capital Allocation** | Loan committees decide who receives funding; requires 3 years revenue history, collateral, personal guarantee | Task board matches visible needs to available skills; decisions made by participants |

Banks do not create value. They intermediate between parties who have resources and parties who need them, charging both sides. A coordination network eliminates the intermediary.

For external transactions (taxes, mortgages, medical bills), banks remain necessary. For internal needs met by network members, the bank is an unnecessary intermediary.

## 6.2 Internal Exchange Rate Models

The most common objection to skill-based exchange: market rates differ across skills. A plumber commands $95/hour on the open market; a babysitter commands $18/hour. Three models resolve this problem.

### Model 1: Hour-for-Hour

One hour of labor equals one hour of labor, regardless of skill type.

- **Rationale:** The network is not a market. Exchange rates are set by mutual need, not market comparison. No money changes hands, so market rate is irrelevant.
- **Best for:** Small networks, 5-20 members.
- **Infrastructure required:** Basic log (notebook or spreadsheet).
- **Advantage:** Forces equal valuation of all members' time. Simple to administer.

### Model 2: Points Bank

Members earn points for hours contributed and spend points for hours received. One hour = one point. Tracked on a shared spreadsheet.

- **Best for:** Medium networks, 15-50 members.
- **Infrastructure required:** Shared spreadsheet with a designated ledger-keeper.
- **Advantage over Model 1:** Eliminates the need for simultaneous swaps. Members bank hours in one period and spend in another.
- **Critical rule:** Points expire after 90 days. This prevents hoarding and ensures active contribution. In practice, expiration rarely triggers because members with banked points use them.

### Model 3: Tiered Rates

Different skill categories earn different point values per hour.

| Tier | Examples | Points per Hour |
|---|---|---|
| Specialized | Electrical, plumbing, legal advice | 1.5 |
| General | Cleaning, hauling, yard work | 1.0 |

- **Best for:** Larger networks where specialists report feeling undervalued.
- **Risk:** Reintroduces market hierarchy. Creates disputes over tier classification.
- **Constraint:** Limit to two tiers maximum. Three or more tiers recreate payroll complexity.

### Model Selection Guide

| Network Size | Recommended Model | Upgrade Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| 5-20 members | Hour-for-Hour | Specialists leaving due to perceived inequity |
| 15-50 members | Points Bank | Need for asynchronous exchange |
| 50+ members | Tiered Rates (2 tiers max) | Points Bank failing to retain high-skill members |

Most networks never need to move beyond hour-for-hour.

## 6.3 The Lending Circle

A points bank enables lending. A member who needs a burst of labor beyond their current point balance receives a "loan" from the network: other members perform work now, and the borrower repays in contributed hours over 60-90 days.

**Mechanics:**

1. Member requests labor beyond current point balance.
2. Network members perform the work.
3. Borrower contributes equivalent hours to the network within 60-90 days.
4. No interest. No collateral requirement. No credit check.

**Collateral mechanism:** Reputation. In a 20-person network, defaulting on a labor loan creates a relationship rupture with people who interact daily. Social collateral cannot be discharged in bankruptcy.

**Historical precedents:**

| Name | Region | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Tanda | Mexico | Rotating savings and lending circle |
| Susu | West Africa | Pooled contributions with rotating payouts |
| Hui | China | Group lending based on social trust |

These systems have operated for centuries across cultures. A coordination network with a points bank applies the same principle with a tracking spreadsheet.

## 6.4 Community Investment Through Labor

A mature network (60+ members with a functioning points bank) possesses collective labor capacity of hundreds of hours per month. This capacity functions as a community development engine.

**Example: Community Workshop Build**

| Approach | External Cost | Labor Source | Value Retained Locally |
|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional (cash-funded) | $40,000+ | Hired contractors | 0% of labor value |
| Network (labor-funded) | ~$3,000 (materials + rent) | 115 hours coordinated member labor | 100% of labor value |

**Labor breakdown for network approach:**

| Task | Hours |
|---|---|
| Workbench construction | 40 |
| Electrical work | 20 |
| Tool drive organization | 10 |
| Bookkeeping and insurance paperwork | 15 |
| Cleanup and painting | 30 |
| **Total** | **115** |

The external cost covers only materials and rent that cannot be sourced internally. All labor investment stays within the network.

## 6.5 The Hybrid Model

Pure time-based economies cannot cover all needs. Materials, taxes, and external services require cash. Effective networks operate a hybrid: time-based exchange for labor, shared purchasing for materials.

### Shared Purchasing

The network pools cash for bulk material purchases.

| Item | Individual Price | Bulk (20 households) | Typical Savings |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lumber | Retail | Contractor pricing | 15-25% |
| Heating fuel | Standard rate | Group rate | Varies by region |
| Internet service | Individual plan | Group negotiation | Varies by provider |
| Auto parts | Retail | Wholesale | 10-20% |

### Community Fund

A small monthly dues structure funds shared expenses.

| Dues per Member | Members | Monthly Fund | Annual Fund |
|---|---|---|---|
| $25 | 50 | $1,250 | $15,000 |
| $50 | 50 | $2,500 | $30,000 |

**Fund uses:** Bulk material purchases, shared infrastructure costs (workshop rent, tool inventory), emergency loans.

**Requirements:** A checking account and member agreement. No grant application, board of directors, or 501(c)(3) required.

## Implementation Steps

1. Select an exchange model based on current network size (see Model Selection Guide in Section 6.2).
2. Create a shared tracking spreadsheet with columns: Date, Provider, Recipient, Hours, Skill Type, Status.
3. Establish the 90-day point expiration rule if using a Points Bank.
4. Identify 3-5 immediate skill matches from the existing inventory and execute exchanges within the first week.
5. Propose a shared purchasing arrangement for one commonly purchased item (heating fuel, cleaning supplies, coffee).
6. Execute the first bulk purchase and calculate dollar savings.
7. Report savings to all members.
8. After 90 days of successful exchange tracking, introduce lending circle rules for labor-based loans.
9. If monthly dues are adopted, open a shared checking account for the community fund.
