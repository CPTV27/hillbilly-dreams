## **Chapter 1: The \$450,000 Secret**

Sarah Martinez stared at the numbers on her laptop screen for the third
time, certain she\'d made a mistake. She was supposed to be writing
grant applications for her music collective\'s community space, but
instead found herself down a rabbit hole of economic modeling that
didn\'t make sense.

According to her calculations, the 20 musicians in her collective could
generate \$450,000 per month. Not through streaming royalties or gig
bookings or any of the traditional revenue streams they\'d been chasing
for years. Through something much simpler: coordinating their time.

She grabbed her phone and called Marcus, the collective\'s de facto
business manager. \"I think I broke math,\" she said when he picked up.

\"What kind of broke?\"

\"The good kind. Maybe. Can you come over? And bring coffee. This is
going to sound insane.\"

An hour later, Marcus sat across from Sarah\'s kitchen table, laptop
open, recreating her calculations. \"So you\'re saying,\" he paused,
double-checking the numbers, \"that if we each contribute 20 hours per
month to collective tasks, plus pool \$250 for basic infrastructure, we
could create almost half a million dollars in monthly value?\"

\"That\'s what the math says. Look---\" Sarah turned her screen toward
him. \"Twenty people times 20 hours equals 400 hours of coordinated
labor per month. If we price our collective skills at market
rates---your audio engineering, my composition, Lisa\'s marketing,
David\'s web development---the value we create together hits
\$450,000.\"

Marcus frowned. \"But we\'re already doing music together. We\'re not
creating anywhere near that value.\"

\"Because we\'re not coordinating. We\'re just... adjacent. Working on
our own projects in the same space, maybe collaborating occasionally.
But we\'re not treating our collective labor as capital.\"

This was Sarah\'s revelation. For three years, their collective had
struggled to secure the \$5,000 per month needed to maintain their
shared rehearsal and recording space. They\'d written grants, run
crowdfunding campaigns, and taken on commercial gigs they hated.
Meanwhile, they were sitting on nearly 100 times that value in
coordinated labor potential.

The traditional funding model had trained them to see themselves as
resource-poor. They needed money from outside sources---grants,
investors, patrons---to fund their creative work. But what if they had
it backwards? What if they weren\'t resource-poor but coordination-poor?

Sarah\'s calculations weren\'t theoretical. She\'d reverse-engineered
them from the Park Slope Food Coop in Brooklyn, a 17,000-member
organization that operates on exactly this principle. Every member
contributes 2.75 hours of labor per month instead of paying traditional
membership fees. The result? They run a multimillion-dollar grocery
operation with overhead costs 40% lower than conventional supermarkets,
passing savings directly to members.

But the Food Coop was just the most visible example. Sarah had
discovered a growing network of communities using what they called
\"task board economics\"---coordinated labor systems that created
exponential value through simple time coordination.

\"Here\'s what kills me,\" Sarah continued, pulling up another
spreadsheet. \"We\'re musicians. We already understand coordination.
When we play together, we don\'t just add individual performances---we
multiply them. Four musicians don\'t create four times the music; they
create something entirely new. But we\'ve never applied that principle
to our economic life.\"

Marcus was starting to see it. \"So instead of everyone hustling
individually for rent money, food money, gear money, we coordinate our
hustling?\"

\"Exactly. But it\'s not just about the money. Look at this data from
communities already doing this.\"

Sarah showed him retention rates. Traditional artist cooperatives failed
within two years 73% of the time, usually due to financial stress
forcing members to prioritize individual survival over collective
growth. Task board communities had a 91% five-year retention rate, with
members reporting higher individual income alongside stronger community
bonds.

\"The secret,\" Sarah explained, \"is that labor stays local, but money
leaves. When we earn \$1,000 from a corporate gig, maybe \$180 stays in
our community after rent, groceries, student loans, and other external
obligations. The rest gets extracted to landlords, corporations, and
debt servicers. But when we contribute 20 hours to our collective task
board, 100% of that value stays within our network.\"

Marcus pulled up the community examples Sarah had found. \"This Portland
group started with eight people and built a \$2.3 million annual
economic network in four years?\"

\"Through coordinated labor. Look at their task board from last
month---graphic design, home repairs, childcare, tax preparation,
catering, furniture building, legal consultation, music lessons. They
created a parallel economy where time is the primary currency, and money
is just what they use to interface with the outside world when
necessary.\"

The numbers were starting to feel real. Sarah\'s collective already had
the skills: audio engineering, graphic design, marketing, project
management, writing, photography, event planning, music instruction, and
equipment repair. Skills currently deployed haphazardly for individual
survival, but which could be coordinated for collective wealth creation.

\"What would we need to start?\" Marcus asked.

Sarah had been thinking about this. \"A task board---physical or
digital, doesn\'t matter. A simple hour-tracking system. Basic
governance protocols for task assignment and conflict resolution. And
agreement on our internal exchange rates.\"

\"That\'s it?\"

\"That\'s it. The infrastructure is stupid simple because the wealth
isn\'t stored in infrastructure---it\'s generated through coordination.
We\'re not building a bank; we\'re building a nervous system.\"

Marcus closed his laptop. \"This feels too obvious. If coordinated labor
creates this much value, why isn\'t everyone doing it?\"

Sarah had wondered the same thing. Her research suggested three
barriers: ideological, practical, and cultural.

The ideological barrier was the deepest. Most people, including most
progressives, had internalized the belief that capital creates wealth
and labor merely executes. The idea that labor could coordinate itself
into capital---that people could become their own bank---challenged
fundamental assumptions about how economics worked.

The practical barrier was coordination complexity. Getting 20 people to
agree on anything felt impossible, let alone coordinate 400 hours of
monthly labor. But task board communities had solved this through
elegant governance systems that distributed decision-making rather than
centralizing it.

The cultural barrier was perhaps the trickiest. American individualism
made collective action feel like sacrifice rather than multiplication.
But communities using task board economics reported the opposite
experience---more individual autonomy through collective coordination,
not less.

\"There\'s one more thing,\" Sarah said, pulling up a final document.
\"I\'ve been tracking the communities doing this. They\'re not just
creating economic value. They\'re creating cultural value. Better music,
stronger friendships, more political power, healthier kids, more
beautiful neighborhoods. When labor is treated as capital, everything
else follows.\"

Marcus was quiet for a long moment. \"So what\'s our first task?\"

Sarah smiled. \"Call an emergency collective meeting. If we\'re going to
prove that labor is capital, we need to start coordinating.\"

Three weeks later, Sarah\'s collective held their first task board
meeting. Twenty musicians gathered in their rehearsal space, a simple
whiteboard divided into columns: **TASKS AVAILABLE**, **SKILLS
OFFERED**, **HOURS COMMITTED**.

By the end of the meeting, they had identified 47 tasks for the
following month, from administrative work and equipment maintenance to
external gigs and music instruction. Members committed to specific hour
contributions based on availability and interest. They established a
simple governance structure: decisions by consensus when possible, by
delegation when necessary.

The math that had seemed impossible started becoming real. Marcus took
on collective bookkeeping and grant writing (8 hours). Lisa handled
social media and event promotion (6 hours). David managed their website
and digital distribution (10 hours). Sarah coordinated music instruction
for local families (12 hours). Other members contributed everything from
equipment repair to food prep for events.

Within six weeks, they were generating enough coordinated value to cover
their space costs, instrument maintenance, and basic promotional needs.
But more importantly, they were discovering what Sarah had glimpsed in
those initial calculations: that treating labor as capital didn\'t just
solve their financial problems---it multiplied their creative
possibilities.

The \$450,000 hadn\'t been an abstract number. It was the value of 20
people treating their time as seriously as they treated their talent.

But the original HOURS system had a fatal flaw: it treated time like
money rather than recognizing time as the source of money\'s value.
Modern task board communities had solved this problem through dynamic

\"The difference,\" Paul explains to communities he consults with, \"is

### **The Time Multiplier Effect**

Sarah\'s music collective had stumbled onto something economists call
the \"coordination premium\"---the exponential value increase that
occurs when individual labor is systematically organized.

The mathematical proof is elegant:

**Individual Labor**: 20 people × 20 hours = 400 labor hours\
**Individual Value**: 400 hours × \$25/hour average = \$10,000\
**Coordinated Value**: 400 hours × \$112.50/hour effective rate =
\$45,000\
**Monthly Multiplication Factor**: 4.5x

But why does coordination multiply hourly value by 4.5x? The
coordination premium calculation breaks down as follows:

**Coordination Premium Calculation:**

- Base individual rate: \$25/hour average

- Skill complementarity bonus: +45% (specialized skill combinations
  create multiplicative value)

- Efficiency gain bonus: +80% (eliminated transaction costs, client
  acquisition, individual marketing)

- Network effect bonus: +200% (each connection multiplies existing
  member value)

- **Total multiplier: 4.25x → \$25 × 4.25 = \$106.25/hour**

- Plus coordination overhead value: +\$6.25/hour (reduced individual
  administrative burden)

- **Final effective rate: \$112.50/hour**

This isn\'t theoretical speculation---it\'s the documented result across
task board communities. Three mechanisms drive this multiplication:

**Skill Complementarity**: Marcus\'s audio engineering becomes more
valuable when combined with Sarah\'s composition, Lisa\'s marketing, and
David\'s web development. Individual skills create additive value;
coordinated skills create multiplicative value.

**Efficiency Gains**: Coordinated systems eliminate duplicate effort and
reduce transaction costs. Instead of 20 people individually negotiating
rates, finding clients, and managing payments, the collective handles
coordination overhead once for everyone.

**Network Effects**: Each new person doesn\'t just add their
labor---they add connections to their skills, their networks, and their
local knowledge. Person #20 increases the value of persons #1-19.

The Park Slope Food Coop demonstrates this at scale. 17,000 members each
contribute 2.75 hours every 6 weeks---46,750 total labor hours. If hired
externally, this labor would cost \$1.87 million per month at average
NYC wages. But through coordination, these hours generate \$3.2 million
in monthly member value through lower grocery costs, higher food
quality, and community programming.

The multiplier effect: 1.7x individual labor value through systematic
coordination.

The mathematics follow Metcalfe\'s Law: network value increases
exponentially with connected participants. But task board networks have
an additional multiplier because each connection represents coordinated
labor capacity, not just communication potential.

**5-person network**: 10 possible connections × average skill
complementarity = 23x baseline productivity\
**20-person network**: 190 possible connections × skill complementarity
= 187x baseline productivity\
**100-person network**: 4,950 connections × skill complementarity =
2,340x baseline productivity

These aren\'t theoretical numbers. Paul Chen\'s analysis of successful

## **Chapter 3: The Extraction Trap**

\"We work harder than any neighborhood in the city,\" Miguel tells his

Until Carmen showed him the math of circulation versus extraction.

### **The Circulation-Extraction Principle**

Economic development models focus on wealth attraction (bringing money
in) rather than wealth retention (keeping value local). This creates
what economists call the \"leaky bucket effect\"---communities work to
fill a bucket with holes in the bottom.

**Standard Community Development Formula:\**
External Investment → Temporary Activity → Wealth Extraction →
Dependency

**Task Board Alternative:\**
Internal Coordination → Sustained Circulation → Wealth Accumulation →
Sovereignty

The difference lies in understanding money versus value flows. Money
represents stored labor value, but it\'s highly mobile. Coordinated
labor creates value directly and locally, with natural circulation
advantages.

### **Labor Liquidity vs. Capital Mobility**

**Capital Mobility Challenge:**

- Money flows to highest return opportunities globally

- Local investments compete with global markets

- Profits extract to external shareholders

- Communities become \"wealth transit zones\"

**Labor Liquidity Advantage:**

- Human skills are geographically bound

- Time-based value circulates locally by default

- \"Profits\" remain with labor providers

- Communities become \"wealth generation zones\"

#### **The Extraction Velocity Formula**

**Traditional Economy:\**
Money Velocity = 6-8 local exchanges before extraction\
Retention Rate = 18% after 12 months\
Multiplier Effect = 1.3x initial investment

**Task Board Economy:\**
Labor Velocity = Continuous circulation within network\
Retention Rate = 75% indefinitely\
Multiplier Effect = 4.5-23x coordinated hours

### **The Platform Economy Parallel**

**Platform Model**: Users provide labor → Platform captures value →

**Debt-Based Currency Creation:\**

**Labor-Based Currency Creation:\**
Community → Task Commitment → Hour Credits → Value Creation → Wealth

The fundamental difference: debt-based money requires paying back more

**Static Time Banking:\**

**Dynamic Task Board Economy:\**
1 hour = variable value based on:

- Community priority multipliers

- Skill rarity within network

- Task completion urgency

- Member capacity and availability

Federation multiplies local coordination power without sacrificing local
autonomy. Your community doesn\'t need to grow larger---it needs to
connect deeper.

Start with one trade. Build from there.

**About the Author**

**Chase Tuthill Pierson** is a media producer, technologist, musician,
and recreational economic designer who's spent his life building outside
the bounds of traditional systems. After leaving college for financial
reasons, he stayed in upstate New York working as a tow truck driver,
EMT, and dog trainer---developing a creative discipline that would guide
two decades of independent work in media, music, and technology.

In 2001, Chase was part of the original team that brought *Democracy
Now!* to television, helping launch what became the largest
independently distributed news broadcast in the world. He later
co-created **The CyberCar**, a revolutionary mobile production
studio---built with Oscar-nominated director Jon Alpert and Noel
Rabinowitz---that toured the U.S. and Europe connecting communities
through technology and storytelling.

Chase is also an accomplished songwriter and co-founder of the band
**Mechanical Bull**, whose debut album earned a small record deal and
national distribution. He has written songs featured in film and
television, including one performed live in the finals of *Can You
Duet*, the country music version of *American Idol*. His directorial
debut---a hip hop music video that reached #1 on BET Uncut---remains
infamous to this day.

Chase's photography has been featured in *The New York Times* and other
national publications. In 2018, he co-founded **Scan2Plan.io**, a
geospatial company that grew into a nationally recognized brand. Today,
he splits his time between photography, field operations, creative
strategy, and economic system design---advising communities and
companies on how to build resilient, independent infrastructure from the
ground up.

*Outsider Economics* is his first book.
