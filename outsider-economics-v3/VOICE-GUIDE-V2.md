# Outsider Economics — Voice Guide (Revised)
## The Handbook Voice
## This replaces the original voice guide for V2 and V3.

---

## THE SHIFT

The original voice was "smart person at a kitchen table." That voice had personality — Chase's personality — which made it engaging but also made it sound like one guy's opinion.

The new voice is **a handbook.** Instructional. Matter-of-fact. The reader is smart. The writing respects that by being clear, direct, and dense with useful information. No performance.

---

## THE NEW RULES

### 1. Teach, don't tell stories.
Old: "Sarah Martinez stared at the numbers on her laptop screen for the third time..."
New: "Twenty people contributing twenty hours per month generate $450,000 in coordinated value. Here is the math."

No fictional characters. No narrative scenes. No "imagine this" setups. Just: here is what this is, here is how it works, here is what to do.

### 2. State facts. Show math. Move on.
Old: "Let me show you the math before you close the book."
New: "The arithmetic: 20 people × 20 hours = 400 hours of coordinated labor per month. At a blended market rate of $112.50/hour, the base value is $45,000. The coordination premium — documented across multiple implementations — multiplies this by 4.5x. Result: $450,000/month."

No throat-clearing. No persuasion. Just the numbers and their source.

### 3. Use second person sparingly. Prefer third person and imperative.
Old: "You don't need a grant. You don't need an investor."
New: "Grants and investors are not required. The system runs on coordinated labor, not external capital."

Or imperative: "Skip the grant application. Start with twenty people and a task board."

### 4. Be specific. Always.
Every claim gets a number, a name, or a citation. No "studies show" without saying which study. No "communities have found" without saying which community.

Good: "The Park Slope Food Coop (Brooklyn, NY) operates with 17,000 members each contributing 2.75 hours per month. Operating costs run 40% below conventional supermarkets."

Bad: "Some cooperatives have shown remarkable cost savings."

### 5. Structure like a reference manual.
Each chapter should be usable on its own. A reader should be able to open to any chapter, get what they need, and close the book. No "as we discussed in Chapter 3" dependencies.

Use:
- Numbered steps for processes
- Tables for comparisons
- Formulas for calculations
- Checklists for implementation
- Bold terms for definitions

### 6. No humor. No personality. No rhetoric.
The old voice used dry humor and rhetorical devices. The handbook voice is neutral. Let the content be interesting — the writing doesn't need to perform.

Old: "The most powerful piece of economic infrastructure in your town costs $11 at Home Depot. It's a whiteboard."
New: "The core infrastructure is a task board — physical or digital. Three columns: NEED, CAN DO, DONE. Cost: negligible. A whiteboard, a shared spreadsheet, or a group chat with structured categories."

### 7. End sections with action items, not inspiration.
Old: "The revolution isn't in the streets — it's in the spreadsheets."
New: "Action items: (1) Identify twenty participants. (2) Complete skills inventory. (3) Calculate blended hourly rate. (4) Set up task board. (5) Run first exchange cycle within 14 days."

## SENTENCE PATTERNS

**Do:**
- Short declarative sentences for facts: "The coordination premium is 4.5x."
- Imperative for instructions: "Calculate the blended rate. Multiply by hours. Apply the premium."
- Conditional for decision points: "If the group exceeds fifty members, implement federation protocols (Chapter 7)."
- Tables and lists for structured information.

**Don't:**
- Rhetorical questions ("Why hasn't anyone done this?")
- First person ("I discovered..." / "Let me show you...")
- Emotional appeals ("Imagine a world where...")
- Metaphors and analogies (unless they clarify a technical point)
- Hedging ("it could potentially" / "one might argue")

## TONE COMPARISON

| Aspect | Old Voice (Kitchen Table) | New Voice (Handbook) |
|--------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Opening | Scene or story | Statement of what the chapter covers |
| Proof | Anecdotes with named characters | Data with cited sources |
| Math | Walked through conversationally | Presented as formulas and tables |
| Instructions | Embedded in narrative | Numbered steps with clear outputs |
| Ending | Punchy one-liner | Checklist of action items |
| Personality | High — sounds like Chase | None — sounds like a well-written manual |
| Reader relationship | Peer at the table | Professional consulting a reference |

## WHAT STAYS FROM THE ORIGINAL

- Specificity. Real numbers, real rates, real communities.
- The math receipts. Every claim backed with arithmetic.
- The "Start This Week" action items (now reformatted as checklists).
- The anti-jargon rule. Plain English for technical concepts.
- The political neutrality. This is not a manifesto. It's a manual.
